# AP Art History — Theme: Conflict & Resistance

**AP Definition:** Investigates artworks produced in contexts of war, conquest, protest, or oppression that reflect, document, resist, or commemorate conflict and social struggle.

---

| Artwork ID | Artwork Name | Connection Level | Thematic Connection |
|---|---|---|---|
| 106 | Y no hai remedio (And There's Nothing to Be Done) | essential | Goya's etching from The Disasters of War — a man bound to a post, about to be shot — is the definitive anti-war image in Western art: made from direct observation of Napoleonic atrocities in Spain, published only after Goya's death, and refused the heroic conventions of military art. The title ("And there's nothing to be done") names the condition of helplessness that the image embodies. No other work in the AP 250 more directly confronts the human cost of military conflict without recourse to allegory or glorification. |
| 134 | Memorial Sheet for Karl Liebknecht | essential | Kollwitz's woodcut — mourning figures bent over the body of the assassinated Communist leader, the faces of the grieving workers pressing forward — is the paradigmatic example of art as political mourning and activist witness. Made in the immediate aftermath of Liebknecht's murder by right-wing paramilitaries, the work uses the rough, urgent marks of the woodcut medium to express grief as collective political experience rather than private sentiment. |
| 225 | Vietnam Veterans Memorial | essential | Maya Lin's design — two walls of polished black granite meeting at an angle, descending into the earth, inscribed with 58,279 names — is the most discussed and consequential contemporary war memorial in American history. Its refusal of heroic monumentality, its insistence on naming each individual dead, and its reflective surface (which mirrors the living visitor beside the names of the dead) make it the AP exam's defining example of how contemporary art reconceives what conflict commemoration requires. |
| 40 | Alexander Mosaic | top-pick | The Republican Roman mosaic from the House of the Faun depicts Alexander the Great at the Battle of Issus — his spear thrust toward Darius III, who reaches back from his fleeing chariot — as an image of military triumph that circulated as the defining portrait of Alexander's genius for three centuries. The mosaic is a document of conflict as legitimizing narrative: Alexander's defeat of Persia defined his identity and shaped how subsequent rulers understood the role of military conquest in establishing political authority. |
| 45 | Forum of Trajan | top-pick | Trajan's Column's 155-scene spiral relief — narrating the Dacian Wars from departure to victory — is the fullest surviving ancient monument to military conflict as political achievement. The column transforms war into permanent commemorative spectacle, and its systematic documentation of Roman military technology, tactics, and the treatment of enemies (including the suicides of Dacian leaders at the end) makes it both a triumph monument and an ethnographic record of conquest. |
| 47 | Ludovisi Battle Sarcophagus | top-pick | The Late Roman sarcophagus — its entire surface a compressed tangle of Roman and barbarian bodies in violent combat — represents conflict not as clean triumph but as brutal, chaotic mass struggle. The patron general appears at the center, unhelmed, his expression deliberately unmoved amid the surrounding violence, constructing a specific identity of aristocratic composure under military pressure. |
| 108 | Liberty Leading the People | top-pick | Delacroix's painting of the July 1830 Revolution — the allegorical Liberty bare-breasted with the tricolor, striding over the dead, flanked by a worker and a bourgeois gentleman — is the defining image of armed popular insurrection in Western art. The painting transforms political violence into heroic allegory and asks whether the dead bodies in the foreground are the price of liberty or its condition of possibility. |
| 133 | Self-Portrait as a Soldier | top-pick | Kirchner's Expressionist self-portrait — the artist in uniform, his painting hand severed at the wrist, a nude model visible and unreachable behind him — represents the psychological dimension of conflict: the destruction of creative identity, the severing of the artist's relationship to his work and to civilian life, the war's violence registered not as external event but as bodily self-mutilation. The wound is symbolic (Kirchner was not physically injured) but psychologically precise. |
| 141 | The Migration of the Negro, Panel no. 49 | top-pick | Lawrence's panel — part of his 60-panel series on the Great Migration — depicts a family cramped into a railway car, part of the mass movement of Black Americans fleeing the racial violence of the Jim Crow South. The series frames the Great Migration as a response to systemic conflict (lynching, sharecropping, disenfranchisement), documenting resistance through migration — the decision to move as an act of survival and self-determination. |
| 59 | Bayeux Tapestry | top-pick | The tapestry's 68-meter narrative of the Norman Conquest — Harold's oath, William's invasion, the Battle of Hastings, Harold's death — is among the most complete surviving visual records of medieval military conflict. It is also a document of conflict's representational stakes: made by the victors (or their English subjects), it frames the Conquest as legitimate succession rather than invasion, demonstrating that the representation of conflict is always a political act. |
| 13 | Palette of King Narmer | related | The Narmer Palette's depiction of the pharaoh smiting an enemy with a mace — the canonical image of Egyptian royal military power — establishes from the very beginning of Egyptian art the convention of representing conflict as the king's personal victory over a subordinated enemy. The image is propaganda before propaganda had a name: it constructs the Conquest of Lower Egypt as a divinely sanctioned act of a superhuman ruler. |
| 25 | Lamassu from Dur Sharrukin | related | Sargon II's palace gate guardian figures embody the Neo-Assyrian ideology of military intimidation: the hybrid of bull strength, eagle flight, and human intelligence projects the image of an empire whose power combines natural force with divine favor and human intelligence. The Lamassu are visual arguments in the grammar of imperial threat. |
| 30 | Audience Hall (apadana) at Persepolis | related | The apadana's relief sculptures of tribute-bearing delegations from across the Persian empire represent conflict's aftermath: conquest rendered as peaceful, orderly gift-giving. The visual argument — that subjugated peoples voluntarily bring tribute to the Persian king — translates military domination into an image of cosmopolitan order, demonstrating how imperial art converts conflict into harmony. |
| 38 | Great Altar of Zeus at Pergamon | related | The Gigantomachy frieze — the gods defeating the Giants, the struggle rendered in violent, emotionally intense high relief — is an allegory for the Pergamene kings' military victories over the Gauls. The mythological language of cosmic conflict translates specific military history into eternal struggle between civilization and chaos, a representational strategy for turning conflict into legitimizing narrative. |
| 81 | Frontispiece of the Codex Mendoza | related | The frontispiece — showing Tenochtitlan's founding and the eagle symbol of Aztec authority surrounded by conquered cities — documents the Mexica empire's foundation in military conflict. Made under Spanish colonial supervision, the document sits at the intersection of two conquests: the Aztec conquest of central Mexico and the Spanish conquest of the Aztec empire, with each act of documentation reflecting different interests in controlling the record of conflict. |
| 90 | Angel with Arquebus | related | The colonial Andean angel-arquebusier paintings make the instruments of Spanish military conquest (the arquebus) sacred, dressing archangels in the armor and weapons of the conquerors. The work documents how indigenous artists processed colonial military domination through religious art, transforming the symbols of conquest into sacred imagery — a form of resistance through appropriation that neither fully accepts nor fully rejects the colonial power structure. |
| 111 | Slave Ship | related | Turner's painting — the bodies of enslaved people thrown overboard during a typhoon to collect insurance payments, the sea churning red around the drowning figures — is the AP exam's most direct engagement with the violence of the transatlantic slave trade as subject for Western landscape painting. Turner uses the conventions of sublime seascape to document an act of mass murder, asking whether beauty can be a medium for moral witness or whether aestheticizing atrocity is itself a form of complicity. |
| 157 | Templo Mayor | related | The Mexica temple complex was the site of mass sacrificial ritual directly connected to military conflict: captured warriors were sacrificed at the pyramid's summit to sustain the sun, making military conquest a religious and cosmological imperative. The Calendar Stone and the Coyolxauhqui Stone at the base document a belief system in which conflict was not a political option but a cosmic necessity. |
| 159 | City of Cusco / Qorikancha | related | The Inka capital's layered architecture — with the colonial Spanish convent of Santo Domingo built directly on top of the Qorikancha's stone walls — is a physical record of conquest: the Spanish literally built their religious architecture on the foundation of the Inka sacred center. The surviving Inka stonework beneath the colonial addition makes the history of conflict spatially visible in the contemporary built environment. |
| 203 | Night Attack on the Sanjô Palace | related | The Kamakura Period handscroll — depicting the 1159 raid on the Sanjô Palace, the burning building, the fleeing court, the violence of mounted warriors — is among the earliest Japanese narrative paintings to take military conflict as its primary subject. The work documents a specific historical event (the Heiji Disturbance) with vivid, kinetic immediacy, and establishes the emaki (picture scroll) format as a medium for representing conflict as sequential, dramatic narrative. |
| 226 | Horn Players | related | Basquiat's painting — overlaying anatomical diagrams, text fragments, and jazz musician references — engages the structural violence of American racism as a form of ongoing conflict: the Black body subjected to the clinical gaze of medicine, the genius of Black artists (Parker, Dizzy Gillespie) simultaneously celebrated and extracted by white commercial culture. Basquiat's visual language of erasure, crossing-out, and layering represents the experience of being simultaneously visible as body and invisible as person. |
| 233 | Trade (Gifts for Trading Land with White People) | related | Jaune Quick-to-See Smith's canvas uses the visual language of American abstract painting (collage, gestural marks) to document ongoing colonial conflict: newspaper clippings about Indigenous land rights, the ironic listing of contemporary commodity items as "gifts for trading land," and the central canoe make the work an argument that the conflict between Native sovereignty and settler-colonial possession did not end in the nineteenth century. |
| 243 | Darkytown Rebellion | related | Walker's cut-paper silhouettes — Black bodies in scenes of antebellum violence, slavery, and rebellion, projected onto gallery walls with a device that implicates the viewer's own shadow in the scene — represent conflict not as past historical event but as ongoing visual and psychological experience. The "rebellion" of the title frames enslaved people as active resisters rather than passive victims, making the work both a document of historical conflict and an act of contemporary resistance to its forgetting. |
| 246 | Stadia II | related | Mehretu's large-scale abstract painting — layered architectural plans, stadium diagrams, and gestural marks that suggest the movement of crowds or military formations — engages conflict at the level of systems and structures rather than bodies. The work asks what the built environments of power (stadiums, arenas, civic centers) have to do with the organized movement of populations in conflict, making visible the structural conditions that enable mass violence. |
| 248 | Shibboleth | related | Doris Salcedo's crack running the length of the Tate Modern's Turbine Hall floor — widening from a hairline fracture at one end to a deep crevasse at the other — represents the invisible structural violence of colonial history as a physical rupture in the architecture of a Western cultural institution. The "shibboleth" of the title (the word used to identify and kill outsiders) frames the work as a monument to the violence of exclusion — the conflict that does not announce itself as conflict but operates as structural condition. |
