# AP Art History — Theme: Power & Authority

**AP Definition:** Examines how art expresses, legitimizes, or challenges political and social power—leaders, states, institutions, propaganda, patronage, and symbols of authority.

---

| Artwork ID | Artwork Name | Connection Level | Thematic Connection |
|---|---|---|---|
| 43 | Augustus of Prima Porta | essential | The canonical example of Roman imperial portrait propaganda and the AP exam's go-to work for discussing how likeness is subordinated to ideology: Augustus is presented as perpetually young, physically ideal, and divinely descended (Cupid at his feet), while the breastplate's cosmic imagery argues that his rule restored universal order. No work in the AP 250 more economically demonstrates how portraiture constructs rather than records political identity. |
| 93 | Palace at Versailles | essential | The apotheosis of absolutist architecture and the definitive example of space as political instrument: the axial approach, the Hall of Mirrors celebrating Louis XIV's military campaigns, the geometry imposed on nature in the gardens, and the forced residence of French nobility all demonstrate that every element of the built environment can be designed to produce and reinforce political authority. Students writing about power and architecture should almost always anchor here. |
| 206 | Forbidden City | essential | The Ming imperial palace complex encodes political authority in urban planning at the largest possible scale: the north-south axis, the sequence of increasingly restricted courtyards, the yellow-roof hierarchy, and the city's formal name (the "Purple Forbidden City," inaccessible to the world) all communicate that the emperor is the fixed pivot of political and cosmic order. Essential for any discussion ranging beyond the European or ancient Mediterranean tradition. |
| 212 | Chairman Mao en Route to Anyuan | essential | The defining example of modern state political propaganda and mass reproduction as instruments of authority: the oil painting — and its distribution as the most reproduced image in Chinese history — constructed a heroic founding myth for Mao through deliberate visual choices (youth, purposeful gaze, solitary figure against an opening sky). Essential for discussions of how twentieth-century states weaponize image-making for ideological legitimacy. |
| 13 | Palette of King Narmer | top-pick | The foundational example of art as political statement: hierarchical scale places Narmer as the dominant figure, and composite iconography — pharaoh in both crowns, smiting enemies, inspecting the beheaded — legitimizes the unification of Egypt as divinely sanctioned conquest. The Narmer Palette establishes visual conventions for representing royal power that Egyptian art would maintain for three thousand years. |
| 17 | Great Pyramids and Great Sphinx | top-pick | Among history's most direct expressions of royal power made permanent in stone: scale, astronomical alignment, labor mobilization on an almost inconceivable scale, and the Sphinx's guardian function all project pharaonic authority as cosmic, permanent, and divine. The pyramids make the equation of built environment with political power as literal as it has ever been. |
| 19 | Code of Hammurabi | top-pick | The stele's relief imagery — Hammurabi receiving the rod and ring of divine authority from Shamash — visually fuses law with sacred sanction, a foundational prototype for the idea that political power derives its legitimacy from divine delegation. The legal code inscribed below is only authoritative because the image above it argues that the king received the authority to make it directly from the god. |
| 30 | Audience Hall (apadana) of Darius and Xerxes | top-pick | The apadana's relief program — delegates bearing tribute from across the empire, shown approaching in orderly procession — is among antiquity's most sophisticated examples of imperial propaganda through architectural decoration. Dozens of subjugated peoples rendered as peaceful gift-givers transform military domination into an image of cosmopolitan order and voluntary submission to Achaemenid authority. |
| 45 | Forum of Trajan | top-pick | The entire complex — markets, basilica, libraries, and the spiral column — is a permanent advertisement of imperial achievement. Trajan's Column specifically is the most sustained exercise in historical narrative as political monument in the ancient world: 155 scenes converting the Dacian Wars into a permanent unscrollable record demonstrate how military conquest is translated into political legitimacy through commemoration. |
| 51 | San Vitale | top-pick | The Justinian and Theodora apse mosaics are the defining image of Byzantine imperial-ecclesiastical authority: the emperor carries the Eucharistic paten, wears a halo, and stands flanked by clergy and military guard, collapsing the distinction between sacred and political power. No work in the curriculum more directly visualizes the Byzantine theory of the emperor as God's earthly representative. |
| 86 | Henri IV Receives the Portrait of Marie de' Medici | top-pick | The Marie de' Medici Cycle is the Baroque era's most ambitious example of royal propaganda-as-patronage, and this panel — in which Jupiter and Juno personally endorse the royal marriage — shows how a queen regent used commissioned painting to construct and legitimize authority that her gender and widowhood made structurally precarious. Strong for discussions of how patronage and political legitimization intersect. |
| 193 | Terra cotta warriors | top-pick | The First Emperor of China's buried army — thousands of individualized soldiers in military formation — projects imperial authority across the threshold of death itself. The army's scale and the political statement of its production (the resources of an entire empire redirected to serve a single man's afterlife) make this one of the curriculum's most vivid demonstrations of power as command over labor and resources. |
| 199 | Angkor Wat | top-pick | Angkor Wat fuses cosmological order with royal authority in a single architectural program: the temple-mountain represents Mount Meru, the king is identified with Vishnu, and the bas-relief galleries narrating military victories make the complex simultaneously a sacred site, a royal monument, and a permanent statement of Khmer imperial legitimacy. |
| 208 | Jahangir Preferring a Sufi Shaikh to Kings | top-pick | A sophisticated statement of Mughal imperial ideology that works through deliberate comparative diminishment: Jahangir on his hourglass throne dispenses favor to a holy man while European and Ottoman kings recede to the margins, and putti hold a divine halo around his head. The painting argues for Mughal authority as spiritually superior and cosmically endorsed — power constructed through imagery of hierarchy and preference rather than force. |
| 20 | Temple of Amun-Re and Hypostyle Hall | related | Built and expanded by successive pharaohs as simultaneous acts of religious piety and political self-promotion, Karnak's scale reflects the inseparability of religious and political authority in Egypt — each pharaoh who added a pylon or hall inscribed his name and image throughout, making the temple a layered record of royal patronage as political currency. |
| 21 | Mortuary temple of Hatshepsut | related | Hatshepsut's temple — with its Osiride statues, colonnaded terraces, and reliefs depicting her divine birth and coronation — is a case study in how a female pharaoh used art and architecture to legitimize unconventional authority by fully adopting the visual conventions of male kingship, including the false beard and masculine titulary. |
| 35 | Acropolis (Parthenon) | related | Built under Pericles as a statement of Athenian imperial power following the Persian Wars, the Parthenon's sculptural program — the Panathenaic frieze, metopes, and pediments — identifies Athens's civic community with divine favor and constructs its political identity as the rightful center of the Greek world. |
| 59 | Bayeux Tapestry | related | Norman political propaganda in textile form: the sequential narrative validates William's claim to England by depicting Harold's oath and its "betrayal," framing invasion as legitimate succession and demonstrating that the representation of political conflict is always itself a political act. |
| 103 | The Oath of the Horatii | related | David's Neoclassical canvas conflates civic virtue with absolute obligation to the state, making the willing sacrifice of individual life for political authority into a heroic visual ideal on the eve of the French Revolution — an emblem of the new political ideology's total claim on citizens' bodies and loyalties. |
| 155 | Yaxchilán | related | The lintels document bloodletting rituals in which Maya rulers draw blood to communicate with ancestors and gods, making royal authority cosmologically necessary — the king as indispensable ritual intermediary whose political legitimacy derives from his unique capacity to access the sacred. |
| 157 | Templo Mayor | related | The Mexica dual temple, repeatedly enlarged with each new military campaign, is a physical record of imperial expansion: each rebuilding layer corresponds to a new conquest, making the monument itself an accumulating index of political and military authority growing over time. |
| 169 | Wall plaque from Oba's palace | related | Benin court bronzes document and legitimize royal authority through a permanent visual archive of dynastic history, military campaigns, and court hierarchy — demonstrating that African royal courts used cast metal as deliberately as any European monarch used painted portraiture to construct and preserve political identity. |
| 170 | Sika dwa kofi (Golden Stool) | related | Among the most conceptually distinctive examples of political authority embodied in an object: believed to contain the soul of the Ashanti nation, the Golden Stool is never sat upon even by the king, making it simultaneously the source of royal legitimacy and an authority that exceeds and constrains any individual ruler. |
| 243 | Darkytown Rebellion | related | Walker's cut-paper silhouette installation uses the visual conventions of genteel authority — the polite Victorian silhouette — to construct a counter-narrative to antebellum political power, making the visual language of authority the medium for staging its critique and exposing how that language has always encoded racial violence. |
| 250 | Kui Hua Zi (Sunflower Seeds) | related | Ai Weiwei's 100 million hand-painted porcelain seeds — each unique, together indistinguishable — critiques both mass conformity under Chinese state authority and the labor conditions that produce it, while the work's existence as international contemporary art tests the limits of artistic dissent under the CCP's political power. |
