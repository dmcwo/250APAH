# AP Art History — Theme: Trade & Exchange

**AP Definition:** Looks at how movement of goods, ideas, and stylistic influences via trade, migration, and contact affects materials, techniques, and iconography.

---

| Artwork ID | Artwork Name | Connection Level | Thematic Connection |
|---|---|---|---|
| 188 | Basin (Baptistère de St. Louis) | essential | Muhammad ibn al-Zain's brass basin — inlaid with gold and silver imagery of Christian and Islamic court scenes — was made by a Muslim artist for an Islamic patron, then acquired for use in French royal baptisms. It is the AP exam's single most concentrated example of cross-cultural exchange through luxury goods: the object's material (brass), technique (inlay), iconographic program (mixed Christian/Islamic courtly imagery), and eventual function (Christian baptism) each reflect a different layer of Mediterranean trade and cultural contact. |
| 204 | The David Vases | essential | The Yuan Dynasty blue-and-white porcelain David Vases — whose cobalt pigment was imported from Persia along Silk Road trade networks — are the definitive example of material exchange between Asia and the Islamic world producing a new visual form. The cobalt-blue-on-white aesthetic that would define Chinese porcelain for centuries was not indigenous to China: it was a product of trade, and its adoption reshaped both Chinese ceramic production and global luxury markets. |
| 244 | The Swing (after Fragonard) | essential | Shonibare's use of Dutch wax batik fabric — manufactured in the Netherlands, printed with Indonesian batik patterns, and now globally recognized as an emblem of African identity — makes trade and exchange the literal material of the work. The fabric's complex history (Dutch industrial production, Indonesian aesthetic origin, African market, contemporary global signifier) enacts in textile form the argument that identity and cultural exchange are always already entangled in colonial commerce. |
| 196 | Gold and jade crown | top-pick | The Three Kingdoms Period Silla crown — its jade comma-shaped gogok beads and gold metalwork reflecting influences from Central Asian steppe cultures and Chinese court art reaching the Korean peninsula via trade and diplomatic exchange — documents the Silk Road's role as a conduit for artistic forms as well as goods. The crown's hybrid aesthetics make the cultural traffic of trade networks visible in a single luxury object. |
| 16 | Standard of Ur | top-pick | The Standard of Ur's materials — lapis lazuli from Afghanistan, shell from the Persian Gulf, red limestone from elsewhere in the Near East — document the long-distance trade networks of third-millennium B.C.E. Sumer before writing fully emerges as historical record. The object is simultaneously a work of art and an archaeological inventory of the raw materials that Sumerian urban culture required from distant sources. |
| 94 | Screen with the Siege of Belgrade and hunting scene | top-pick | The biombo (folding screen) produced in colonial New Spain combines a Japanese artistic format (the byōbu screen), European narrative subject matter (the Ottoman siege of Belgrade), and a Mexican craft tradition, using shell inlay (enconchado) that references pre-Columbian featherwork. The object could only exist at the intersection of the Manila Galleon trade (bringing Japanese aesthetic forms to Mexico), the Atlantic trade (bringing European imagery), and the indigenous craft knowledge that provided the technique. |
| 163 | Bandolier bag | top-pick | The Lenape bandolier bag's glass beads — all imported through European trade networks — are deployed in floral patterns that synthesize Eastern Woodlands aesthetic traditions with European botanical motifs encountered through trade contact. The object documents how Indigenous communities selectively adopted trade goods not as passive recipients of colonial culture but as active artists who transformed imported materials into new visual expressions of cultural identity. |
| 126 | Les Demoiselles d'Avignon | top-pick | Picasso's incorporation of African and Iberian mask forms — encountered through Paris's Trocadéro ethnographic museum, itself a repository of colonial extraction — demonstrates how Western Modernism's formal innovations were enabled by the cultural appropriation that imperialism made possible. Trade and colonial collection brought African objects to Europe; Picasso's formal borrowing without acknowledgment of source or meaning is itself an index of the power imbalance structuring that exchange. |
| 189 | Bahram Gur Fights the Karg | top-pick | This folio from the Great Il-Khanid Shahnama illustrates the artistic synthesis produced by the Mongol conquests: the Il-Khanid dynasty that commissioned it ruled Persia but were of Mongol origin, and the painting combines Persian manuscript illumination traditions with Chinese landscape conventions (the rocks, the spatial compression) absorbed through Mongol cultural networks connecting Persia to East Asia. Trade and conquest here produce a new hybrid visual language. |
| 211 | Under the Wave off Kanagawa (Great Wave) | top-pick | Hokusai's woodblock print — itself a product of Japan's sophisticated commercial print industry and made from pigments that included imported Prussian blue — was acquired by European artists through the trade networks that opened Japan after the Meiji Restoration, catalyzing Japonisme and influencing Monet, Van Gogh, and Art Nouveau. The work exemplifies how artistic exchange flows through trade networks in both directions: Japanese commercial art reshaped European Modernism. |
| 7 | Jade cong | related | Liangzhu jade congs and related forms appear across a wide geographic area, suggesting either long-distance trade in jade objects or the movement of jade-working knowledge and forms. Jade itself had to be traded, often over long distances, and the distribution of the cong form documents early Chinese networks of exchange that preceded historical records. |
| 53 | Merovingian looped fibulae | related | The Merovingian fibulae's garnets — imported from Sri Lanka and India via Byzantine and Sassanian trade intermediaries — document the reach of early medieval exchange networks that connected Frankish Europe to South Asia. The cloisonné technique for setting garnets, adapted from Byzantine goldwork, also shows how artistic technique traveled through the same networks as the materials themselves. |
| 57 | Pyxis of al-Mughira | related | The Umayyad ivory pyxis — carved in Córdoba from elephant ivory that had traveled through sub-Saharan African or South Asian trade routes to Islamic Spain — reflects the luxury goods networks of the early medieval Islamic world. The figural imagery of court scenes on an object made from a globally sourced material documents how Andalusian court culture depended on long-distance trade networks for both its raw materials and its sense of cosmopolitan prestige. |
| 65 | Alhambra | related | The Alhambra's materials and techniques — the glazed tile dados, the muqarnas stucco vaulting, the hydraulic garden engineering — reflect the synthesis of technologies and aesthetic forms that circulated through the medieval Islamic world, connecting Andalusia to North Africa, Persia, and the broader Dar al-Islam through networks of architectural knowledge and craftsmen as well as goods. |
| 90 | Angel with Arquebus | related | The colonial Andean angel-arquebusier paintings were produced by indigenous artists trained in European techniques through Jesuit missionary networks, combining Catholic iconographic tradition with local sensibilities in luxury objects that circulated within colonial trade and gift networks. The arquebus itself — a Spanish military import — appears as a sacred object in an indigenous-colonial hybrid context, documenting how material culture traveled with conquest. |
| 95 | Virgin of Guadalupe (González) | related | González's enconchado Virgin of Guadalupe uses a shell-inlay technique (enconchado) that reflects the exchange between pre-Columbian Mexican craft traditions and Japanese lacquerwork aesthetics introduced via the Manila Galleon trade, while the subject matter (the Virgin) and format (devotional painting) are European. The object is a material record of the Pacific trade route's cultural consequences. |
| 169 | Wall plaque from Oba's palace | related | Benin bronzes were cast using brass (copper-zinc alloy) imported via trans-Saharan and later Portuguese Atlantic trade networks — the raw material of Benin's most prestigious art form arrived through commerce. Portuguese traders appear on some plaques, documenting the moment of contact; the incorporation of Portuguese imagery into Benin court art shows how visual culture absorbed and processed the encounter with European trade partners. |
| 191 | Ardabil Carpet | related | The Ardabil Carpet's silk-and-wool construction, its Chinese cloud-band and lotus motifs in a Persian medallion format, and its eventual acquisition by a British collector document the layered exchange networks that connected Persian Safavid court production to Chinese decorative sources, Ottoman consumer markets, and ultimately European collections. The carpet is a luxury trade object whose aesthetic form was itself shaped by the cross-cultural contacts of Silk Road commerce. |
| 208 | Jahangir Preferring a Sufi Shaikh | related | Bichitr's miniature includes a European oil painting of James I that Jahangir has been offered as a diplomatic gift — making trade and diplomatic exchange literally visible within the painting's frame. The allegorical arrangement (Jahangir choosing the spiritual over the worldly, including a European king) reflects the Mughal court's self-conscious positioning within a world of competing empires connected by diplomatic exchange and luxury trade. |
| 237 | Pisupo Lua Afe | related | Tuffery's bull assembled from flattened corned beef tins makes the history of Pacific trade and colonial economic dependence into sculptural form: corned beef (pisupo) arrived via nineteenth-century colonial trade networks and became embedded in Pacific diets and culture. The sculpture's material directly embodies how global commodity trade transformed Samoan cultural identity and material life, making trade's consequences visible as art. |
| 245 | Old Man's Cloth | related | El Anatsui's tapestry of flattened aluminum liquor bottle caps and copper wire was assembled from discarded bottle tops collected in Nigeria — materials that arrived through the West African colonial alcohol trade. The work transforms the residue of colonial trade networks (the bottle cap as a marker of alcohol's role in colonial exchange and exploitation) into monumental abstract textile, making the history of trade and its aftermath the medium of contemporary art. |
