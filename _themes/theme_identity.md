# AP Art History — Theme: Identity

**AP Definition:** Explores how art constructs and communicates personal, group, gender, ethnic, national, or class identities through iconography, portraiture, dress, and cultural markers.

---

| Artwork ID | Artwork Name | Connection Level | Thematic Connection |
|---|---|---|---|
| 231 | Untitled (#228), History Portraits | essential | Sherman's photograph of herself costumed as a figure from a Old Master portrait — wig, ruff collar, painted décolletage, prosthetic nose — deconstructs how Western art history has constructed feminine identity through male-authored images. By inhabiting those images from the inside, Sherman exposes femininity as performance and asks whose body, whose gaze, and whose values Western portraiture actually represents. |
| 235 | Rebellious Silence | essential | Neshat's photograph — the artist's face bisected by a rifle barrel, Persian calligraphy covering the visible skin — is a layered statement about Iranian women's identity under the Islamic Republic: simultaneously inside and outside tradition, armed and veiled, political subject and political object. The work makes identity itself a site of conflict between national, religious, gender, and personal claims. |
| 243 | Darkytown Rebellion | essential | Walker's cut-paper silhouettes use the genteel Victorian form of the silhouette — a medium associated with polite portraiture — to construct identities defined and destroyed by antebellum American racial violence. By making Black bodies visible only as shadows, Walker forces a reckoning with the historical conditions under which African American identity has been both constructed by and against white supremacy. |
| 43 | Augustus of Prima Porta | top-pick | Imperial Roman portraiture at its most programmatic: Augustus is presented as perpetually young, physically ideal, divinely descended (Cupid at his feet), and militarily triumphant (the breastplate's cosmic narrative). The portrait is not a likeness but a constructed identity — the visual argument for what it means to be Augustus, emperor, and living god simultaneously. |
| 99 | Portrait of Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz | top-pick | Miguel Cabrera's portrait of the seventeenth-century Mexican nun, poet, and intellectual presents an identity constructed through books, writing instruments, and religious habit — a woman who used the limited institutional space available to her (the convent) to construct a public intellectual identity in colonial New Spain. The portrait is a document of self-fashioning under constraint. |
| 126 | Les Demoiselles d'Avignon | top-pick | Picasso's proto-Cubist painting — five female nudes confronting the viewer, their faces fractured into African and Iberian mask forms — enacts a violent collision of identities: the Western female nude tradition, African and Oceanic art appropriated as formal device, male sexual anxiety, and the beginning of Modernism's project of breaking apart stable representation. The work is a founding document of how identity (especially female identity) is fragmented, exoticized, and reconstructed by the male avant-garde gaze. |
| 140 | The Two Fridas | top-pick | Kahlo's double self-portrait externalizes the internal rupture of mestiza identity: the European-dressed Frida (heart severed, vein bleeding) and the Tehuana-dressed Frida (heart whole, holding a miniature of Diego Rivera) represent the irreconcilable colonial split in Mexican identity between indigenous and European inheritances. The exposed, connected hearts literalize the idea that identity is relational, constructed through connection and severance. |
| 220 | Tamati Waka Nene | top-pick | Lindauer's portrait of the Māori rangatira (chief) depicts his full facial tā moko (tattoo) with ethnographic precision — each spiral records his genealogy, tribal affiliation, and social rank in a system where the face is literally a written identity document. The portrait engages the tension between Māori visual self-construction and European portrait conventions, producing an image that operates within both systems simultaneously. |
| 165 | Painted elk hide | top-pick | Attributed to the Eastern Shoshone artist Cotsiogo (Cadzi Cody), this painted hide depicts the artist's own life narrative — battle exploits, ceremonial scenes, encounters — in a first-person visual autobiography. Produced in the context of early reservation life, the work asserts Plains warrior identity against the conditions of cultural suppression and documents a tradition of self-representation that colonial history was actively working to erase. |
| 162 | All-T'oqapu tunic | top-pick | The Inka tunic covered in t'oqapu geometric panels — each a distinct symbol readable as an ethnic, regional, or rank designation — is identity made textile. The garment was a communication system: a high-status tunic with multiple t'oqapu panels signaled the wearer's place in the Inka social and administrative hierarchy, making dress itself a legible identity text in a culture without alphabetic writing. |
| 232 | Dancing at the Louvre | top-pick | Faith Ringgold's quilt painting — Black women dancing in front of the Louvre's European masterpieces, the work framed in African American quilting tradition — inserts Black female identity into spaces from which it has been historically excluded. The medium (story quilt), the setting (European high culture), and the subjects (Black women in joyful motion) together assert an identity claim against canonical art history. |
| 244 | The Swing (after Fragonard) | top-pick | Shonibare's recreation of Fragonard's Rococo painting as a mixed-media installation — with a headless mannequin in Dutch wax batik fabric replacing the aristocratic French woman — substitutes African identity into the history of European class and leisure. The Dutch wax fabric, manufactured in Europe for African markets but now a marker of African identity, makes the work a meditation on how colonial trade routes construct cultural identity. |
| 42 | Head of a Roman patrician | related | Republican Roman verism is itself an identity construction: the emphasis on age, wrinkles, and individuating marks of a lived life builds a specific identity claim — civic gravitas (dignitas), ancestral connection (the imagines tradition), and masculine virtue (virtus) — through the strategic rejection of Greek idealization. |
| 68 | The Arnolfini Portrait | related | Van Eyck's painting is dense with identity markers — Flemish bourgeois dress, domestic luxury objects, the elaborate signature on the wall — that construct the social identity of its subjects as prosperous merchant-class members of a specific time and place. The painting functions as a social identity document as much as a portrait. |
| 69 | David (Donatello) | related | Donatello's bronze David — the first large-scale freestanding male nude since antiquity — constructs a complex identity: the adolescent hero wears only a hat and boots, his foot resting on Goliath's severed head, his pose ambiguously victorious and vulnerable. The commission by the Medici family layers civic Florentine identity (David as republic's defender) over a visual identity that has been extensively discussed in terms of gender and desire. |
| 80 | Venus of Urbino | related | Titian's reclining nude in an aristocratic Venetian interior constructs female identity as simultaneously erotic spectacle and domestic managerial subject (she directs servants in the background). The work is an identity document for the male patron's possession and for the normative femininity — beauty, availability, domestic competence — that Renaissance portraiture prescribed for elite women. |
| 86 | Henri IV Receives the Portrait of Marie de' Medici | related | The Marie de' Medici Cycle is a sustained project of identity construction: Rubens was commissioned to create a visual biography of the queen regent that would legitimate her authority after her husband's assassination. The cycle's mythological framing makes Marie's personal and political identity a product of divine plan, not historical contingency. |
| 91 | Las Meninas | related | Velázquez's painting is a labyrinth of identity: the painter depicts himself painting, the Infanta Margarita surrounded by her attendants occupies the foreground, the king and queen appear only in a mirror, and the viewer occupies the position of the royal sitters. The work destabilizes the normally fixed identities of artist, subject, viewer, and patron into a recursive, interchangeable system. |
| 104 | George Washington (Houdon) | related | Houdon's marble constructs Washington's identity through deliberate iconographic choices: Roman republican dress (toga-like cloak over civilian clothes), the fasces (Roman symbol of republican authority), the plow (gentleman-farmer), and the sword sheathed at his side (military power voluntarily restrained). The sculpture argues that American national identity is defined by the voluntary limitation of power. |
| 133 | Self-Portrait as a Soldier | related | Kirchner's Expressionist self-portrait depicts the artist in military uniform with his painting hand severed at the wrist — though he was not physically wounded, he suffered a breakdown during WWI. The painting makes visible the rupture between artist identity and soldier identity, arguing that military service was literally mutilating to his creative self. |
| 158 | Ruler's feather headdress of Motecuhzoma II | related | The headdress constructs Aztec royal identity through the language of sacred materials: quetzal feathers (restricted by law to the highest nobility), cotinga blue, and gold work all signal the ruler's identity as the earthly avatar of Quetzalcóatl. No other object in the AP 250 more completely collapses personal, political, and sacred identity into a single wearable object. |
| 163 | Bandolier bag | related | The Lenape bandolier bag — its surface covered in glass bead floral patterns that combine Native and European aesthetic conventions — is an identity object produced at the intersection of colonial trade and Indigenous continuity. Worn at ceremonies, the bag communicated cultural belonging and aesthetic refinement within a community actively resisting cultural erasure. |
| 164 | Transformation mask | related | The Kwakwaka'wakw transformation mask — which opens during ceremony to reveal a second face within — is an identity object that performs the fundamental cosmological belief that human and animal identities are not fixed but transformable. The mask makes identity itself the subject: to wear it is to enact the crossing of boundaries between human and spirit worlds. |
| 171 | Ndop of King Mishe miShyaang maMbul | related | The Kuba royal ndop does not attempt physical likeness; instead, it constructs royal identity through an identifying emblem (ibol) carved at the base and the idealized, powerful posture of kingship. The portrait is an identity argument — this is what it means to be king — rather than a record of what the specific king looked like. |
| 173 | Female (Pwo) mask | related | The Chokwe Pwo mask constructs an idealized feminine identity — elaborate coiffure, filed teeth, tattoo-like decorations — that is worn by male dancers in ceremonies honoring female ancestors. The mask performs femininity as a cultural ideal rather than a biological given, making visible the distinction between gender identity as construction and gender identity as embodied experience. |
| 205 | Portrait of Sin Sukju | related | The Korean Joseon-era hanging scroll portrait adheres to strict conventions of official portraiture: the sitter in court robes and official hat, rendered with psychological stillness that erases individual personality in favor of institutional rank. Identity here is entirely social and administrative — the portrait records not who Sin Sukju was but what position he occupied. |
| 226 | Horn Players | related | Basquiat's painting, which overlays anatomical diagrams with text fragments, jazz musician names (Parker, Dizzy), and graffiti-style mark-making, constructs African American cultural identity through layers of reference — the Black body, medical/colonial objectification, the genius of jazz as a counternarrative. The work insists on the complexity and multiplicity of Black identity against the simplifications of both racism and romanticization. |
| 230 | Pink Panther | related | Koons's glazed porcelain sculpture — the cartoon Pink Panther embracing a buxom blonde woman — collapses distinctions between high and low culture, masculine and feminine, innocence and sexuality. The work reflects on how consumer culture constructs and markets identity through brand images, celebrity, and the controlled chaos of desire. |
| 233 | Trade (Gifts for Trading Land with White People) | related | Jaune Quick-to-See Smith's canvas layers Native American cultural markers (the canoe, newspaper clippings about land rights, commodity packaging) against the conventions of American abstract painting, constructing a Native American identity that is simultaneously ancient, contemporary, and in active political contestation with settler-colonial identity formation. |
| 237 | Pisupo Lua Afe (Corned Beef 2000) | related | Tuffery's bull constructed from flattened corned beef tins enacts the contradiction at the heart of contemporary Pacific identity: corned beef (pisupo) arrived through colonial trade and became embedded in Pacific diet and culture, simultaneously a marker of colonial dependence and of Samoan cultural identity. The work asks what identity means when its materials are always already colonial. |
| 247 | Preying Mantra | related | Mutu's collaged figure — assembled from fashion magazine fragments, medical imagery, and African cultural markers — constructs an identity that is fragmented, commodified, and hybrid. The work reflects on how Black women's bodies are simultaneously exoticized, medicalized, and aestheticized by global image culture, making identity a site of both violence and resistant self-construction. |
