# AP Art History — Theme: Narrative & Storytelling

**AP Definition:** Focuses on how art tells stories—myth, history, literary scenes, sequential imagery, and visual strategies used to convey events and meanings.

---

| Artwork ID | Artwork Name | Connection Level | Thematic Connection |
|---|---|---|---|
| 63 | Arena (Scrovegni) Chapel | essential | Giotto's complete fresco cycle is the AP exam's foundational example of continuous narrative in monumental painting: the life of Joachim and Anna, the life of the Virgin, the life of Christ, and the Last Judgment are organized across all four walls and the barrel vault in a coherent narrative sequence that a visitor reads moving through the space. Giotto's innovations — consistent light source, foreshortened figures, psychologically individualized faces — are all in service of making sacred narrative emotionally immediate and spatially believable. |
| 59 | Bayeux Tapestry | essential | The Bayeux Tapestry is the AP exam's definitive example of sequential narrative in a non-painting medium: 68 meters of embroidered linen narrate the Norman Conquest from Edward the Confessor's death through the Battle of Hastings, using Latin captions, a continuous frieze format, and a rich visual vocabulary of gesture, architectural framing, and border imagery to tell a complex political and military story. The tapestry demonstrates that narrative art is not limited to any single medium or cultural tradition. |
| 75 | Sistine Chapel ceiling | essential | Michelangelo's ceiling is the most ambitious narrative program in Western art: nine Genesis scenes (from the Separation of Light and Darkness through the Drunkenness of Noah) across the vault's center, flanked by twelve Prophets and Sibyls who foretold the Christian story, with the Ancestors of Christ in the lunettes and spandrels. The program constructs a theological narrative of human creation, fall, and the prefiguration of redemption — a story told in fresco across 500 square meters of ceiling above the space where the Church gathered to elect its leaders. |
| 45 | Forum of Trajan (Trajan's Column) | top-pick | Trajan's Column is the most sustained exercise in historical narrative in ancient art: 155 scenes in continuous spiral relief narrate the two Dacian Wars from departure through victory celebrations, with over 2,500 individual figures. The narrative conventions — the repeated appearance of Trajan as the work's protagonist, the use of architectural settings as scene dividers, the consistent movement of the narrative from bottom to top — establish a visual grammar for sequential historical storytelling that influenced medieval and Renaissance narrative programs. |
| 13 | Palette of King Narmer | top-pick | The Narmer Palette is among the earliest surviving examples of visual narrative: its register-organized composition tells the story of the unification of Egypt — the smiting of enemies, the inspection of the beheaded, the binding of serpopards — through a visual grammar of hierarchical scale, composite view, and symbolic action that establishes conventions used in Egyptian narrative art for the next three millennia. The palette is a narrative document where visual conventions and political argument are identical. |
| 190 | The Court of Gayumars | top-pick | Sultan Muhammad's folio from Shah Tahmasp's Shahnama — depicting the legendary first king of Persia surrounded by his court in a paradisiacal landscape — is the peak of Persian manuscript illustration and its approach to narrative: the entire pictorial field is charged with storytelling, from the central courtly scene to the faces peering from the rocks and the detailed natural world that frames the human action. Persian manuscript painting developed a sophisticated visual vocabulary for telling the stories of the Shahnama (Book of Kings) across hundreds of illustrated folios. |
| 203 | Night Attack on the Sanjô Palace | top-pick | The Kamakura Period handscroll is the AP exam's primary example of Japanese narrative picture scroll (emaki): the viewer unrolls the scroll from right to left, moving through the sequence of the night raid in real time — the approach, the attack, the burning building, the fleeing court figures. The format and the reading experience are identical: narrative is not depicted but performed, the viewer's physical act of unrolling the scroll enacting the temporal unfolding of the story. |
| 50 | Rebecca and Eliezer at the Well, from the Vienna Genesis | top-pick | The Vienna Genesis is the AP exam's primary example of Early Byzantine manuscript illumination and its approach to narrative economy: multiple moments of a single story (Rebecca at the well, Rebecca's journey to Isaac) are depicted simultaneously within a single pictorial field, collapsing the narrative into a spatial arrangement where time is expressed through repetition of figures rather than sequential division of scenes. |
| 55 | Lindisfarne Gospels | top-pick | The Lindisfarne Gospels' relationship between the carpet pages (pure abstract pattern), the evangelist portrait pages (the author as narrator-figure), and the incipit pages (the decorated opening words of each Gospel) constructs a visual argument about the nature of sacred narrative: the Word is pattern and image before it is text, and the illuminated manuscript's visual elaboration of scripture is itself a form of theological commentary and narrative interpretation. |
| 155 | Yaxchilán | top-pick | The carved limestone lintels at Yaxchilán narrate specific historical events — royal bloodletting rituals, dynastic ceremonies, the capture of named enemies — with dated inscriptions that make them among the most precisely documented historical narratives in pre-Columbian art. The lintels demonstrate that Maya narrative art was concerned with specific historical facts (the names, dates, and participants of ritual events) as well as mythological precedent, making them historical chronicles as well as sacred documents. |
| 24 | Last Judgment of Hu-Nefer | related | The Book of the Dead papyrus organizes the narrative of the soul's afterlife journey — from death through the weighing of the heart to the final judgment — into a sequential visual program that the deceased was meant to navigate literally, the papyrus functioning as both map and guide. The narrative sequence encodes Egyptian beliefs about what happens after death in a form that is simultaneously storytelling and ritual instruction. |
| 33 | Niobides Krater | related | The red-figure krater attributed to the Niobid Painter is the AP exam's key example of Greek vase painting confronting the challenge of depicting a complex multi-figure mythological narrative (Apollo and Artemis killing the children of Niobe) across a curved ceramic surface. The painter's solution — figures placed at different ground levels to suggest spatial depth without a conventional ground line — marks an important formal innovation in Greek narrative pictorial convention. |
| 38 | Great Altar of Zeus at Pergamon | related | The Gigantomachy frieze narrates the mythological battle between gods and Giants across all four sides of the altar base in a continuous, densely populated narrative program. The emotional intensity, the individualized figures, and the dramatic use of the building's spatial sequence (approaching the staircase, ascending between the battling figures) make the narrative spatially immersive — the visitor enters the story rather than observing it from outside. |
| 51 | San Vitale | related | The San Vitale mosaics deploy narrative in service of theological argument: the Old Testament scenes in the lunettes (Abraham's sacrifice of Isaac, Abel and Melchizedek offering to God) are typologically related to the Eucharistic offering that Justinian and Theodora make in the apse mosaics, constructing a three-part narrative argument that the emperor's liturgical gifts fulfill the Old Testament's sacred story. |
| 61 | Dedication Page with Blanche of Castile and King Louis IX, from Bibles moralisées | related | The Bibles moralisées are the most systematic example of typological narrative in medieval art: each page pairs a biblical scene (above) with its moral or typological interpretation (below) in a structure that tells two stories simultaneously — the literal biblical narrative and its allegorical meaning for contemporary Christian life. The dedication page's pairing of royal patrons with the image of God as architect adds a third narrative layer about the relationship between royal and divine authority over sacred storytelling. |
| 64 | Golden Haggadah | related | The Golden Haggadah's illuminated cycles — depicting the Plagues of Egypt, scenes of Liberation, and Preparation for Passover — visualize the Exodus narrative for use during the Passover Seder, the ritual re-enactment of that narrative. The illuminations are not merely illustrative but participatory: they prepare the reader to re-experience the Exodus story as if personally present, making the visual narrative a tool for ritual remembrance. |
| 81 | Frontispiece of the Codex Mendoza | related | The Codex Mendoza's narrative structure — the founding of Tenochtitlan in the frontispiece, followed by annual tribute records and a section on Aztec daily life — tells three interlocking stories: the mythological origin of the Aztec empire, its administrative present, and the social fabric that sustained it. The codex's creation under Spanish direction adds a fourth narrative layer about who controls the telling of indigenous history after conquest. |
| 86 | Henri IV Receives the Portrait of Marie de' Medici | related | The Marie de' Medici Cycle constructs a biographical narrative of the queen regent through 24 paintings, each depicting a key moment of her life framed by mythological allegory. The sequence is a visual biography — the first extended secular biographical narrative program in Western painting — and its mythological framing argues that Marie's personal story participates in a larger providential narrative about French history. |
| 91 | Las Meninas | related | Velázquez's painting encodes a narrative puzzle: the painter at his canvas, the Infanta and her attendants in the foreground, the royal couple visible only in the mirror — what story is being told, and from whose viewpoint? The painting's narrative is deliberately incomplete, posing the question of who is the subject and who is the viewer as itself the story. |
| 143 | Dream of a Sunday Afternoon in Alameda Park | related | Rivera's mural brings together figures from five centuries of Mexican history in a single dream-space walk through the Alameda Park: Cortés, the Inquisition, Maximilian, Benito Juárez, Frida Kahlo, José Guadalupe Posada, and Rivera as a child all share the same promenade. The mural tells Mexico's entire history as a simultaneous dream, collapsing linear narrative into a spatial panorama where past and present coexist. |
| 165 | Painted elk hide | related | Cotsiogo's painted elk hide narrates the artist's own life history in a first-person visual autobiography: battle exploits, ceremonial scenes, and encounters with Europeans are depicted in the Plains pictorial tradition's visual shorthand — the bird above a figure indicates flight or speed, the horse and rider indicate a raid, the war honors accumulated across a life are recorded as a cumulative visual narrative. |
| 177 | Lukasa (memory board) | related | The Luba lukasa memory board is a mnemonic narrative device: the arrangement of beads and shells on the board encodes genealogical, historical, and ritual knowledge that trained Mbudye Society members can read as an oral narrative. The board does not tell a story that can be read cold by an outsider, but in the hands of a trained reader it is a complete narrative instrument — demonstrating that visual narrative can operate through abstract spatial arrangement rather than figurative representation. |
| 189 | Bahram Gur Fights the Karg | related | This Il-Khanid Shahnama folio illustrates an episode from Firdausi's Persian epic — the legendary king Bahram Gur fighting a mythical rhinoceros-like beast — in a format that integrates text and image into a unified narrative field. The Persian manuscript illustration tradition developed a sophisticated visual language for depicting the Shahnama's stories, and the Great Il-Khanid Shahnama was one of its most ambitious productions. |
| 229 | A Book from the Sky | related | Xu Bing's installation — books and scrolls covered in invented Chinese characters that look authentic but are unreadable — is a work about the failure of narrative: the viewer approaches the text expecting to read a story but finds that the visual apparatus of meaningful narrative (the book, the scroll, the Chinese character) has been systematically emptied of meaning. The work interrogates what narrative requires of both the teller and the reader, and what happens when those conditions are deliberately undermined. |
| 232 | Dancing at the Louvre | related | Faith Ringgold's story quilt — with hand-written narrative text in the fabric border around the central painted scene of Black women dancing in the Louvre — combines two narrative traditions: the African American story quilt (which uses textile and text to preserve community history and personal narrative) and Western painting (which typically excludes the kind of figures Ringgold depicts as central subjects). The work tells multiple stories simultaneously: the story depicted in the painted scene, the story written in the border, and the story of whose narratives get told in whose institutional spaces. |
