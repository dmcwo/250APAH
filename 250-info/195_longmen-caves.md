## #195 — Longmen Caves (Grottoes)

**Late Northern Wei through early Tang dynasties, China** · c. 493–1127 C.E. (main phase 5th–8th c.) · Limestone with remnants of pigment · Luoyang, Henan province, China · ~1 km along the Yi River

### Summary

A massive cliffside complex of Buddhist cave-shrines carved under **imperial patronage**, culminating in the colossal Vairocana Buddha of the Tang-era Fengxian Temple.

- **Historical Context.** Begun in 493 C.E. after **Emperor Xiao Wen** of the Northern Wei moved his capital south to **Luoyang** and embraced Chinese/Buddhist culture. Carving continued across the Sui and into the early Tang, reaching its peak under **Empress Wu Zetian** in the late 7th century. Over 2,300 niches, 2,800 inscriptions, and ~100,000 figures were cut into the limestone cliffs flanking the Yi River. Indian and Central Asian Buddhist styles arrived via the Silk Road.
- **Function.** Devotional cave-temples for meditation and merit-making; sponsors (emperors, monks, lay donors) dedicated niches and images to honor deceased relatives or pray for the dynasty.
- **Content.** The **Fengxian Temple** features a ~55-ft-high seated **Vairocana Buddha** flanked by bodhisattvas, disciples, heavenly kings, and vajrapani guardians — said to resemble Empress Wu. The earlier **Central Binyang Cave**, sponsored by Emperor Xuan Wu in memory of his father, shows a more linear, elongated Wei style. Pentads, haloes, celestial flying figures, and carved lotus ceilings.
- **Form.** High-relief colossal figures in the Tang caves have full, fleshy bodies and serene smiles; Wei-period figures are flatter and more abstract, with drapery stylized into rhythmic patterns. Originally painted in brilliant blue, red, ochre, and gold — mostly lost to weathering.

**Vocabulary:** limestone · Buddhism · stupa · stele · relics · imperial patronage · Vairocana
