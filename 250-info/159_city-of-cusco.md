### Summary

The **Inka imperial capital** — "navel" of the empire — and the Spanish colonial city layered over it.

- **Historical Context.** The name *Cusco* derives from a Quechua word meaning "navel" or "center." A minor city-state before **Pachacuti** expanded the Inka empire in the 1400s; at Spanish contact it held up to 200,000 people. The city was divided into four quarters for immigrants from the four imperial provinces and into upper (**hanan**) and lower (**hurin**) halves along ancestral lines. Said to be laid out in plan as a **puma**, with **Sacsayhuaman** as the head. After the 1533 Spanish conquest, colonial Baroque churches — notably Santo Domingo — were built directly on top of Inka foundations.
- **Function.** Axis mundi — political, religious, economic, and military capital. **Qorikancha** ("Golden Enclosure") was the temple of the sun god **Inti**; Sacsayhuaman was its fortress and ceremonial plaza.
- **Content.** City of **kanchas** — enclosed building-groups around courtyards — of which Qorikancha was the grandest. The fortress walls of Sacsayhuaman include the famous **Stone of Twelve Angles**. After 1533 the Spanish built the Church of Santo Domingo on Qorikancha's plinth, keeping the Inka foundation walls intact.
- **Form.** **Ashlar masonry** — polygonal blocks cut so precisely that no mortar is needed — for elite walls, with thatch and mud brick for lower-status construction. The fit of stones (including one with 12 angles) resists earthquakes. The overall plan uses hierarchy of scale, with Sacsayhuaman dominating the north.

**Vocabulary:** ashlar masonry · kancha · hanan / hurin · hierarchy of scale
