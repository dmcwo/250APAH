## #65 — Alhambra

**Nasrid Dynasty** · 1354–1391 C.E. · Whitewashed adobe stucco, wood, tile, paint, and gilding · Granada, Spain · 26 acres

### Summary

The fortified palace-city of the **Nasrid** sultans — the **last Muslim rulers** of Iberia — where carved stucco, tilework, poetry, fountains, and gardens create a paradisiacal retreat inside austere outer walls.

- **Historical Context.** Built by the **Nasrid dynasty** (1232–1492), Islam's last toehold in Spain, after the Christian **Reconquista** had taken most of the peninsula. Founded by **Muhammad I**; the most celebrated palace interiors are the work of **Yusuf I** (Comares Palace) and **Muhammad V** (Palace of the Lions). Conquered by the Catholic Monarchs (Ferdinand and Isabella) in 1492; served as a Christian royal residence and later fell into neglect until 19th-century restoration.
- **Function.** A **fortified palace-city** — seat of Nasrid government and royal residence. Contained within the walls: three palaces, an alcazaba (citadel), mosque, baths, workshops, and a town.
- **Content.** The **Court of the Lions** (12 marble lions around a fountain), the **Hall of the Two Sisters** (muqarnas vault of some 5,000 cells), the **Comares Palace** (Throne room), the **Generalife** garden villa, and the **Partal Palace**. Arabic **poetry by Ibn Zamrak** carved at eye level "voices" the palace's parts.
- **Form.** **Whitewashed adobe stucco** over brick; **tile** dadoes in intricate geometric patterns; **muqarnas** (stalactite) vaulting; courtyard fountains and reflecting pools. **Horseshoe and lobed arches**; a plain exterior concealing elaborate interiors — a legacy of Umayyad taste. Water throughout reflects the Qur'anic vision of paradise as a "garden watered by delightful streams."

**Vocabulary:** medieval palaces · Palacio de los Leones · Generalife · Salón de Comares · Islamic architecture · Moorish citadel · Cuarto Dorado · Hall of Two Sisters (muqarnas)
