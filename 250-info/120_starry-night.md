## #120 — The Starry Night

**Vincent van Gogh** · 1889 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Museum of Modern Art, New York · 2′5″ × 3′0″

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Van_Gogh_-_Starry_Night_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/500px-Van_Gogh_-_Starry_Night_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" width="240" alt="The Starry Night">

### Summary

A Post-Impressionist night landscape painted largely from imagination and memory while Van Gogh was committed in an asylum.

- **Historical Context.** Van Gogh was a Dutch painter and major figure of **Post-Impressionism**. After moving to Paris in 1886 he adopted brighter palettes and looser brushwork under the influence of the impressionists and neo-impressionists. *The Starry Night* was painted while he was at the **Saint-Paul-de-Mausole asylum**, to which he had been admitted after cutting off his own ear. He was deeply religious, expressed in letters his wish to paint the starry night, and produced the work largely in the studio from imagination, memory, and earlier works. Only one painting sold in his lifetime; he was also a socialist who believed modern life separated people from one another.
- **Function.** To express his emotional and psychological responses to the world. While battling mental illness, Van Gogh sought respite through painting; dissatisfied with modern life, he emphasized nature to reconnect viewers with themselves. He felt he should paint a scene as he experienced, imagined, and interpreted it rather than exactly as seen.
- **Content.** The **cypress tree** reaches to the sky from the ground like a flame — sky representing death, earth representing life, the tree a bridge linking the earthly realm to the afterlife (a period belief held people journeyed to the stars after death). The brightest star is **Venus**, whose namesake goddess evokes Van Gogh's longing for a companion. The pulsing sky shows the power of nature; the swirling waves match astronomical observations of nebulae.
- **Form.** Post-Impressionist **impasto** — thick paint, clearly visible brushstrokes rather than blended surfaces — over a composite landscape. Dark nocturnal colors and short strokes give motion and energy. **Prussian blue** (developed in the 19th century to enrich blue) saturates the sky. The composition is ordered by the cypress, the steeple, and the nebulae.

**Vocabulary:** impasto · Post-Impressionism · composite landscape

### Compare-and-contrast exercise (LO 3.5)

**Prompt:** How does context influence artistic decisions about creating a work of art?

> Context of the artist's beliefs and environments can influence artistic decisions in a work of art. For example, Van Gogh was a highly religious man that believed in the afterlife and heaven. The Starry Night contains symbols representing the afterlife, connecting his beliefs to his art. He was also a man who emphasized the beauty of nature. The subject of many of Gogh's paintings focused on nature instead of the modern world. The environment around Gogh during his time in Paris was filled with influential artists that inspired his painting style. The work of impressionists and neo-impressionists led Gogh to develop his own style of post-impressionism in reaction.
