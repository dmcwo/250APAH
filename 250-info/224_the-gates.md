## #224 — The Gates

**Christo and Jeanne-Claude** · 1979–2005 C.E. (installed February 12–27, 2005) · Mixed-media installation: vinyl frames, steel footings, saffron fabric · Central Park, New York City · 7,503 gates, each 16 ft high, spanning 23 miles of walkways

### Summary

A brief, saffron-colored invasion of **Central Park** — a **$21-million temporary public artwork** that reframed an urban landscape for sixteen days.

- **Historical Context.** Proposed in 1979 by the artist duo **Christo and Jeanne-Claude**, the project was rejected multiple times by New York City over concerns about damage to the park. It was finally approved under Mayor Bloomberg and installed from **February 12 to 27, 2005**. Christo and Jeanne-Claude **self-funded the entire $21 million cost** through sales of preparatory drawings, refusing sponsorships. About four million visitors came during the installation.
- **Function.** Public installation art reimagining the **designed landscape** of Central Park (by Frederick Law Olmsted and Calvert Vaux, 1858) and making viewers aware of the walkways, of the park as construct, and of art as ephemeral experience.
- **Content.** 7,503 rectangular **gates**, each 16 ft high and of varying widths (5½–18 ft) matching their path, threaded along 23 miles of walkways. From each hung a panel of saffron nylon fabric that fluttered in the winter wind.
- **Form.** Repetition and seriality — identical frames in rhythm. Brilliant saffron color against leafless winter trees and snow-covered ground. After deinstallation, materials were recycled.

**Vocabulary:** Earthworks · site-specific
