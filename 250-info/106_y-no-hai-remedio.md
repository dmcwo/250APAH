## #106 — Y no hai remedio (And There's Nothing to Be Done), from Los Desastres de la Guerra, plate 15

**Francisco de Goya** · 1810–1823 C.E. (published 1863) · Etching, drypoint, burin, and burnishing · Metropolitan Museum of Art · 14 × 16.7 cm

### Summary

A **print protest** against Napoleonic occupation — one of 82 bitter etchings chronicling the Peninsular War.

- **Historical Context.** Made during the **Peninsular War** (1808–1814), after Napoleon's brother Joseph was installed as king of Spain. **Francisco de Goya** — former court painter to Charles IV — secretly produced the *Disasters of War* series (82 plates) between 1810 and 1820; they were too inflammatory to publish in his lifetime and only appeared posthumously in **1863**.
- **Function.** Protest art. Indicts the **atrocities committed on civilians** during the French occupation and Spanish reprisals, and the pointless cruelty of war generally. "**And there is nothing to be done**" — the caption — underscores helpless inevitability.
- **Content.** A man in ragged white, **blindfolded and tied to a pole**, awaits execution by firing squad; a mangled corpse lies at his feet; more bound victims line up behind. A row of **rifles** projects into the scene from the right, aimed at the central figure.
- **Form.** Black-and-white etching with dense cross-hatching (burin, drypoint, burnishing) producing rich shadow and tenebrous atmosphere. The tied figure in near-Christ-like white contrasts starkly with encompassing darkness; extreme depth suggests the slaughter continues indefinitely.

**Vocabulary:** drypoint · burnisher · etching · The Disasters of War
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.3, 2.1
