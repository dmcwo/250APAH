## #66 — Annunciation Triptych (Merode Altarpiece)

**Workshop of Robert Campin (Northern Renaissance)** · 1427–1432 C.E. · Oil on wood · The Cloisters, Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York

### Summary

A small **Northern Renaissance triptych** by the **Workshop of Robert Campin** relocating the **Annunciation** into a meticulously detailed Flemish **bourgeois interior** — an emblem of how private devotion and a rising **merchant class** reshaped religious art.

- **Historical Context.** Early-15th-century Flanders was a booming trade center; a new **merchant middle class** commissioned small devotional works for home use, not church display. The Campin workshop (often identified with the Master of Flémalle) pioneered the **Early Netherlandish oil** technique and its hyper-detailed naturalism.
- **Function.** A private **devotional altarpiece** for a wealthy patron (a donor couple, probably Peter Engelbrecht and Margarete Scrynmaker, kneel at left). Meant for use in a home oratory, not a public church.
- **Content.** **Center panel:** the **Annunciation** — Gabriel appears to Mary as she reads; a tiny **Christ-child bearing a cross** slides down rays of light toward her ear (the **Incarnation**). Symbolic objects saturate the room: extinguished candle (God's presence), lilies (purity), towel (Mary's role as vessel). **Left panel:** the donors kneel at the door; **right panel:** **Joseph** in his carpentry shop, making **mousetraps** (a metaphor: Christ sprung on the devil, per Augustine).
- **Form.** **Oil on wood**, a Northern innovation that permits micro-detail, glazes, and subtle atmosphere. Inconsistent perspective and domestic (anachronistic) setting; hyperreal textures — wood, metal, cloth, light.
