## #151 — Spiral Jetty

**Robert Smithson** (Environmental / Land Art) · 1970 C.E. · Earthwork: mud, precipitated salt crystals, rocks, and water coil · Rozel Point, Great Salt Lake, Utah

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Spiral-jetty-from-rozel-point.png/330px-Spiral-jetty-from-rozel-point.png" width="240" alt="Spiral Jetty">

### Summary

The defining **Land Art / earthwork** of the American 1970s.

- **Historical Context.** Built at Rozel Point on the **Great Salt Lake** by Smithson with a construction crew — the many hands involved deliberately refused the idea that art is a unique product of a single artist. Part of the broader 1960s–70s move to pull art out of galleries.
- **Function.** To relocate the work of art from the museum into the landscape. Meaning comes from the work's **location** and from its changing state over time rather than from any fixed content.
- **Content.** A 1,500-foot counterclockwise spiral coil extending into the pink salt waters of the Great Salt Lake, inspired partly by the microbial red-pink water color and the mythic sense of the site.
- **Form.** Basalt rocks, mud, and salt crystals. Originally black basalt, now encrusted white with precipitated salt against the pink water. Submerged and re-exposed as lake levels change.

**Vocabulary:** earthwork · Land Art · environmental art
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.2, 3.1
