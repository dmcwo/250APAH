## #48 — Catacomb of Priscilla

**Late Antique Europe** · c. 200–400 C.E. · Excavated tufa and fresco · Rome, Italy

### Summary

An **Early Christian** underground burial complex on the Via Salaria — its plastered-over tufa walls hold some of the **earliest Christian paintings**, including an iconic image of the **Good Shepherd**.

- **Historical Context.** Begun in the second century C.E., the Catacomb of Priscilla is the oldest part of the Roman Christian catacombs — dug into the soft volcanic **tufa** outside the city walls. Early Christians could not build above-ground churches, so burial and occasional clandestine worship happened here.
- **Function.** **Christian burial site** and occasional meeting place. Stacked passageways ("galleries") line niches (**loculi**) that held wrapped bodies; larger chambers (**cubicula**) provided space for funerary rites and imagery.
- **Content.** Wall paintings of Old and New Testament scenes — Jonah, the Three Young Men in the Furnace, the Good Shepherd — meant to provide hope for salvation. The famous **Greek Chapel** decoration includes the earliest known depiction of the Virgin and Child.
- **Form.** **Plaster** over rock, then **fresco** painting in a late-Roman style with loose brushwork and flat color fields that prefigures Byzantine iconography. The complex is five miles long and holds some **40,000 tombs**.
