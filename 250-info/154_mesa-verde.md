## #154 — Mesa Verde cliff dwellings

**Ancestral Puebloan (Anasazi)** · 450–1300 C.E. · Sandstone · Montezuma County, Colorado · up to ~150 rooms per dwelling (Cliff Palace)

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Cliff_Palace-Colorado-Mesa_Verde_NP.jpg/330px-Cliff_Palace-Colorado-Mesa_Verde_NP.jpg" width="240" alt="Mesa Verde cliff dwellings">

### Summary

Stone-and-mortar **cliff dwellings** of the Ancestral Puebloan people.

- **Historical Context.** Built by the **Ancestral Puebloans** (formerly called Anasazi, a term now rejected by modern Puebloans) — farmers of corn, beans, and squash in the Four Corners region. They likely descended from the Oshara tradition and Picosa culture. The cliff dwellings were constructed from roughly 1000–1100 C.E. and abandoned during a later migration for still-debated reasons (drought, resource depletion, conflict).
- **Function.** Residential and communal. Each dwelling clustered rooms around a **kiva** — a circular, subterranean chamber used for ceremonies, rituals, and daily life.
- **Content.** More than 600 dwellings survive at Mesa Verde; the largest, **Cliff Palace**, has ~150 rooms and over 20 kivas. Multi-story structures reached only by retractable ladders; interior walls painted with landscape murals.
- **Form.** **Sandstone** with mud mortar, wooden-beam supports, adobe. Rectangular rooms for stability; cylindrical kivas for ritual. Buildings stack one to four stories and fit the contours of the natural cliff alcoves.

**Vocabulary:** kiva · Ancestral Puebloan · cliff dwelling
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4
