## #4 — Running Horned Woman

### Summary

A Saharan rock painting often read as a **goddess or ritual figure**, painted when the region was green savanna rather than desert.

- **Historical Context.** Found deep in a canyon at **Tassili n'Ajjer, Algeria**, a UNESCO site rich in Neolithic rock art. Long known to local inhabitants before being documented by Lieutenant Brenans (French Foreign Legion) and publicized by ethnographer **Henri Lhote**, whose early Egyptian-influence reading has since been discredited. Belongs to the **Round Head period** of Tassili rock art.
- **Function.** Unknown, but the figure's ceremonial dress, horns, and scale suggest a **religious or ritual role** — perhaps a goddess, priestess, or dancer. Its remote, hard-to-access location hints at sacred use.
- **Content.** A large horned female figure in full stride (or dance), dotted with white markings, surrounded by smaller, less-detailed figures — an early use of **hierarchical scale**.
- **Form.** Pigment on stone, sharply outlined in white. The body is rendered in **twisted perspective** (frontal torso, profile legs). Flat, overlapping figures create no sense of depth.

**Vocabulary:** pictograph · hierarchical scale · twisted perspective
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.4, 3.3
