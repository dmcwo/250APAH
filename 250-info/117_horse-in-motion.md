## #117 — The Horse in Motion

**Eadweard Muybridge** · 1878 C.E. · Albumen print · San Francisco · 24 photographs

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Eadweard_Muybridge-Sallie_Gardner_1878.jpg/250px-Eadweard_Muybridge-Sallie_Gardner_1878.jpg" width="240" alt="The Horse in Motion">

### Summary

A motion-capture photographic series that settled whether a galloping horse ever goes **fully airborne** — and helped lay the groundwork for cinema.

- **Historical Context.** Muybridge moved to the United States from a small English town in 1850, worked briefly as a bookseller, then took up photography. He rose to fame in 1872 helping **Leland Stanford** — politician and co-founder of Stanford University — settle the popular debate over whether a horse goes completely airborne during a gallop. Using new motion-capture technology, Muybridge produced a never-before-seen moving-image series; up until then artists had always depicted galloping horses with all four feet outstretched. He later copied the silhouettes onto a disc viewed through his **zoopraxiscope**, an ancestor of the movie projector, and his work is foundational to modern cinema.
- **Function.** Created to capture the running motion of a horse frame by frame and resolve Stanford's $25,000 wager. Muybridge set up a line of glass-plate cameras along the racetrack at Stanford's **Palo Alto Stock Farm** (now Stanford University), each shutter triggered by a **tripwire** as the horse passed. The resulting photographs confirmed Stanford's bet.
- **Content.** Shows **Sallie Gardner**, one of Stanford's Standardbred trotting horses, airborne at the trot. Due to low camera quality, the horse and rider appear only as dark shapes against a white background. In total, 24 images capture the horse in various stages of its running cycle.
- **Form.** A sequential documentation of a moving subject. The horse stays centered in frame even at top speed thanks to the tripwire system. Each image shows a distinct position; the gait resolves into a 1-2-3-4 beat — left hind, left front, right hind, right front.

**Vocabulary:** zoopraxiscope · gallop
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 2.1

### Compare-and-contrast exercise (LO 3.5)

**Prompt:** Based on the form, function, and/or context of Muybridge's Horse in Motion, infer or explain the possible intentions for creating a specific work of art.

> The horse is in the center of every photograph. The image set depicts the progression of a horse galloping. Because each image captures a still moment in time, it allows the viewer to more closely observe the gait of a horse in motion. During the time period of creation, how or even if a horse went completely airborne while running was a highly debated question. So, the artist's intention was to precisely document a horse's gallop for scientific observation and to settle a longstanding inquiry.
