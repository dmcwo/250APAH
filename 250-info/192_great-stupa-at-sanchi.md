## #192 — The Great Stupa at Sanchi

**Buddhist India (Mauryan through Shunga/Satavahana)** · 3rd century B.C.E.–1st century C.E. · Stone masonry · Sanchi, Madhya Pradesh, India · Dome ~120 ft diameter, ~54 ft tall

### Summary

The earliest and most iconic surviving **Buddhist stupa**, built to enshrine relics of the Buddha.

- **Historical Context.** Originally commissioned by **Emperor Ashoka** (r. 269–232 B.C.E.) of the Mauryan dynasty, who converted to Buddhism after the Kalinga war and sponsored the spread of the religion through an extensive building campaign — legend credits him with 84,000 stupas. The core brick dome was enlarged in the 2nd century B.C.E. under the Shungas; the carved stone **toranas** (gateways) were added c. 1st century B.C.E.–1st century C.E.
- **Function.** A reliquary monument housing sacred relics of the Buddha. Pilgrims **circumambulate** the dome clockwise along a raised walkway, earning merit; the act re-enacts the cosmic order.
- **Content.** Solid hemispherical dome (*anda*) on a circular plinth, crowned by a square *harmika* and a triple parasol *yashti* (world axis). Four torana gateways at the cardinal directions carry dense narrative relief of **jataka tales** (Buddha's past lives) and yakshi tree-spirit figures. The Buddha himself is rendered only through symbols (footprints, empty throne, wheel) — aniconic.
- **Form.** Rubble-core dome faced with dressed stone. Low-relief and high-relief carving cover the toranas, bordering on horror vacui; the dome surface is unadorned. Axial symmetry at cardinal points.

**Vocabulary:** sepulchral · stupa · torana · yashti · harmika · jataka · circumambulation
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.2, 3.3
