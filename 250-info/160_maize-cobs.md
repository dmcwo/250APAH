## #160 — Maize cobs

**Inka** · c. 1440–1533 C.E. · Sheet metal / repoussé, metal alloys (gold, silver, copper)

### Summary

Life-size **gold-and-silver maize cobs** from the gardens of the Inka **Qorikancha**.

- **Historical Context.** Produced during the Inka empire's height, when tribute from across the Andes flowed into **Cusco**. Part of the legendary garden attached to the Qorikancha temple — the Spanish described an entire garden of gold and silver llamas, women, men, and plants that performed imperial power and wealth. Most of it was melted down after conquest.
- **Function.** A political and religious display: evidence of the empire's reach and of the Inka ruler's divine descent from the sun god **Inti**. Corn was the sacred, life-sustaining staple of Andean civilization.
- **Content.** Ears of **maize** rendered at natural size, each kernel individually formed. Unusually naturalistic — Inka art generally favored stylized or geometric form, so the realism here is a stylistic choice.
- **Form.** Beaten **sheet metal / repoussé**, combining silver and copper alloys to mimic the colors and textures of actual corn. Surviving examples often show husks peeled back to reveal kernels.

**Vocabulary:** repoussé · sheet metal · Qorikancha · Inti
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.3, 2.2
