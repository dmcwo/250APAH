## #115 — Olympia

**Édouard Manet** · 1863 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Musée d'Orsay, Paris · 51 in × 75 in

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Edouard_Manet_-_Olympia_-_Google_Art_ProjectFXD.jpg/500px-Edouard_Manet_-_Olympia_-_Google_Art_ProjectFXD.jpg" width="240" alt="Olympia">

### Summary

A direct, confrontational nude that challenged academic taste by swapping the goddess for a **Parisian prostitute**.

- **Historical Context.** Manet painted within an artistic era dominated by the **French Royal Academy** and its standards of taste, and designed the work as a response to the Academy's lofty ideals for the female nude. He was also living through rapid industrialization and population growth, which brought cultural changes he wanted to represent rather than ignore.
- **Function.** Intended to **challenge artistic conventions** in style, subject matter, and theme. The broad, quick, visible brushstrokes stand against the academy's preference for invisible handling, and the choice to show a Parisian prostitute with her maid rather than a goddess or odalisque was considered too vulgar for exhibition.
- **Content.** A prostitute and her maid, surrounded by signs of prostitution and the seedy underbelly of modern Paris: the **cat at Olympia's feet**, the name *Olympia* itself, and the ornaments she wears all carry that association. The **maid** — rendered through the racial stereotypes of the time — stands in the background presenting flowers.
- **Form.** The nude figure is very pale and positioned atop pale sheets, contrasting starkly with the **dark background** and the maid's complexion. The effect is amplified by the painting's **shallow depth**, which seems to cover only a few feet.

**Vocabulary:** Realism · Salon · Impressionism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3
