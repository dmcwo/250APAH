## #229 — A Book from the Sky (Tianshu)

**Xu Bing (China)** · c. 1987–1991 C.E. · Hand-printed books and wall/ceiling scrolls printed from hand-carved wood letterpress type; ink on paper · Installation, Metropolitan Museum of Art · Books 18⅛ × 20 in each; ceiling scrolls 38 in × ~115 ft

### Summary

An immersive library of **invented, unreadable Chinese characters** — a post-Cultural-Revolution meditation on language, meaning, and cultural rupture.

- **Historical Context.** **Xu Bing** (b. 1955) grew up in Beijing during the **Cultural Revolution**, a period when language was politicized — characters were simplified and countless books destroyed. Both his parents were scholars; he was exposed to classical texts forbidden to most. After the Revolution's end, Xu hand-carved **over 4,000 invented, meaningless Chinese characters** using the standard Kangxi radicals, and printed them with traditional wood letterpress type into bound books and giant scrolls. First exhibited in Beijing in 1988; it shocked Chinese intellectuals.
- **Function.** **Conceptual critique**. By making characters look authoritative yet deliver no meaning, Xu confronts viewers with the fragility of language and the experience of Chinese readers cut off from their textual heritage by the Revolution's reforms. Western viewers who can't read Chinese experience exclusion either way.
- **Content.** Hand-bound books in the traditional **thread-binding** style, enormous wall scrolls, and suspended ceiling scrolls, all covered in carefully set rows of nonsense characters.
- **Form.** Strict, authoritative black-on-white columns modeled on classical print culture. Gives the illusion of sacred scripture; the scale dwarfs the viewer. Impeccable traditional craft at the service of meaninglessness.

**Vocabulary:** wood letterpress · radical · Kangxi radicals
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.2, 3.3
