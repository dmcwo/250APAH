## #197 — Todai-ji

**Japanese Buddhist, various architects and sculptors (Unkei and Kaikei for Nio guardians)** · 743 C.E.; rebuilt after 1180 C.E. (Kamakura) and again c. 1700 · Wood, bronze, plaster · Nara, Japan · Daibutsuden 187 ft long, 160 ft high

### Summary

The **Great Eastern Temple** at Nara — the head temple of Japanese Kegon Buddhism and once the world's largest wooden building, housing Japan's largest bronze Buddha.

- **Historical Context.** Commissioned by **Emperor Shomu** in 743 C.E. during the Nara period as the central temple of a nationwide Buddhist network, tying the state to the newly imperial religion. Construction required nearly all the copper in Japan for the **Daibutsu** (Great Buddha), a monumental undertaking that strained the treasury. The complex was destroyed during the **Genpei War** (1180); the Kamakura-era reconstruction introduced a more robust, Chinese-influenced (daibutsuyō) style and the **Nandaimon** (Great South Gate) with its pair of guardian kings carved by **Unkei and Kaikei** (1203).
- **Function.** Main temple for state Buddhism; center for scholarship, sutra translation, and lay worship. The network of provincial kokubunji temples radiated from it.
- **Content.** The **Daibutsuden** houses a 49-ft bronze seated Vairocana Buddha (current statue 17th c.) flanked by Bodhisattvas. The **Nandaimon** frames the 26-ft-tall **Ungyō and Agyō** guardian kings (Nio), muscular wood sculptures representing birth and death. Also on site: Nigatsudo, Hokke-do, and the Shoso-in treasury preserving Silk Road objects.
- **Form.** Post-and-beam wooden architecture with deep, tiled hip-and-gable roofs; bracketing systems hold up wide eaves. The Daibutsuden appears two-storied externally but is a single space inside.

**Vocabulary:** Daibutsu · Daibutsuden · Nandaimon · Nio guardian kings
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.2
