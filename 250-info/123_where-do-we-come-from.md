## #123 — Where Do We Come From? What Are We? Where Are We Going?

**Paul Gauguin** · 1897–1898 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Museum of Fine Arts, Boston · 139 × 375 cm (55 × 148 in)

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Paul_Gauguin_-_D%27ou_venons-nous.jpg/500px-Paul_Gauguin_-_D%27ou_venons-nous.jpg" width="240" alt="Where Do We Come From? What Are We? Where Are We Going?">

### Summary

A large symbolic canvas meditating on the **cycle of human life**, painted in Tahiti during a period of personal crisis.

- **Historical Context.** Paul Gauguin was a French artist initially trained in Impressionism in Paris, where he befriended **Pissarro** and **Van Gogh** and was heavily influenced by **Cézanne**, whose parallel, constructive brushstrokes he adopted. After several visits to Tahiti and a desire to escape what he saw as artificial European culture, he settled in French Polynesia. The work was produced shortly after the death of his beloved daughter **Aline** — he was in grief, had attempted suicide by self-poisoning, and was already suffering the degenerative effects of syphilis. He died at 54 and was buried in the Marquesas Islands.
- **Function.** To depict Gauguin's fascination with Tahitian life and culture — a place he sought out in isolation from modern society after becoming disillusioned with his career as a stockbroker in Europe — and to express his emotional suffering after his daughter's death and his interpretation of the life cycle.
- **Content.** A symbolism of the cycle of life from birth to old age and death. On the **far right**, three nude women — two meeting the viewer's gaze — and beside them a **newborn child on a rock**, the beginning of life. In the **center**, a figure **picks fruit from a tree**, a reference to **Adam and Eve** and the Tree of Knowledge — the loss of innocence. On the **left**, an old and weak figure **covers her ears**, unable to find peace; this pose recalls Munch's *The Scream* and may parallel Gauguin himself, in despair from the memories of his earlier life.
- **Form.** Oil on canvas; Gauguin is renowned for his masterful, emotive use of color. Though the palette is largely warm, the tone gives a deep and dark feeling. There is a detectable contrast of light and dark: the **background is mainly blue and green**, depicting an Eden-like garden, while the **foreground figures are yellow and red**, highlighting the artist's focus.

**Vocabulary:** Post-Impressionism · Tahiti · catechism · Adam and Eve
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 1.4
