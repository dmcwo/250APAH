## #111 — Slave Ship (Slavers Throwing Overboard the Dead and Dying, Typhoon Coming On)

**Joseph Mallord William Turner** (British) · 1840 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Museum of Fine Arts, Boston · 2.98 × 4.02 ft

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Slave-ship.jpg/330px-Slave-ship.jpg" width="240" alt="Slave Ship">

### Summary

A **Romantic** seascape turning the 1781 **Zong massacre** into an abolitionist plea through the sublime wrath of nature.

- **Historical Context.** **Romanticism** reacted against the Enlightenment's logical, emotionally-removed mindset — instead chasing strong emotions like disgust, horror, awe, and transcendence. Turner was part of the British **Royal Academy**, known for landscapes that manipulated light to create otherworldliness. He was prompted to create this work after reading about the slave ship **The Zong** in Thomas Clarkson's book: in 1781, The Zong's captain threw sick and dying enslaved people overboard to collect insurance money. Slaves had been freed in England since 1833, but exceptions still permitted it.
- **Function.** Painted to speak in favor of the **abolitionist movement**. Turner inscribed his poem "The Fallacies of Hope," expressing the wrath of nature/the universe against those engaging in the slave trade — echoed in the vivid blood-red horizon and chaos of sea and sky.
- **Content.** Depicts the historical event aboard **The Zong**, whose captain discarded sick and dying enslaved people because insurance paid only for accidental drownings, not sickness from poor conditions.
- **Form.** Dramatic color creates awe and otherworldly nature. Light represents God; the brewing storm sky shows the wrath incurred. Rapid brushwork evokes the chaos of the storm. The ship and figures in the ocean are small and hard to make out — the viewer is overcome with the power of the sea and sky.

**Vocabulary:** Romanticism · abolition · The Zong
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4
