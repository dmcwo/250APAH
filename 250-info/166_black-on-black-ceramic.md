## #166 — Black-on-black ceramic vessel

**Maria Martínez and Julian Martínez** (Tewa, Puebloan, San Ildefonso Pueblo, New Mexico) · c. mid-20th century C.E. · Blackware ceramic · 11⅛ × 13 in.

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Maria_Martinez_pot.jpg/250px-Maria_Martinez_pot.jpg" width="240" alt="Black-on-black ceramic vessel">

### Summary

A **Pueblo revival olla** made for a non-Native fine-arts market, fired to a deep matte-and-glossy black.

- **Historical Context.** **Maria Martínez** (1887–1980) of San Ildefonso Pueblo trained in traditional Pueblo hand-coiling and revived ancient Pueblo **blackware** after excavations in what is now Bandelier National Monument revealed black-on-black pottery sherds of the 12th–17th centuries. The 1880 arrival of the railroad flooded New Mexico with cheap imported pots, displacing utilitarian Pueblo work — Maria responded by shifting toward the Art Deco–era fine-arts market. She won prizes at the St. Louis, New York, and Chicago World's Fairs. Julian, her husband, painted the matte designs.
- **Function.** Non-utilitarian — intentionally fired to be softer and not watertight, so it would be sold as art rather than used for cooking, storage, or ceremony. Its **Art Deco** geometry made it marketable to non-Native buyers.
- **Content.** Two bands of matte stylized natural forms — rain clouds, feathers, cornstalks, a bird in flight — possibly read as a **prayer for rain** rising to the sky.
- **Form.** **Blackware** olla: hand-coiled (no wheel), stone-burnished to a mirror gloss, painted in matte guaco (reduced wild-plant juice), then fired in a manure-smothered reduction fire that blackens the clay. The contrast between matte and burnished black is the signature Martínez effect.

**Vocabulary:** olla · matte · Art Deco · blackware · burnishing
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.2
