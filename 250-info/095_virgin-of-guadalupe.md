## #95 — The Virgin of Guadalupe (Virgen de Guadalupe)

**Miguel González** · c. 1698 C.E. · Oil on canvas on wood, inlaid with mother-of-pearl · LACMA (originally Basilica of Guadalupe, Mexico City) · 39 × 27½ in

### Summary

A luxury **enconchado** version of Mexico's most venerated miraculous image, fusing Catholic devotion, indigenous identity, and Asian-derived craft.

- **Historical Context.** Refers to the 1531 apparition of the Virgin to **Juan Diego Cuauhtlatoatzin**, an early indigenous convert, on the hill of **Tepeyac**. The Virgin appeared with **mestiza features** and spoke Nahuatl, asking for a shrine to replace the pagan Aztec site. Her image miraculously imprinted itself on his **tilma** after he carried Castilian roses to Bishop Zumárraga. The miracle accelerated mass indigenous conversion and became a core symbol of Mexican identity. **Miguel González** — likely mestizo or Amerindian — worked in late 17th-century Mexico and was a leading practitioner of *enconchado* alongside his father and brother.
- **Function.** A devotional icon for prayer and veneration; the expensive materials suggest a wealthy patron's home altar or a church.
- **Content.** The Virgin stands in contemplative prayer with **ashen mestiza skin**, crowned, surrounded by sun rays, standing on a crescent moon — iconography drawn from the **Woman of the Apocalypse** (Revelation). Four corner **roundels** show the apparition narrative; a medallion below carries the **seal of Mexico City**. White lilies (purity) and four-petaled roses (Juan Diego's roses) line the borders.
- **Form.** Oil on canvas laid on wood, with **mother-of-pearl inlay** (*enconchado*) across frame, crown, and mantle — a technique influenced by Japanese *maki-e* lacquer brought by Iberian traders.

**Vocabulary:** enconchado · marianismo · cherub · mestizo/a · tilma · roundel
