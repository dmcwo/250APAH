## #141 — The Migration of the Negro, Panel no. 49

**Jacob Lawrence** (Harlem Renaissance / modernist) · 1940–1941 C.E. · Casein tempera on hardboard · The Phillips Collection, Washington, D.C. · 18 × 12 in.

### Summary

Panel 49 of Lawrence's 60-panel **Migration Series**, captioned *"They found discrimination in the North. It was a different kind."*

- **Historical Context.** Lawrence (b. 1917) moved to Harlem in 1924 and studied at the Harlem Art Workshop during the **Harlem Renaissance**. He looked to African American history for subject matter and painted this as part of his 60-panel series documenting the **Great Migration** — the movement of African Americans from the rural South to northern cities in search of work, housing, and freedom from Jim Crow. The series was split evenly between MoMA and The Phillips Collection in 1942.
- **Function.** To force a white public to see that Northern segregation still existed. Each panel is paired with a caption; the captions form an objective narrative voice that contrasts with the emotional images, juxtaposing an outside view with an inside one.
- **Content.** A dining room split by a taut **gold rope** that literally segregates the space: white diners on one side, Black diners on the other. One white figure looks almost haughty; another is absorbed in his newspaper. The Black figures are silhouetted, given form by the contours of their bodies rather than by faces — only the white figures get individual facial features. A small upper-right figure emphasizes isolation.
- **Form.** **Casein tempera** on hardboard; a neutral palette of browns and greys, flattened background, strong diagonals, silhouetted figures. Simplified angular forms rooted in **Cubism** but stripped down to essentials for narrative immediacy. The gold rope moves the eye across the composition.

**Vocabulary:** narrative · genre painting · Cubism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 2.1
