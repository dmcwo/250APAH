## #121 — The Coiffure

**Mary Cassatt** · 1890–1891 C.E. · Drypoint and aquatint · National Gallery of Art, Washington, D.C. · 14⅜ × 10½ in

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Mary_Cassatt%2C_The_Coiffure%2C_1890-1891%2C_NGA_5933.jpg/250px-Mary_Cassatt%2C_The_Coiffure%2C_1890-1891%2C_NGA_5933.jpg" width="240" alt="The Coiffure">

### Summary

An intimate print that fuses **Japanese ukiyo-e** aesthetics with French Impressionism to portray a moment of bourgeois domestic life.

- **Historical Context.** *The Coiffure* was directly influenced by the **Japonisme** movement of the 19th century. Cassatt was American by heritage but spent most of her life in France; in April 1890 she visited an exhibition of Japanese woodblock prints (**ukiyo-e**) at the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris. European interest in Japanese art had grown since Commodore Perry's 1853 opening of Japan, and Cassatt — like Degas, Pissarro, and Monet — began working in that style. She drew directly on **Kitagawa Utamaro's *Takashima Ohisa Using Two Mirrors to Observe Her Coiffure***, and possibly on Ingres's *La Grande Odalisque*, which she had studied in the 1860s. The result is a cultural blend of Japanese and Impressionist styles.
- **Function.** The work has no explicit traditional function — it shows the impact of foreign influence on art, a glimpse of bourgeois daily life, and the nude female figure presented **without sensuality**.
- **Content.** A half-nude young woman does her hair in a bedroom to get ready for the day. Her back is turned to the viewer, her front reflected in a mirror facing the viewer. There is no pose — she is depicted naturally, unaware of being observed (the central subject turned away from the viewer is an influence from ukiyo-e).
- **Form.** Primarily **pastel colors**, with contrasts between the smooth curves of the figure and the sharp, straight lines of mirror, wall, and furniture. The curvy wallpaper designs contrast with the harder edges elsewhere. Cassatt used **drypoint and aquatint** — an intaglio technique carved into copper plates with acid producing the tones. The work is more intimate and tender than many typical Impressionist works.

**Vocabulary:** Impressionism · aquatint · drypoint · avant-garde · japonisme · coiffure
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.1, 2.2, 2.3

### Compare-and-contrast exercise (LO 3.5)

#### Takashima Ohisa Using Two Mirrors to Observe Her Coiffure (Kitagawa Utamaro, c. 1795) — *not in image set*

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Kitagawa_Utamaro_-_Takashima_Ohisa_Using_Two_Mirrors_to_Observe_Her_Coiffure_Night_of_the_Asakusa_Marketing_Festival_-_MFA_Boston_21.6410FXD.jpg/250px-Kitagawa_Utamaro_-_Takashima_Ohisa_Using_Two_Mirrors_to_Observe_Her_Coiffure_Night_of_the_Asakusa_Marketing_Festival_-_MFA_Boston_21.6410FXD.jpg" width="180" alt="Takashima Ohisa Using Two Mirrors">

**Prompt:** How was a singular piece of artwork, such as Mary Cassatt's *The Coiffure*, or a group of related works, influenced by specific traditions and/or changes?

> The Coiffure was directly influenced by Japanese art; specifically, Takashima Ohisa Using Two Mirrors to Observe Her Coiffure, by Kitagawa Utamaro. Takashima Ohisa Using Two Mirrors to Observe Her Coiffure depicts two women doing their hair and looking in a mirror, much alike The Coiffure. The Japanese style of depicting 'everyday life' is prevalent in the influences on The Coiffure. Additionally, both works have a lack of shadow, stronger outlines, and the central figure(s) are oblivious to the viewer.
