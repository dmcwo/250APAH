## #88 — San Carlo alle Quattro Fontane

**Francesco Borromini (architect)** · 1638–1646 C.E. · Stone and stucco · Rome, Italy · ~20 × 12 m

### Summary

A tiny, **undulating Italian Baroque church** — Borromini's first solo commission and a prototype for dynamic wall architecture.

- **Historical Context.** Designed for the Spanish **Discalced Trinitarian** order on a cramped corner of the Quirinal Hill in Rome named for its **four fountains**. Borromini, trained as a stonemason and nephew/rival of Bernini, had to wrestle with a non-perpendicular, narrow site. His first independent project (façade completed after his 1667 suicide by his nephew Bernardo).
- **Function.** Conventual church for the Trinitarians, a small community of Spanish monks, plus their monastery and cloister; the order was founded to ransom Christians held captive by Muslim corsairs.
- **Content.** An unprecedented **oval plan** derived from a Greek cross stretched on the long axis, fitted ingeniously onto an impossible site. Exterior **façade undulates** in a concave-convex-concave rhythm, broken by niched sculptures. Interior dome is ornamented with deep **coffering** in geometric hexagons, octagons, and Greek crosses that shrink upward to exaggerate height.
- **Form.** Stone and stucco. Serpentine walls, minimal color, dramatic upward compression of the dome, hidden windows washing it with light. The two-story façade shifts in style between Borromini's lower half and his nephew's upper half.

**Vocabulary:** façade · dome · cherubs · coffering
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.4, 2.2, 3.5
