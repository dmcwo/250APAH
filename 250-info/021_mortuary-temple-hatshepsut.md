## #21 — Mortuary Temple of Hatshepsut

**New Kingdom Egypt, 18th Dynasty** · c. 1473–1458 B.C.E. · Sandstone, partially carved into a rock cliff, and red granite · Deir el-Bahri, near Luxor, Egypt · ~97 ft tall

### Summary

A terraced mortuary temple for **Hatshepsut**, one of the few female pharaohs, whose colonnaded levels are carved directly into a **limestone cliff** at Deir el-Bahri.

- **Historical Context.** Commissioned by **Hatshepsut**, who took the throne after her husband's death and ruled for twenty years — often depicting herself in male pharaonic regalia to legitimize her rule. A successful **Punt expedition** during her reign enriched Egypt and is commemorated on the temple's colonnades. Construction took about fifteen years.
- **Function.** A **memorial** to Hatshepsut (she was actually buried in the **Valley of the Kings**), with chapels for **Hathor**, **Anubis**, a royal cult chapel, a solar cult chapel, and a **sanctuary for Amun**. Family members were interred within the complex.
- **Content.** Three broad **terraces** linked by ramps, fronted by colonnades. The **Punt Colonnade** narrates Hatshepsut's trade expedition; the **Birth Colonnade** depicts her as divinely fathered by Amun through Thutmose I, legitimizing her rule. The image-set **Kneeling Statue of Hatshepsut** wears male regalia.
- **Form.** **Sandstone** partly carved into the cliff face behind it — an unusual fusion of freestanding architecture and in situ rock-cut architecture. Unadorned columns meet the entablature directly, producing a starkly rhythmic colonnade. Axial symmetry; large open courtyards.

**Vocabulary:** mortuary temple · pharaoh · courtyard · colonnade · chapel
