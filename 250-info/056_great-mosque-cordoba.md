## #56 — Great Mosque of Córdoba

**Umayyad (Islamic Iberia)** · c. 785–786 C.E. · Stone masonry · Córdoba, Spain · 590 × 425 ft

### Summary

A **hypostyle** mosque of seemingly endless **red-and-white double arches** — built by the exiled Umayyad prince **Abd al-Rahman I** in Iberia, later partly converted into a Christian cathedral.

- **Historical Context.** After the **Abbasids** overthrew the Umayyad dynasty in Damascus (750 C.E.), **Abd al-Rahman I** fled to Spain and established a new Umayyad capital at **Córdoba**, then one of Europe's wealthiest and most learned cities. The mosque's original core was expanded several times; in the 13th century the **Reconquista** reclaimed Córdoba and a Christian cathedral was later built *inside* the mosque.
- **Function.** A **Friday mosque** (jami) and political-symbolic heart of Umayyad al-Andalus. After 1236, repurposed as a Catholic cathedral without demolishing the Islamic fabric.
- **Content.** A vast **hypostyle prayer hall** with **856 reused Roman and Visigothic columns** supporting distinctive **horseshoe arches** in red-brick and white-stone voussoirs. An ornate **mihrab** niche faces Mecca (oriented south — unusual, a local adaptation). Outside: orange-tree courtyard, minaret.
- **Form.** Stone masonry. **Two-tiered horseshoe arches** (lower and upper) multiply the visual height of the reused Roman columns and produce a forest-like interior with no fixed focal point. The mihrab dome is decorated with intricate **ribbed vaulting** and gold mosaic calligraphy.

**Vocabulary:** hypostyle hall · mosque · mihrab
