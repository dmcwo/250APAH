## #113 — The Stone Breakers

**Gustave Courbet** · 1849 C.E. (destroyed in 1945) · Oil on canvas · Gemäldegalerie, Dresden · 5′ 5″ × 8′ 5″

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Gustave_Courbet_-_The_Stonebreakers_-_WGA05457.jpg/330px-Gustave_Courbet_-_The_Stonebreakers_-_WGA05457.jpg" width="240" alt="The Stone Breakers">

### Summary

A foundational **Realist** painting dignifying rural laborers on a monumental scale.

- **Historical Context.** Part of the **Realist** movement. Courbet was inspired after driving on the highway and noticing two men breaking stones; struck by this true expression of poverty, he later arranged for them to sit in his studio. The painting was made only one year after **Marx and Engels** wrote *The Communist Manifesto* (1848). Poverty was extremely prevalent during this time.
- **Function.** Bringing awareness to the brutality behind severe poverty — an extremely realistic depiction of poor people's lives in the 1850s. Fine detail and color scheme allowed wider audiences to understand rural hardship, and emphasize the isolation of being physically and economically trapped.
- **Content.** A daily-life piece recording the abuse and deprivation that was common in mid-century French rural life. The figures break stones down to rubble to be used for paving. "The figures were born poor and will continue being poor for the rest of their existence." The realism allows them to be treated with basic honesty and sincerity.
- **Form.** Oil on canvas. The hill reaches the top of the canvas everywhere except the upper-right corner, where a tiny patch of bright blue sky appears. Brushwork is rough, uncommon for the time. Courbet refused to focus only on the parts of the image that would usually receive the most attention — attending to faces and rock equally. Dominant hues of brown and ochre.

**Vocabulary:** stone · poverty · paving · Realism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 3.2, 3.3
