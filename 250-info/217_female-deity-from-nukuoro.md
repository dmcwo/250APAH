## #217 — Female Deity from Nukuoro

**Nukuoro, Caroline Islands, Micronesia (Polynesian Outlier)** · 18th–19th century C.E. · Wood · Nukuoro, Micronesia · 220 cm

### Summary

A serenely abstracted wood **tino aitu** figure from the Polynesian-outlier atoll of Nukuoro, believed to house ancestral and divine spirits.

- **Historical Context.** Made on **Nukuoro**, a remote atoll in the Caroline Islands whose population speaks a Polynesian language — a Polynesian outlier within Micronesia. Figures of this kind were carved for specific families and deities and placed in the main *amalau* (cult house) of the atoll. Central to the annual **Mataariki** festival, which marked the start of the harvest year. Very few examples survive; most were destroyed or removed after Christian conversion in the late 19th century.
- **Function.** A **tino aitu** — a body for a deity or ancestral spirit. During rituals, the spirit "entered" the figure; decorated with cloth, loom bands, and feathers for festivals; received offerings of food and prayers for agricultural success, health, and protection.
- **Content.** Idealized but anonymous standing female figure. Spiritual identification came from ritual dressing and naming, not carved detail.
- **Form.** Subtractive wood carving with sleek, polished surface. Minimal facial features (brow ridge, hint of eyes, small mouth), elongated torso, swelling hips tapering to bent legs. Pronounced abstraction and simplification — a geometry of human form.

**Vocabulary:** tino aitu · subtractive sculpture · deity
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.4, 2.1
