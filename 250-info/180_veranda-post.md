## #180 — Veranda Post of Enthroned King and Senior Wife (*Opo Ogoga*)

**Olowe of Ise** (Yoruba) · 1910–1914 C.E. · Wood and pigment · Nigeria · 10 ft 8 in × 11 × 14 in base

### Summary

A carved **Yoruba veranda post** by the famous master **Olowe of Ise** — a statement of royal authority and female power.

- **Historical Context.** Carved by **Olowe of Ise (d. 1938)**, one of the most renowned Yoruba sculptors, as one of four posts for the **palace at Ikere**. The Yoruba live primarily in Nigeria and Benin; Olowe's identifiable style was itself a status symbol for the patron — only elite commissioners could afford his work.
- **Function.** Nominally holds up the roof of a veranda, but this particular post doesn't bear weight. Its real function is to project the political authority of the king and — pointedly — to assert that no king can rule without his senior wife. Olowe's signature presence also projects the patron's cultural sophistication.
- **Content.** A general Yoruba king seated on a throne. Behind him stands his **senior wife**, who is larger than the king and whose hands rest on his crown — she is **giving him the crown** and thus the legitimacy of his rule. In front, a smaller junior wife and the trickster god **Esu**; originally a (now lost) fan-bearer. The king's tall **crown** topped with a bird connects him to divine attention and protection.
- **Form.** Carved from a single wooden column; still holds traces of pigment. Columnar overall, with abundant negative space; strong diagonals in jaws, breasts, and arms. Hierarchical scale: the senior wife dwarfs the king, who in turn dwarfs the junior wife and Esu.

**Vocabulary:** Veranda · in the round · enthroned
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3
