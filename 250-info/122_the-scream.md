## #122 — The Scream

**Edvard Munch** · 1893 C.E. · Tempera and pastels on cardboard · Oslo · 66 × 83 cm

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Edvard_Munch%2C_1893%2C_The_Scream%2C_oil%2C_tempera_and_pastel_on_cardboard%2C_91_x_73_cm%2C_National_Gallery_of_Norway.jpg/330px-Edvard_Munch%2C_1893%2C_The_Scream%2C_oil%2C_tempera_and_pastel_on_cardboard%2C_91_x_73_cm%2C_National_Gallery_of_Norway.jpg" width="240" alt="The Scream">

### Summary

An iconic **Symbolist** image of existential dread that helped spark the **Expressionist** movement.

- **Historical Context.** The work is iconic of the Symbolist movement and is said to have sparked the Expressionist movement of the early 1900s. Munch made **four versions**, all included in his series **"The Frieze of Life."** A Norwegian, he studied at the Oslo Academy in the 1800s and was going through a very dark period when he painted *The Scream*: his sister had been committed to an asylum, and Munch felt he too was drifting into insanity. The setting is a hill in **Ekeberg, Norway**, near both a slaughterhouse and the madhouse his sister was in. Munch wrote in his diary that the work was more symbolic — on a walk at sunset he felt "a scream from nature" and a sudden sadness, describing the almost violent redness of the sky.
- **Function.** An abstract representation of Munch's central themes — **sickness, isolation, fear, and death** — and of his own mindset at a time when he felt he might be succumbing to madness. Munch had lost his mother to tuberculosis at age 5 and had almost no family, saying he was married to his paintings.
- **Content.** A genderless, simplified figure stands on a walkway beside a railing, overlooking a river and a hill. The figure is highly unrealistic, as is the background. The **extreme reds of the sky** over swirling water and sky contrast with the straight lines of the bridge.
- **Form.** Made in four versions — two paintings, two pastels. The first combined oil, tempera, and pastel; the second was pure tempera; the pastels were drawn on cardboard. Munch's decision to produce four versions reflects his dedication to experimentation, a spark of innovation characteristic of the Symbolist movement and later extended by Expressionism.

### Compare-and-contrast exercise (LO 3.5)

**Prompt:** Students analyze how contextual variables lead to different interpretations of a work of art.

> Most of the interpretations of this artwork are based off of what is known of Munch's personal life at the time of the painting's creation. Context is extremely important both at the micro and macro level of the painting—on the micro level, the artist Munch had been going through very dark times throughout his life and thus expressed these emotions on the canvas. At the macro level, in the early 1900s, the Symbolist movement was blossoming, in which artists did often express themselves, an era in which the artist's personal history did matter in contrast with earlier art in which most artists were unknown or simply created commissioned art.
