## #188 — Basin (Baptistère de Saint Louis)

**Muhammad ibn al-Zain** (Mamluk) · 1320–1340 C.E. · Brass with silver and gold inlay · West Asia · h. 22.2 cm, diam. 50.2 cm

### Summary

A masterpiece of **Mamluk metalwork** — a royal basin that became a French royal **baptismal font**.

- **Historical Context.** Made during the **Mamluk Period** (1250–1517), when the Mamluks — originally Turkic warrior-slaves — ruled Egypt and Syria and sponsored a flourishing of Islamic art in metalwork, glass, and textiles. Later captured by French crusaders or acquired in trade, the basin was reused in **France** as the baptismal font for children of the **royal house**, hence its name.
- **Function.** Originally a ceremonial basin — likely for **hand-washing before a royal meal** or related court ritual. Reappropriated later as the baptismal font in the Sainte-Chapelle de Vincennes.
- **Content.** Continuous horizontal **friezes** of Mamluk warriors, hunters, and courtly figures on horseback — the four horsemen encircle the outside. Interior shows water creatures (eels, fish). Unusually for a major Islamic work, no calligraphic inscription.
- **Form.** Cast brass with intricate **silver and gold inlay** forming the figures and decorative bands; cylindrical body flares slightly at the mouth. Horror-vacui surface packed with detail; symmetrical placement of the four horsemen.

**Vocabulary:** friezes · calligraphy (or lack thereof) · arabesque · Mamluk
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.2, 2.1
