## #10 — Tlatilco Female Figurine

**Tlatilco culture, Central Mexico** · c. 1200–900 B.C.E. · Ceramic · Princeton University Art Museum · 3.8 in tall

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b3/Tlatilco_female_figurine.jpg" width="240" alt="Tlatilco Female Figurine">

### Summary

A small ceramic "**Pretty Lady**" figurine from the Early Formative period, notable for its **two heads** (or two faces) and exaggerated hips.

- **Historical Context.** Made at **Tlatilco** in the Valley of Mexico during the **Early Formative period**, as settled village life was taking hold in Mesoamerica (c. 2000 B.C.E. onward). Dozens of related figurines have been excavated from **burials**, where they were the most common grave good. The culture was contemporary with — and influenced by — the early Olmec.
- **Function.** Unclear, but likely tied to **women's roles in fertility and the cosmos**; the **double-head / dual-face** variant may invoke ideas of duality (life/death, seen/unseen) central to later Mesoamerican thought, or record actual cases of conjoined twinning.
- **Content.** A standing nude female with **tiny breasts and waist but dramatically wide hips** and carefully rendered hair. The figurine is **two-headed** (two faces sharing a body), arms stiffly extended.
- **Form.** **Hand-modeled ceramic**, originally painted; colors now lost to weathering. Rounded, voluptuous volumes; fine incised marks in the hair; stylized, symmetrical facial features repeated across the figurine corpus.

**Vocabulary:** shamanism · Mesoamerica
