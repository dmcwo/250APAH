## #33 — Niobides Krater

**Classical Greek, Niobid Painter** · c. 460–450 B.C.E. · Clay, red-figure technique (white highlights) · Louvre, Paris · 21.3 × 22 in

### Summary

An Early Classical **red-figure calyx-krater** showing **Artemis and Apollo slaughtering the Niobids**, painted with a figural arrangement that breaks the ground line — pointing toward the look of lost **wall painting**.

- **Historical Context.** Painted in the early decades of the Classical period by the anonymous artist called the **Niobid Painter**. The krater was a vessel for mixing wine with water. In myth, Niobe boasted her fourteen children outshone the two (Apollo and Artemis) of the goddess **Leto**; Leto's children killed them in revenge.
- **Function.** A **krater** for mixing wine and water at a symposium.
- **Content.** **Side A** depicts the massacre of the Niobids by Artemis and Apollo. **Side B** shows **Herakles and Athena** surrounded by warriors — ambiguously, possibly pre-battle honoring of Herakles as a protective hero. Figures scatter at multiple heights with no single ground line.
- **Form.** **Red-figure technique** — figures reserved in the clay color, background painted black. **Contrapposto** poses borrowed from Polykleitan sculpture; stoic faces; thick ornamental borders survive from the Geometric tradition. Distributing figures across multiple levels implies landscape depth, an early move away from the frieze arrangement of earlier Greek vases.

**Vocabulary:** krater · red-figure painting
