## #43 — Augustus of Prima Porta

**Imperial Roman** · Early 1st century C.E. · Marble · Villa of Livia, near Prima Porta, Rome · 2.03 m tall

### Summary

A marble portrait of **Augustus** as perpetually youthful commander, blending Polykleitan **contrapposto** with an iconography-packed **cuirass** — imperial image-making as propaganda.

- **Historical Context.** Likely commissioned c. 20 B.C.E. to celebrate the diplomatic return of Roman standards from the Parthians — a signature event of the early Augustan **Pax Romana**. Found at the Villa of Augustus's wife **Livia**.
- **Function.** **Imperial propaganda.** The statue projects an idealized, youthful, and godlike Augustus, dressed as a military commander addressing his troops (*adlocutio* gesture).
- **Content.** Augustus stands barefoot (a divine attribute) in military regalia; the **breastplate** is dense with symbolism — the return of the Parthian standards, personifications of conquered peoples, Sol and Caelus, Apollo and Diana. A tiny **Cupid on a dolphin** at his ankle asserts his descent from **Venus** (the Julian family myth).
- **Form.** **Contrapposto** after Polykleitos's Doryphoros; idealized musculature; serene, youthful, generic face (Augustus is always depicted this way despite aging). High relief across the cuirass; originally brightly painted.

**Vocabulary:** contrapposto · symbolism · militaristic · directional
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.3, 2.1, 2.3
