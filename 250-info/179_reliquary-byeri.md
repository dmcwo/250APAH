## #179 — Reliquary figure (byeri)

**Fang peoples** (southern Cameroon) · c. 19th–20th century C.E. · Wood · ~2 ft tall

### Summary

A **Fang guardian figure** mounted atop a reliquary box holding ancestral bones.

- **Historical Context.** The **Fang** were historically nomadic, which reinforced a cultural emphasis on order and stability. Honorable deeds — killing an elephant, trading first with Europeans, bearing many children — marked one as a revered ancestor. Wood was sacred to Fang and to much of Africa; palm oil preserved the carving.
- **Function.** The byeri sat on top of a cylindrical **reliquary box** containing the skulls and bones of ancestors who had performed great deeds — clan leaders, warriors, craftsmen. Its role was to guard the relics from the **"forbidden gaze of women and uninitiated boys"** and from evil spirits, acting like a talisman. Elders consulted the figure at moments of important decision-making; young men were educated about lineage in front of it.
- **Content.** A highly stylized male figure (also made female): elongated torso, rounded head, closed calm eyes, protruding belly button (symbol of umbilical tie to ancestors), clasped hands, muscular limbs. Hairstyle of three crests reflects a high-status men's fashion.
- **Form.** Subtractive wood carving, abstracted into near-geometric forms. Small and portable to suit the migratory life of the Fang. Palm-oiled surface. Symmetrical, serene face; emphasis on internal spiritual balance rather than naturalism.

**Vocabulary:** reliquary · talisman · abstraction · byeri
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.1
