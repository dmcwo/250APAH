## #199 — Angkor Wat

**Khmer Empire (patron: Suryavarman II)** · c. 1113–1150 C.E. (main construction); site occupied c. 800–1400 C.E. · Stone masonry, sandstone · Angkor, Cambodia · ~500 acres; central tower ~200 ft tall

### Summary

The largest religious monument in the world — a Hindu temple-mountain that doubled as the **mausoleum of King Suryavarman II**, later converted to Buddhist use.

- **Historical Context.** Commissioned by **Suryavarman II** (r. 1113–c. 1150), who seized the Khmer throne by killing his great-uncle and sought legitimacy through unprecedented building. Unlike most Khmer temples (usually dedicated to Shiva), Angkor Wat honored **Vishnu**, with whom the king identified. Employed tens of thousands of workers and was never fully finished. Converted to a Theravada Buddhist site in the 14th century after the Khmer Empire's decline.
- **Function.** State temple housing the essence of Vishnu (and by identification, the king); astronomical observatory aligned to solstice and equinox sunrises; probable funerary monument for Suryavarman II, whose ashes may have been interred here.
- **Content.** Concentric galleries rise toward a central quincunx of five towers modeled on **Mount Meru**, cosmic home of the gods, surrounded by a vast moat (the cosmic ocean). Over 2,000 apsaras (celestial dancers) and long narrative bas-relief galleries illustrate Hindu epics — including the famous **Churning of the Sea of Milk**, where gods and demons pull the serpent Vasuki to extract **amrita** from the ocean.
- **Form.** Sandstone construction on a laterite core, oriented west (unusual for Khmer temples — supporting the funerary reading). The stepped pyramid form mirrors Mount Meru; the layout functions as a **mandala**. Corbelled vaults; no true arches.

**Vocabulary:** mandala · amrita · axis mundi · Mount Meru
