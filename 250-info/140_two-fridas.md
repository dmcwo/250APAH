## #140 — The Two Fridas

**Frida Kahlo** (Mexican modernism) · 1939 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Museo de Arte Moderno, Mexico City · 67-11/16 × 67-11/16 in.

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f9/The_Two_Fridas.jpg" width="240" alt="The Two Fridas">

### Summary

A double self-portrait made in the year Kahlo divorced **Diego Rivera**.

- **Historical Context.** Painted in 1939, the same year Kahlo divorced the Mexican muralist **Diego Rivera** (they remarried in 1940). Before marrying Rivera in 1929 Kahlo dressed in modern European clothing; Rivera encouraged her embrace of **Tehuana** indigenous dress, which became central to her identity. Kahlo painted roughly 200 works, most self-portraits; this double portrait marks an emotional counterweight to her 1931 *Frida and Diego Rivera* wedding double-portrait.
- **Function.** A personal expression of the divorce — resilience paired with grief. Kahlo noted it also drew on a childhood memory of an imaginary friend.
- **Content.** Two Fridas hold hands on a bench before a stormy sky. Left: "European" Frida in a white Victorian dress, her heart torn open, bleeding onto her lap where she holds surgical pincers that have cut the artery running between the two figures. Right: "Mexican" Frida in a traditional **Tehuana** dress, her heart intact, holding a small portrait of Rivera as a child. The blood trails across her white dress; she is at risk of bleeding out.
- **Form.** Oil on canvas; nearly symmetrical composition fills the frame; the connecting artery is the literal and formal link between the two halves. Agitated clouds externalize Kahlo's inner turmoil; blood is used as a metaphor for union.

**Vocabulary:** self-portrait · indigenous · Tehuana · Mexican modernism
