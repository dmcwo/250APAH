## #114 — Nadar Raising Photography to the Height of Art

**Honoré Daumier** · 1862 C.E. · Lithograph · Museum of Fine Arts, Boston · 27.2 × 22.2 cm

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Honor%C3%A9_Daumier%2C_Nadar_%C3%A9levant_la_Photographie_%C3%A0_la_hauteur_de_l%27Art%2C_1862%2C_NGA_42966.jpg/250px-Honor%C3%A9_Daumier%2C_Nadar_%C3%A9levant_la_Photographie_%C3%A0_la_hauteur_de_l%27Art%2C_1862%2C_NGA_42966.jpg" width="240" alt="Nadar Raising Photography to the Height of Art">

### Summary

A satirical lithograph mocking the claim that **photography could be considered fine art**.

- **Historical Context.** Daumier (b. 1808) was a French printmaker and caricaturist who made his career skewering political and social figures — he first worked for the comic journal *La Caricature* — in a 19th-century France defined by political instability and tension between rulers and public. The print targets **Nadar** (Gaspard-Félix Tournachon), the real photographer famous for taking the **first aerial photographs from a hot air balloon**.
- **Function.** Published as social critique: photography was still a new, contested medium, and Daumier uses humor to argue that it does not belong alongside traditional art.
- **Content.** Nadar in top hat and coat, nearly tumbling out of his balloon's basket as he works a camera; his hat flies off and hair goes wild. Every rectangular building below is labeled "Photographie," and the balloon is labeled "Photographie Nadar"; the buildings repeat and blur toward the horizon.
- **Form.** Small black-and-white lithograph with a soft, sketchy quality and slight shading. The figure and buildings are exaggerated and cartoonish rather than naturalistic — the distortion drives the joke.

**Vocabulary:** Photography · Nadar · camera obscura · daguerreotype · satire · lithograph
