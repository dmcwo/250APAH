## #68 — The Arnolfini Portrait

**Jan van Eyck** · 1434 C.E. · Oil on oak panel · National Gallery, London · 32.4 × 23.6 in

### Summary

A small oil-on-panel double portrait by **Jan van Eyck** — probably the Italian merchant **Giovanni di Nicolao Arnolfini** and his wife in their Bruges bedroom — dense with symbolism, hyper-detail, and debated narrative (marriage? document? memorial?).

- **Historical Context.** Made in 1434 by van Eyck, working in **Bruges** at the height of **Early Netherlandish / Northern Renaissance** painting. The north pioneered **oil painting**, which enabled unmatched detail, glazing, and luminosity. Unlike Italian Renaissance emphasis on perspective and anatomy, the Northern Renaissance excels at minute description and loaded symbolism.
- **Function.** Debated. A common theory: van Eyck's signature, "Johannes de eyck fuit hic 1434" ("Jan van Eyck was here, 1434"), above a convex mirror reflecting two figures (witnesses), casts him as witness to a **wedding or betrothal contract**. Alternative readings treat it as a memorial portrait or ostentation of wealth.
- **Content.** An affluent couple holds hands in a richly appointed bedroom: he in dark purple robe, she in green gown gathered over her abdomen (likely fashion, not pregnancy). A single lit **candle** (God's presence), a **dog** (fidelity), **shoes removed** (sacred ground), **oranges** (wealth/fertility), a **mirror** ringed with **Passion of Christ** scenes, a **Saint Margaret** figurine on the bedpost (patron of childbirth).
- **Form.** **Oil on oak panel** built up in glazes, allowing extraordinary **photographic detail** — hairs on the dog, beads of the chandelier, woven carpet patterns. Lighting enters from the left window, grounding the space; the **convex mirror** expands the scene into the viewer's space.

**Vocabulary:** tempera · symbolism · double portrait · chiaroscuro
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.2, 1.4, 3.2
