## #198 — Borobudur Temple

**Sailendra Dynasty, Mahayana Buddhist (architect: Gunadharma, attributed)** · c. 750–842 C.E. · Volcanic stone masonry · Central Java, Indonesia · ~26,910 ft² footprint; ~115 ft tall

### Summary

The world's largest Buddhist monument — a **stone mandala** mountain that pilgrims climb as a spiritual ascent toward enlightenment.

- **Historical Context.** Built under the **Sailendra** rulers of central Java during a period of intense cultural exchange between India and Southeast Asia, when **Mahayana Buddhism** reached Java alongside indigenous ancestral worship. Abandoned by the 14th century (possibly due to volcanic eruption or conversion to Islam) and buried under ash and jungle; rediscovered in 1814 under the British administration of Thomas Stamford Raffles.
- **Function.** A **pilgrimage monument** without interior space. Devotees climb and circumambulate the terraces clockwise, reading the relief panels as they ascend — a physical journey through the three realms of Buddhist cosmology toward nirvana.
- **Content.** Nine stacked platforms: six square terraces below, three circular terraces above, crowned by a central stupa. The three levels represent **Kamadhatu** (desire), **Rupadhatu** (form), and **Arupadhatu** (formlessness). Over **1,400 narrative reliefs** tell Jataka tales and sutras; **504 Buddha statues** sit within niches and in 72 perforated bell-shaped stupas on the upper circular terraces.
- **Form.** Stepped-pyramid mandala aligned to the cardinal directions; axis mundi rising toward the central stupa. Dense narrative relief on the lower walls transitions to serene abstraction above as the pilgrim approaches enlightenment.

**Vocabulary:** Mahayana Buddhism · axis mundi · circumambulate · stupa · mandala
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.1, 3.2
