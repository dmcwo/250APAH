## #228 — Androgyne III

**Magdalena Abakanowicz (Poland)** · 1985 C.E. · Burlap, resin, wood, nails, and string · Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York · 50 × 60 × 20 in

### Summary

A **hollowed, headless body** on wooden stretcher-poles — Abakanowicz's meditation on dehumanization under war and totalitarianism.

- **Historical Context.** **Magdalena Abakanowicz** (1930–2017) grew up in an aristocratic Polish family forced into hiding during World War II and later survived Soviet-imposed Socialist Realism in communist Poland. Her direct experience of war, communist repression, and the anonymous crowd drove a career-long meditation on the fragility of human identity. She is the namesake of the **Abakan** — her monumental, semi-abstract woven fiber sculptures.
- **Function.** No practical use; a **witness-object**. Abakanowicz often made such figures in large series (**Backs**, **Seated Figures**) so the accumulation of headless, limbless bodies would impress the viewer with "the countless" victims of 20th-century violence.
- **Content.** A seated, life-sized human torso — genderless (hence "**androgyne**"), hollow, missing head, arms, and legs, placed on a wooden stretcher whose poles substitute for the absent legs. The burlap surface reads as wrinkled skin or scarred flesh.
- **Form.** Built by stiffening **burlap sackcloth** with **resin** around a wooden frame. The back appears solid; the front reveals the figure as a shell. Highly textured — reminiscent of bones, veins, and muscle. Sculpture in the round.

**Vocabulary:** androgynous · social realism · Abakans
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3
