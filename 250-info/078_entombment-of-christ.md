## #78 — Entombment of Christ

**Jacopo da Pontormo** · 1525–1528 C.E. · Oil on wood · Capponi Chapel, Santa Felicita, Florence · 300 × 203 cm

### Summary

A foundational **Mannerist** altarpiece — anti-classical, weightless, and swirling with emotion.

- **Historical Context.** Painted in Florence as the High Renaissance gave way to Mannerism, amid the **Protestant Reformation**, the Sack of Rome (1527), Copernican cosmology, and the Medici's re-abolition of the Florentine republic. Pontormo was a student of Andrea del Sarto; his style was shaped by Michelangelo's late works.
- **Function.** Altarpiece for the **Capponi Chapel** of Santa Felicita in Florence. It sits beside Pontormo's own Annunciation frescoes, juxtaposing Christ's death with his incarnation.
- **Content.** Christ's pale, limp body is lifted by two youthful figures; the Virgin swoons backward; Mary Magdalene and other mourners press in. Notably, **no tomb and no cross appear** — only bodies and grief. Pontormo inserts a self-portrait at the far right.
- **Form.** Classic **Mannerist** features: elongated limbs, small heads, pastel/high-key color (acid pink, seafoam, lilac), no stable ground line, anticlassical **circular composition** centering on a knot of hands. Figures seem to float; palette chosen to cut through the chapel's dim interior.

**Vocabulary:** anticlassical composition · circular composition · Mannerism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 1.4, 2.1, 3.1, 3.2, 3.5

> The Annunciation Triptych focuses on the Annunciation, where it is announced to the Virgin Mary that she's going to give birth to Christ. Here, the emotion is joyful.
