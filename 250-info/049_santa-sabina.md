## #49 — Santa Sabina

**Late Antique Europe** · c. 422–432 C.E. · Brick and stone, wooden roof · Rome, Italy

### Summary

One of the earliest and most intact **Early Christian basilicas** in Rome — an elegant reuse of the Roman basilica plan for Christian worship, adorned with carved **cypress-wood doors** that include one of the oldest depictions of the Crucifixion.

- **Historical Context.** Built in the early 5th century C.E., after Christianity had become the Empire's official religion. Named after **Saint Sabina**, a Roman matron stoned to death for her conversion. Santa Sabina marks the early transition from catacomb-era Christianity to public monumental church architecture.
- **Function.** A **congregational basilica** for Christian worship, sited on the Aventine Hill. The plan — long nave with side aisles terminating in an apse — would remain the standard Christian church form for centuries.
- **Content.** A tall, sober nave lit by **clerestory windows**, lined by 24 reused Corinthian columns. The famous **wooden doors** carry carved biblical scenes, including a very early **Crucifixion**. Spandrel mosaics and the chi-rho over the apse add color above the white walls.
- **Form.** **Timber-roofed brick basilica** with stone columns. The unadorned white walls and open nave emphasize light and space; Roman engineering (clerestory, arcaded nave) is adapted to Christian liturgical axis.
