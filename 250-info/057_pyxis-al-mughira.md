## #57 — Pyxis of al-Mughira

**Umayyad (Islamic Iberia)** · c. 968 C.E. · Ivory · Musée du Louvre, Paris · ~6 in tall

### Summary

A small carved **ivory box** made as an eighteenth-birthday gift for the Umayyad prince **al-Mughira** — the finest surviving example of the ivory-carving tradition of Islamic Spain, dense with princely iconography.

- **Historical Context.** Produced in the royal ivory workshops at **Madinat al-Zahra** for **al-Mughira**, son of the caliph Abd al-Rahman III. Combines Islamic themes with an Iberian carved-ivory tradition and motifs borrowed from the broader Mediterranean princely repertoire.
- **Function.** A **pyxis** — a lidded container — probably for perfumes, cosmetics, or jewels. A precious, intimate object marking al-Mughira's coming of age and his place in the Umayyad court.
- **Content.** Four lobed medallions wrap the cylinder with princely scenes: a lion attacking a bull (a standard metaphor of power), hunters on horseback, falconers, and — most famously — **three enthroned figures** (perhaps al-Mughira flanked by musicians) being entertained with a lute and flywhisks. An **Arabic inscription** in Kufic script runs around the lid identifying the owner and the date.
- **Form.** Carved **elephant ivory**, once polychromed. Dense, all-over carving balances figural and vegetal motifs — peacocks, vine scrolls, hunting animals — in a hierarchical, symmetrical arrangement that asserts princely authority.
