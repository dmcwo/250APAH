## #221 — Navigation Chart

**Micronesian (Marshall Islands)** · Late 19th to early 20th century C.E. · Wood and fiber (coconut midrib, sennit, shells) · Marshall Islands · ~75.5 cm

### Summary

A Marshallese **stick chart** — a geometric map not of islands, but of the **ocean swells** that Micronesian navigators used to travel between atolls.

- **Historical Context.** Made by **Marshallese navigators** (*ri-meto*) as mnemonic and teaching aids for the centuries-old Micronesian open-ocean navigation tradition. Unique to the Marshall Islands; similar seaworthiness skills existed elsewhere in the Pacific but without these physical diagrams. Three chart types existed: *mattang* (abstract teaching diagrams), *meddo* (specific island groups), and *rebbelib* (larger sections of the archipelago). Charts were personal instruments — each navigator's chart was his own.
- **Function.** **Training and memory aid** for reading ocean swell patterns. A navigator memorized the chart on land, then sailed without it, lying in the canoe to feel how distinctive reflected, refracted, and crossing swells interacted with islands.
- **Content.** Curved sticks trace **swell patterns**; straight sticks mark dominant swell directions and wind currents; small **cowrie shells** mark the positions of **islets and atolls**. Emphasis is on forces and movement, not coastal geography.
- **Form.** Lashed coconut-leaf midribs and sennit fiber; loose, flexible construction. Flat, diagrammatic, non-pictorial — a form of information design.

**Vocabulary:** islet · swell · atoll · ri-meto · mattang · meddo · rebbelib
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 3.3
