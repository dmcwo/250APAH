## #169 — Wall plaque, from Oba's palace

**Edo peoples, Benin (Nigeria)** · 16th century C.E. · Cast brass · H. 19⅜ × W. 16½ × D. 4½ in.

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Benin_brass_plaque_03_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Benin_brass_plaque_03_%28cropped%29.jpg" width="240" alt="Benin wall plaque">

### Summary

A cast-brass plaque from the palace of the **Oba of Benin** — royal propaganda made from Portuguese trade brass.

- **Historical Context.** The **Edo peoples** of the Kingdom of Benin (modern Nigeria) had an established bronze-casting tradition by the 13th century. Hundreds of plaques decorated pillars in the palace of the **Oba** (king). Brass was sourced largely from **Portuguese trade** — melted-down European armbands and **manillas**. In 1897 a British expedition sacked Benin City, looted the palaces, and dispersed the plaques to European museums (the **Benin Bronzes**).
- **Function.** Architectural decoration arranged in visual sequences that recorded court rituals, royal genealogy, and imperial history — a kind of narrative relief.
- **Content.** The **Oba** (king) mounted on horseback, dominating the composition through **hierarchical scale**; attendants on either side raise shields to protect him from the sun and hold ceremonial sword and gift-box. **Coral beads** link the Oba to **Olokun**, god of the sea, who bestows wealth; **quatrefoil** "river-leaf" rosettes in the background also refer to Olokun.
- **Form.** **Lost-wax cast brass**; symmetrical central composition; hierarchical scale; dense background of quatrefoil flowers. Figures shown in composite view typical of Benin court style.

**Vocabulary:** Oba · Iwebo · Ibiwe · hierarchical scale · lost-wax casting
