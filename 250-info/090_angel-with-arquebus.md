## #90 — Angel with Arquebus, Asiel Timor Dei

**Master of Calamarca (La Paz School)** · c. 17th century C.E. · Oil on canvas · La Paz, Bolivia · 160 × 110 cm

### Summary

A colonial Andean **evangelization tool** dressing a Spanish-fashioned, gun-toting angel in the vocabulary of the Counter-Reformation.

- **Historical Context.** Produced in the **Viceroyalty of Peru** at a workshop in Calamarca (Bolivia) under José López de los Ríos. Created during a concerted **extirpation of idolatry** campaign to replace surviving Andean winged deities with Christian angels. Counter-Reformation theology cast angels as a militia of the Church Militant.
- **Function.** Hung in a church to impress indigenous Andean viewers with the **triple authority** of the Spanish crown — aristocratic (court dress), military (firearm), and spiritual (angelic). Native viewers could map the image onto their own sky/weather deities.
- **Content.** A young androgynous **arquebusier angel** — named Asiel Timor Dei ("Fear of God") in the scroll — stands in elegant Spanish court dress holding a loaded **arquebus** (early matchlock musket), rendered in a military drill pose from a European manual.
- **Form.** Flat, frontal Andean-style composition with careful attention to lace collar, sash, and shoes; wings cropped by frame; luxurious fabrics. Oil on canvas in a provincial Baroque idiom.

**Vocabulary:** history painting · devotional painting · arquebus · Viceroyalty of Peru
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3
