## #208 — Jahangir Preferring a Sufi Shaikh to Kings

**Bichitr (Mughal India)** · c. 1620 C.E. · Watercolor, gold, and ink on paper · Freer Gallery of Art, Washington, D.C. · 18 × 25.3 cm

### Summary

A **Mughal allegorical portrait** mixing Islamic, Hindu, and European iconography to proclaim Emperor Jahangir's spiritual superiority over worldly kings.

- **Historical Context.** Painted by the court artist **Bichitr** under **Jahangir** ("Seizer of the World"), fourth Mughal emperor (r. 1605–1627). Akbar, Jahangir's father, had consulted the Sufi saint Shaikh Salim Chishti for a son; Jahangir took his father's advisor's name. Jahangir was a major art patron, especially of portraiture and allegory. His court was deeply cosmopolitan, receiving European diplomats such as Sir Thomas Roe, ambassador of King James I.
- **Function.** **Imperial propaganda**: Jahangir publicly rejects earthly kings in favor of a Sufi holy man, projecting both piety and supreme authority.
- **Content.** Jahangir, enlarged and haloed by a double solar/lunar nimbus, sits on an hourglass throne and hands a book to the Sufi shaikh. Below him, descending in rank: an **Ottoman sultan**, **King James I of England** (copied from an English portrait), and the painter **Bichitr** himself holding his own self-portrait. Two putti hover with a broken bow and weep; a third, on the hourglass, writes a wish for a thousand-year reign.
- **Form.** Flat Persianate composition on a blue ground, with arabesque borders. European elements — putti, nimbus, modeled face of King James — are grafted onto the Mughal miniature idiom. Hierarchical scale and frontal portrait conventions.

**Vocabulary:** Jahangir · Putti · Sufi · Mughal Empire · Nur ud-Din
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 2.2, 3.3
