## #150 — Lipstick (Ascending) on Caterpillar Tracks

**Claes Oldenburg** (Pop Art) · 1969–1974 C.E. · Cor-Ten steel, steel, aluminum, and cast resin; painted polyurethane enamel · Morse College, Yale University, New Haven · 292 × 299 × 131 in.

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/79/Lipstick-catepillar.jpg/250px-Lipstick-catepillar.jpg" width="240" alt="Lipstick (Ascending) on Caterpillar Tracks">

### Summary

A **Pop art anti-war monument** that turns cosmetics into a tank.

- **Historical Context.** Commissioned by a group of Yale architecture students in 1969 to protest the **Vietnam War** — what they called "the second American Revolution." Oldenburg, born in Sweden and educated at Yale, was a central figure in **Pop Art**. He rejected art that "sits on its ass in a museum," instead placing his monument in Yale's Beinecke Plaza where it served as a protest **speaking platform**. It deteriorated, was rebuilt, and was reinstalled at **Morse College**.
- **Function.** Political protest. Juxtaposes the feminine, consumerist product of lipstick with the masculine, violent apparatus of a tank to mock the U.S. obsession with beauty and consumption amid the casualties of Vietnam.
- **Content.** A giant cylindrical tube of lipstick mounted on the treads of a tank — reads simultaneously as a missile firing and a lipstick rising. Originally the lipstick was an inflatable that could be raised or lowered.
- **Form.** **Cor-Ten steel** tank body painted black; cylindrical pink-and-gold metallic lipstick above. Pop Art scale and color; inexpensive industrial materials; deliberately durable public monument aesthetics.

**Vocabulary:** Pop art · monument · Cor-Ten steel
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 1.4
