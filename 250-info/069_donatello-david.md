## #69 — David

**Donatello** · c. 1440–1460 C.E. · Bronze · Museo Nazionale del Bargello, Florence · 5.5 ft

### Summary

**Donatello's** bronze **David** — the first free-standing **nude** since antiquity — a strikingly sensual, almost feminine youth who plants his foot on the severed head of Goliath.

- **Historical Context.** Made in the early Italian Renaissance by **Donatello** (Donato di Niccolò, 1386–1466), a Florentine master of both marble and bronze and the protégé of **Cosimo de' Medici**, who probably commissioned the work. The return of **heroic nudity** was a conscious revival of Greek and Roman traditions; medieval nudity had been confined to moral subjects (Adam and Eve; the damned).
- **Function.** Most likely commissioned by **Cosimo de' Medici** for the central courtyard of the Palazzo Medici. Read variously as: civic symbol (**Florence as David** triumphing over great powers), humanist celebration of antique nudity, Medici family allegory, or a homoerotic personal statement.
- **Content.** The biblical David after his victory over **Goliath** — his slingshot in one hand, Goliath's sword in the other, Goliath's severed helmet-crowned head beneath his feet. He wears only a **shepherd's hat** crowned with laurel (victory) and ornate **leather boots**. A feather from Goliath's helmet trails up David's inner thigh.
- **Form.** **Lost-wax cast bronze**, free-standing in the round (radical at the time). Soft **contrapposto**, polished skin set against rougher boots and curled locks. The **graceful, almost feminine** body contrasts with the brutality of the severed head — a studied ambiguity that contributes to the work's lasting fascination.

**Vocabulary:** bronze · shepherd · nudity · victorious · youthful · femininity · frailty · sensuality
