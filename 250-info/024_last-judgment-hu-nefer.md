## #24 — Last Judgment of Hu-Nefer, from his tomb (page from the Book of the Dead)

**New Kingdom Egypt, 19th Dynasty** · c. 1275 B.C.E. · Painted papyrus scroll · Thebes · 45.7 × 83.4 cm

### Summary

A brightly painted **Book of the Dead** scroll that walks the royal scribe **Hu-Nefer** through the **weighing of the heart** ritual — a commissioned guide to safe passage into the afterlife.

- **Historical Context.** Hu-Nefer was a **royal scribe**, overseer, and steward of **King Sety I**, likely buried at Memphis. The quality of the papyrus signals his wealth. Egyptian religious belief held that the **ka** faces judgment after death: the heart is weighed against the feather of **Ma'at** (truth); heavy hearts are devoured.
- **Function.** A personalized, **spell-filled guide** buried with Hu-Nefer to ensure he passes the judgment. It is part of the broader Book of the Dead tradition lining New Kingdom tombs.
- **Content.** In **continuous narrative**: the **Opening of the Mouth** ritual before the tomb; the weighing of the heart overseen by **Anubis**; the ibis-headed **Thoth** recording the verdict; the monstrous **Devourer (Ammit)** waiting to consume unworthy souls; Hu-Nefer then led before enthroned **Osiris**, flanked by **Isis** and **Nephthys**.
- **Form.** Rigid, brightly colored figures arranged in **registers** and shown in the canonical **composite view**. **Hieratic scale** enlarges gods over mortals; hieroglyphic text runs in vertical columns. A snapshot aesthetic — no continuous motion, but discrete static tableaux.

**Vocabulary:** hieratic scale · continuous narrative · papyrus · ka
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 2.1, 2.2
