## #97 — Spaniard and Indian Produce a Mestizo

**Attributed to Juan Rodríguez Juárez** · c. 1715 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Breamore House, Hampshire, UK · 31.8 × 41.5 in

### Summary

An early **casta painting** classifying the racial mixtures of colonial New Spain within a single idealized family portrait.

- **Historical Context.** Painted in early-18th-century **New Spain (Mexico)** amid intensifying Spanish anxiety about **mestizaje** — inter-ethnic mixing — and a desire to impose legible hierarchy on a fluid colonial society. *Casta* series typically ran 16 panels; earlier works idealize the first mixtures, later ones denigrate more complex ones.
- **Function.** Likely commissioned by viceregal patrons as a gift shipped to Spain — an ethnographic display meant to reassure European audiences that Spanish bloodlines, though mixing, remained ordered and noble. Also served Enlightenment-era classifying impulses.
- **Content.** An **elite Spanish husband** in a powdered wig and French court dress stands beside his **indigenous wife** in a traditional *huipil*; their child (a "**mestizo**") is carried by a young servant boy. All three family members interlock gazes in a circular composition.
- **Form.** European oil-on-canvas portraiture conventions — soft brushwork, realism — with symbolic costume choices coded for race and class. Red accents link the family members; the husband receives the brightest white highlights, emphasizing his "purity."

**Vocabulary:** Caste painting (pinturas de castas) · mestizo · huipil
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.1, 3.3
