## #193 — Terracotta warriors from the mausoleum of the first Qin emperor of China

**Qin Dynasty, China** · c. 221–206 B.C.E. · Painted terracotta · Lintong, Xi'an, Shaanxi province, China · Figures 5′8″–6′2″ tall

### Summary

A buried life-sized army of roughly **8,000 individualized figures**, made to guard the First Emperor in the afterlife.

- **Historical Context.** **Qin Shi Huangdi** unified China in 221 B.C.E. after conquering the Warring States, founding the Qin Dynasty, standardizing script, currency, and measures, and beginning the Great Wall. Obsessed with immortality, he mobilized more than 700,000 conscripted laborers to build his vast mausoleum complex near modern Xi'an; the pit army was discovered by farmers in 1974.
- **Function.** A **funerary guard** to protect the emperor's spirit in the afterlife, part of an enormous simulated underworld city that reportedly included rivers of mercury and a star-map ceiling.
- **Content.** Infantry, archers, cavalry, charioteers, and officers arrayed in battle formation facing east. Each figure's face, hair, and armor differ — likely portraits or composites of actual soldiers. Weapons (swords, crossbow triggers, halberds) were real bronze.
- **Form.** Modular construction: heads, arms, and torsos molded separately, then assembled and individualized with layered clay; fired in kilns and painted in bright mineral pigments (the famous "Chinese purple" among them), most of which has flaked off. Figures average 300–400 lbs.

**Vocabulary:** mausoleum · terracotta · Qin Shi Huangdi · Qin Dynasty · Daoism
