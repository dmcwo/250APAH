## #36 — Grave Stele of Hegeso

**Classical Greek, attributed to Kallimachos** · c. 410 B.C.E. · Pentelic marble and paint · Kerameikos (Dipylon cemetery), Athens · 1.56 × 0.97 m

### Summary

A refined Classical **grave relief** showing **Hegeso, daughter of Proxenos**, seated and examining a piece of jewelry held out by her maid — a Late Classical monument to Athenian female domestic life.

- **Historical Context.** Found in the Dipylon cemetery of Athens and attributed to **Kallimachos**. Classical Athenian women were legally defined by their fathers and husbands and spent most of their time in the household. The stele's inscription names Hegeso and Proxenos; the fine Pentelic marble and high-quality carving imply an aristocratic commission.
- **Function.** A **grave marker** commemorating Hegeso and asserting her family's wealth and status through both its material and its imagery.
- **Content.** Hegeso is seated on an elegant chair with her feet on a footstool. A slave girl (standing) holds out an open jewelry box from which Hegeso lifts an object — once painted, now lost — likely a necklace. An architectural frame with Ionic columns and a pediment supports the inscription.
- **Form.** Low **relief** in Pentelic marble. Naturalistic **drapery** reveals body contours; **hierarchy of scale** keeps the standing slave the same height as the seated Hegeso, so Hegeso's superior status is emphasized by her elevated footstool and central, framed placement rather than by size.

**Vocabulary:** domestic · idealization · grave stele
