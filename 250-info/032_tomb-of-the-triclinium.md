## #32 — Tomb of the Triclinium

**Etruscan** · c. 480–470 B.C.E. · Tufa and fresco · Monterozzi Necropolis, Tarquinia, Italy

### Summary

A small Etruscan **chamber tomb** at Tarquinia whose painted walls turn a burial chamber into a **celebratory dining room**, with frescoed banqueters, dancers, and musicians.

- **Historical Context.** The **Etruscans** (c. 700–300 B.C.E.) were a wealthy Iron Age culture on Italy's central-western coast, trading with Celts to the north and Greeks to the south. Because their written language is only partly understood, their **elaborately painted tombs** supply our best evidence of their culture. The Romans eventually absorbed them.
- **Function.** To ease the deceased's **transition to the afterlife** and to host a festive **funerary banquet** with family. The tomb also held grave goods and the remains of the deceased while commemorating their status.
- **Content.** A back wall fresco shows **three couches (triclinium)** with pairs of male and female diners attended by servants; cats, birds, and a rooster roam underfoot. The side walls show **male and female dancers and a musician** with a **barbiton** (lyre). Men are painted darker than women, per convention; the ceiling is checkered to mimic the tents of funerary celebrations.
- **Form.** A rock-cut chamber of modest size, painted in **buon fresco** with vibrant reds, blacks, and whites. The intimate scale and saturated color create an atmosphere of warmth rather than mourning.

**Vocabulary:** triclinium · fresco · chamber tomb
