## #16 — Standard of Ur

**Sumerian** · c. 2600–2400 B.C.E. · Wood inlaid with shell, lapis lazuli, and red limestone · Royal Tombs at Ur (modern Tell el-Muqayyar, Iraq) · 8.5 × 19.5 in

### Summary

A small Sumerian box inlaid in **shell, lapis lazuli, and red limestone**, depicting matched scenes of **"War" and "Peace"** — an early register-based narrative of Mesopotamian royal ideology.

- **Historical Context.** Recovered from the **Royal Tombs at Ur** by Leonard Woolley. Ur was a major Sumerian city-state dating back to the Ubaid period (c. 3800 B.C.E.), once on the Persian Gulf coast (now far inland).
- **Function.** **Unknown**. Woolley proposed it was carried on a pole as a royal standard; others think it was the **soundbox of a musical instrument**.
- **Content.** Two long sides: the **Peace** panel shows the king feasting at top while subjects bring livestock and goods in procession; the **War** panel shows chariots trampling enemies and prisoners paraded before the king.
- **Form.** **Three registers** per side, organized with **hierarchical scale** (the king is enlarged). Flat, profile figures with emphasized eyes and eyebrows stand on clear ground lines — a mosaic of shell, lapis, and limestone set in bitumen.

**Vocabulary:** inlay · mosaic · register · hierarchical scale
