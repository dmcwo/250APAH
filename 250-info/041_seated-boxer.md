## #41 — Seated Boxer

**Hellenistic Greek** · c. 100 B.C.E. · Bronze · Palazzo Massimo alle Terme, Rome · 4 ft 2½ in

### Summary

An unflinchingly **realist** Hellenistic bronze of a **battered older athlete** — cut lips, broken nose, cauliflower ears, blood-filled scars — designed to elicit **pathos** rather than idealized awe.

- **Historical Context.** One of two bronzes unearthed on the Quirinal Hill of Rome in 1885, carefully buried near the Baths of Constantine. It dates to the **Hellenistic** period (323–31 B.C.E.), when Greek sculptors turned to heightened emotion, wider social range, and unidealized realism.
- **Function.** A **commemorative image of a boxer** — possibly a champion, possibly a generic type. Unlike earlier athletic statues that glorified the ideal body, this one invites **empathy** for the wear of a life in the ring.
- **Content.** A mature, **bearded** boxer sits between bouts, caestus-wrapped hands on his knees. Facial **wounds**, a broken nose, cauliflower ears, and a bruised mouth bespeak rounds of violence. His head turns upward as if hearing someone call his name.
- **Form.** **Lost-wax cast bronze**, assembled from eight segments with nearly invisible joins. **Copper inlays** redden the lips, scars, and blood drops on shoulder, forearm, and thigh. The tense torso still reads as strong — Hellenistic realism does not abandon musculature — but the **pathos** is in the face.

**Vocabulary:** pathos · bronze · sculpture · lost-wax process
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.4
