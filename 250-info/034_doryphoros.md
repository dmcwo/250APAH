## #34 — Doryphoros (Spear Bearer)

**Polykleitos** · Original c. 450–440 B.C.E. · Roman copy in marble of Greek original in bronze · Museo Archeologico Nazionale, Naples (from Pompeii) · 6 ft 11 in

### Summary

**Polykleitos's Canon** embodied — a **contrapposto** spear-bearer whose mathematical proportions (1:7 head to body) defined **High Classical** Greek ideal beauty and shaped Western art for millennia.

- **Historical Context.** Polykleitos, a master sculptor of the **High Classical** period, wrote a treatise (the "Canon") setting out mathematical ratios for the ideal human body; the Doryphoros was his paradigmatic illustration of it. When Rome conquered Greek cities after 211 B.C.E., Roman patrons commissioned **marble copies** of famous Greek bronzes — including this one, via **lost-wax** casting techniques.
- **Function.** Greek original: likely an embodiment of the Canon displayed as an exemplar. The surviving Roman marble was found in a **palestra** in Pompeii — appropriate to inspire athletes. Other copies decorated private homes.
- **Content.** A nude young warrior, probably **Achilles**, originally holding a spear over his left shoulder. **Heroic nudity** and **undistinguished facial features** mark him as an ideal type, not an individual.
- **Form.** **Contrapposto / chiastic stance**: weight on the right leg, left leg relaxed; the opposing arm tenses while the other relaxes — creating a **cross-balance** of tension and repose through the body. Strict **symmetria**, idealized musculature, impassive face. The Roman marble copy adds a tree-stump support that the bronze original did not need.

**Vocabulary:** Canon of proportions · contrapposto / chiastic · symmetria · kallos · idealism · 1:7 ratio
