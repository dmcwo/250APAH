## #185 — The Dome of the Rock

**Umayyad Caliphate** (commissioned by Caliph **Abd al-Malik**) · 685–691 C.E. · Stone masonry and wooden roof with glazed ceramic tile and mosaics; gilt aluminum and bronze dome covered with gold leaf · Temple Mount, Jerusalem

### Summary

The earliest surviving **Islamic monument** and one of the most politically charged sacred buildings in the world.

- **Historical Context.** Built by the Umayyad caliph **Abd al-Malik** on the Temple Mount in Jerusalem (called the Haram al-Sharif by Muslims) — a site holy to Judaism (the First and Second Temples), Christianity, and Islam (the Night Journey of Muhammad). Likely designed by the architects Radjâ ibn Haywâ and Yazid ibn Sallâm. Erected at a contested moment: the Umayyad caliphate competed with a rival caliphate in Mecca, so the monument asserted Islam's presence and prestige in the Judeo-Christian holy city.
- **Function.** A **shrine**, not a mosque — its central octagonal space surrounds and marks the **Foundation Stone**, the rock from which Muhammad is said to have ascended on the **Isra and Mi'raj** (Night Journey). Circumambulated by pilgrims.
- **Content.** A central octagonal plan with a golden dome over the rock; outer and inner ambulatories for circumambulation. Interior mosaics in vegetal and abstract patterns; Arabic **Kufic inscriptions** from the Quran running around the drum (some polemical, asserting Islamic theology over Christian doctrine).
- **Form.** Octagonal plan with a tall central drum and hemispherical **gold-leafed dome**. Exterior tiled in blue and turquoise glazed ceramic added later under Suleiman. Both mosaics and calligraphy strictly aniconic — no human or animal figures.

**Vocabulary:** qubba · Haram al-Sharif · Isra and Mi'raj · aniconism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.2
