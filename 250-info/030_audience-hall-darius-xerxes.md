## #30 — Audience Hall (apadana) of Darius and Xerxes

**Persian (Achaemenid)** · c. 520–465 B.C.E. · Limestone · Persepolis, Iran · 72 columns, each ~25 m tall

### Summary

The immense **columned audience hall** of the Achaemenid kings at **Persepolis**, whose staircase reliefs depict 23 subject nations bringing **tribute** to the Persian king.

- **Historical Context.** The **Achaemenid Persian Empire**, at its height, reached from the Indus to Northern Greece and from Central Asia to Egypt. Begun by **Darius I** and completed by his son **Xerxes**, the apadana celebrates Persia's multi-ethnic rule and the empire's explicit policy of religious and cultural tolerance.
- **Function.** The hall in which the king received **tribute** from the empire's subject nations at the New Year festival. Reliefs on the north and east stairways showed each nation presenting its distinctive gifts, mirroring the delegations who saw themselves depicted as they ascended the stairs.
- **Content.** Two monumental stairways carved with procession reliefs: **23 delegations** in varied dress bring silver and gold vessels, weapons, textiles, jewelry, and animals. Stylized cypress trees separate groups; guards flank the king, who appears centrally.
- **Form.** A massive raised stone platform supporting **72 columns** with complex **bull- or lion-headed capitals** symbolizing royal authority; **verandas** open on three sides (unique among Persepolis palaces). The vanished roof was timber.

**Vocabulary:** apadana · hypostyle · column · relief · veranda · capital
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.4, 2.3
