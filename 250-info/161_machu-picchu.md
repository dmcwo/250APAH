## #161 — City of Machu Picchu

**Inka** · c. 1450–1540 C.E. · Granite (architectural complex) · Central highlands, Peru · ~80,000 acres

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Machu_Picchu%2C_2023_%28012%29.jpg/250px-Machu_Picchu%2C_2023_%28012%29.jpg" width="240" alt="Machu Picchu">

### Summary

An isolated **Inka royal estate** built 8,000 feet up in the Andes.

- **Historical Context.** Built by **Pachacuti Inca Yupanqui**, the Inka emperor whose 15th-century expansion created the largest pre-Columbian empire in the Americas. Located at the top of a steep Andean ridge; unknown to the Spanish conquistadors and so never looted. "Rediscovered" in 1911 by Hiram Bingham.
- **Function.** Primarily a **royal retreat** for Pachacuti and the Inka elite — for banquets, ceremonies, and religious observance. Too remote to serve as an administrative center. Includes an **observatory** for tracking the sun and the **Intihuatana Stone**, aligned so that it casts no shadow at the equinoxes.
- **Content.** Terraced agricultural fields cut into the slope; a dry moat for collecting rainwater; ashlar-masonry temples, houses, the observatory, and the Intihuatana; characteristic **trapezoidal doorways**. Separate zones for royalty (precisely cut stone) and servants (rougher masonry).
- **Form.** Granite **ashlar masonry** — stones shaped to fit together without mortar, quarried locally and moved without the wheel or draft animals. Structures follow the terrain; all the same pale granite gives unity to the complex.

**Vocabulary:** terraced fields · dry moat · ashlar · Intihuatana
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3
