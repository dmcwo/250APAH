## #79 — Allegory of Law and Grace

**Lucas Cranach the Elder** · c. 1530 C.E. · Woodcut and letterpress · Schlossmuseum, Gotha (original painting) · 32.4 × 46.5 in (original painting)

### Summary

A visual catechism of **Martin Luther's theology** — justification by faith, not works — disseminated through the printing press.

- **Historical Context.** Made in Germany during the height of the **Protestant Reformation**. Cranach was Luther's close friend and portraitist in **Wittenberg**. Reformers rejected the sale of indulgences and emphasized that salvation comes by **grace**, not good works. The printing press made the image cheap to reproduce.
- **Function.** Didactic Protestant propaganda illustrating the core Lutheran distinction between Law (condemnation) and Gospel (salvation).
- **Content.** A central **tree** splits the composition. **Left (Law):** a nude "Everyman" is chased by Death and the Devil toward Hell; Moses holds the Tablets; Old Testament scenes (Adam and Eve's Fall, the brazen serpent) loom. **Right (Grace/Gospel):** John the Baptist points the same figure toward the crucified Christ; behind, the risen Christ triumphs over Death. Lutheran Bible verses caption each half.
- **Form.** Woodcut reproducible at scale. Figures are naturalistic and in contrapposto; distinctive **Protestant bodily type** with small heads, round bellies. The dead tree (left) sprouts leaves (right) — a visual unity/contrast device. No deep perspectival space; didactic clarity prioritized.

**Vocabulary:** Protestant Reformation · good works · Gospel/Grace · printing press · woodblock printing
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3
