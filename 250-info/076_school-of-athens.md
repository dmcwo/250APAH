## #76 — School of Athens

**Raphael** · 1509–1511 C.E. · Fresco · Stanza della Segnatura, Vatican · 500 × 770 cm

### Summary

The paradigmatic **High Renaissance synthesis** of classical philosophy and Christian humanism.

- **Historical Context.** Raphael (b. Urbino, 1483) studied in Perugia under Perugino, then moved to Florence c. 1504 absorbing Leonardo's sfumato and Michelangelo's monumentality. In 1508, Pope **Julius II** summoned him to Rome to fresco the **Stanza della Segnatura**, the pope's private library. The room's four walls pair Philosophy (*School of Athens*), Theology (*Disputa*), Poetry (*Parnassus*), and Justice.
- **Function.** Decoration of the papal library / signing chamber of the Apostolic Signatura. The scheme harmonizes secular reason with Christian theology, aligning pagan philosophers with Christian truth.
- **Content.** **Plato** (with *Timaeus*, pointing up to ideal forms) and **Aristotle** (with *Ethics*, gesturing to the material world) stand at center. Pythagoras, Heraclitus (a portrait of Michelangelo), Euclid (portrait of Bramante), Ptolemy, Zoroaster, Diogenes, and a self-portrait of Raphael populate the hall; **Apollo** and **Minerva** flank in niches.
- **Form.** Rigorous **one-point linear perspective** with vanishing point between Plato and Aristotle, set inside a coffered barrel-vaulted basilica echoing Bramante's new St. Peter's. Figures distributed in balanced left/right groupings; bright, open illumination; Renaissance color palette.

**Vocabulary:** fresco · chiaroscuro · linear perspective · foreshortening
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.1, 3.2

### Connections to other works in the image set

#### → #75 — Sistine Chapel ceiling and altar wall frescoes (Michelangelo, 1508–1541)

**Theme:** time period; medium; same building; commissioned by same person

> The Sistine Chapel ceiling and altar wall frescoes were painted at almost the exact same time as the School of Athens. They are both frescoes, although the Sistine Chapel ones are on the ceiling while the School of Athens is on a wall. Both pieces were commissioned by Pope Julius II to decorate the Vatican. The artists, Michelangelo and Raphael, are seen as two artists who epitomized the ideals of the High Renaissance.

#### → #79 — Allegory of Law and Grace (Lucas Cranach the Elder, c. 1530)

**Theme:** conflicts between two different ideas; similar time period

> In Allegory of Law and Grace, Protestant and Catholic ideas towards God are contrasted; the left side features a scene of judgement and represents traditional ideas from the Old Testament while the right side shows God's mercy and represents the New Testament. The same contrast between ideas occurs in the School of Athens: thinkers aligning more with Plato's idealism are on the left while those aligning with Aristotle's realism are on the right. In addition, both art pieces were made during the Renaissance, in the early 1500s.

#### → #73 — Last Supper (Leonardo da Vinci, c. 1494–1498)

**Theme:** grouping; linear perspective; similar time period

> In the Last Supper, figures are painted in groups of three to represent the holy trinity. In the School of Athens, individuals also stand in groups, yet they are more fluid and less organized than in the Last Supper. Both art pieces use linear perspective in order to bring depth to the painting. In addition, both pieces were painted during the Renaissance, but the Last Supper was painted slightly earlier than the School of Athens.
