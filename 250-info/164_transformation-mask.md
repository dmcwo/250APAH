## #164 — Transformation mask

**Kwakwaka'wakw** (Northwest Coast of Canada) · Late 19th century C.E. · Red cedar wood, paint, and string · open 31 × 45 × 47 in.; closed 20.5 × 17 × 29.5 in.

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Kwakwaka%27wakw_transformation_mask.jpg/250px-Kwakwaka%27wakw_transformation_mask.jpg" width="240" alt="Transformation mask">

### Summary

A hinged ceremonial mask that **opens from animal to human face** during performance.

- **Historical Context.** The **Kwakwaka'wakw** live on the western coast of British Columbia. Their cosmologies include ancestors who transform between animal and human forms, shedding skins or masks to reveal human selves within. Clans are tied to sacred animals (raven, thunderbird, eagle, wolf, bear); sub-clans (**numayn**) hold specific privileges, including the right to wear certain transformation masks.
- **Function.** Worn over the head with a costume of shredded cedar bark for ritual performance during **Potlatch** and winter initiation ceremonies. Strings let the dancer open the outer animal face to reveal the human ancestor inside — the dancer literally performs the transformation of a supernatural being.
- **Content.** Here, an eagle (one of the four Kwakwaka'wakw clans) that opens to reveal the face of a human ancestor at its center. During the transformation moment the performer turns his back to the audience to heighten the mystery.
- **Form.** Red **cedar** wood, hinged mechanism, painted in the **formline** style of the Northwest Coast — bilateral symmetry, calligraphic wavy lines, ovoid eyes, U- and S-shaped elements. Vivid reds, blacks, greens, and oranges.

**Vocabulary:** transformation mask · Potlatch · formline · numayn
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.3, 1.4, 3.2, 3.5
