## #2 — Great Hall of the Bulls

**Paleolithic artists (multiple hands)** · c. 15,000–13,000 B.C.E. · Mineral pigment (ochre, charcoal, animal fat) on rock · Lascaux Caves, Montignac, France · largest bull ~17 ft

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1e/Lascaux_painting.jpg" width="240" alt="Great Hall of the Bulls">

### Summary

One of the most famous concentrations of **Upper Paleolithic cave painting**, with monumental animal figures built up by successive generations of artists.

- **Historical Context.** Painted deep inside the Lascaux cave complex over a long period; overlapping images show multiple hands and styles, suggesting the site was revisited across generations by hunter-gatherer cultures. The cave was closed to the public in 1963 and later replicated as **Lascaux II**.
- **Function.** Unknown, but the remote, hard-to-reach location and absence of daily-life traces point to **ritual or shamanic use** rather than domestic display. Likely tied to hunting magic or symbolic communication in a pre-literate society.
- **Content.** Large aurochs, horses, deer, and the enigmatic "unicorn" figure, arranged in two facing rows along the calcite chamber walls. The largest bull runs about 17 ft long — the biggest figure in known cave art.
- **Form.** Mineral pigments (ochre, charcoal, manganese) suspended in groundwater or animal fat and blown or daubed onto the wall. Figures combine **profile body / frontal horns** (twisted perspective). No true ground line, no background, and no linear perspective.

**Vocabulary:** shamanism · hunting-and-gathering · ritualistic · communication
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.3, 1.4, 2.2
