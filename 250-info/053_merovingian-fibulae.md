## #53 — Merovingian looped fibulae

**Early medieval Europe (Merovingian)** · Mid-sixth century C.E. · Silver gilt worked in filigree, with inlays of garnets and other stones · Musée d'Archéologie nationale, Saint-Germain-en-Laye

### Summary

A pair of richly inlaid **bow fibulae** (decorative safety pins) from a Merovingian grave — miniature masterpieces of migration-era **cloisonné** metalwork.

- **Historical Context.** Made in 6th-century **Merovingian Gaul**, a successor kingdom to the western Roman Empire. Fibulae of this type were first popularized by **Roman military campaigns** and were adapted by "barbarian" peoples who had been in long cultural contact with Rome. They survive largely because they were buried with their wealthy owners.
- **Function.** Functional **garment clasps** that also advertised status. Their presence in grave goods suggests personal identification and rank in the afterlife.
- **Content.** Symmetrical **eagle** motifs — a symbol adopted from the Roman empire for its associations with power and status — set with garnets and colored glass. Fine **filigree** and tight geometric patterning cover the surface.
- **Form.** **Silver gilt** with **cloisonné**-style inlay (enamel, glass, or gemstones separated by soldered metal strips). Dense, all-over ornamentation dominates over representational form — characteristic of migration-era art's preference for **animal style** and pattern.
