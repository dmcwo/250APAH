## #174 — Portrait Mask (Mblo) of Moya Yanso

**Owie Kimou (carver), Baule peoples** · Late 19th–early 20th century C.E. · Wood, fiber, pigment, and metal · Côte d'Ivoire, West Africa · 16½ × 6 × 5 in.

### Summary

A rare **named portrait mask** of a specific Baule woman, carved in the idealized style of the Mblo masquerade.

- **Historical Context.** The **Baule** live in central Côte d'Ivoire and have a rich tradition of sculpture for personal shrines and masquerade. **Mblo** and **Goli** are the two main entertainment-mask traditions; in the village of **Kami** where this mask was made, the Mblo dance is called **Gbagba**. Remarkably, both the subject (**Moya Yanso**, the commissioner's wife) and the carver (**Owie Kimou**) are known — unusual for African masquerade masks of this period. A period photograph shows Moya's son holding the mask beside her.
- **Function.** Commissioned by Moya Yanso's husband **Kouame Ziarey** to honor her, worn by him and his sons in masquerade performances with drummers, singers, and dancers. Not decorative wall art — used to **"make something happen"** in ritual. Kept hidden between performances.
- **Content.** An idealized female face with **scarification** scars rendered in brass at the temples and cheeks. Half-slit eyes and high forehead denote modesty and wisdom; cheek folds and the beard-like extensions to the ears suggest age; triangular brass patterns denote health. Six tubular projections on top represent the hairstyle.
- **Form.** Carved wood oiled to a near-black sheen, with **brass** accents highlighting scarification and hair. Monochrome with gold highlights; strongly symmetrical with repeated triangles and cylinders; an idealized version of the real woman rather than a naturalistic likeness.

**Vocabulary:** Mblo · Goli · Gbagba · scarification
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 2.1
