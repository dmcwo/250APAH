## #25 — Lamassu from the citadel of Sargon II, Dur Sharrukin

**Neo-Assyrian** · c. 720–705 B.C.E. · Alabaster (gypsum) · Dur Sharrukin (modern Khorsabad, Iraq) · ~10 ft tall (each)

### Summary

Colossal **human-headed, winged-bull guardians** flanking the palace gates of **Sargon II** — composite protective spirits carved with **five legs** to read correctly from both front and side.

- **Historical Context.** Commissioned for **Sargon II**'s new capital **Dur Sharrukin** (Khorsabad) during the Neo-Assyrian Empire's peak. The **Lamassu** derive from earlier Mesopotamian protective spirits (**lama**), demonized in scale to intimidate enemies.
- **Function.** **Apotropaic gate guardians** of the palace, asserting royal power and protecting the throne room. Cuneiform inscriptions on the body praise the king and **curse** anyone who criticizes him.
- **Content.** Hybrid body: **bull** (strength, royalty), **eagle wings** (heavenly reach), **bearded human head** (intelligence, wisdom), horned crown (divinity). Cuneiform text covers the lower body.
- **Form.** High relief in **gypsum alabaster**. **Five legs**: from the front the beast appears standing (two front legs), from the side it strides forward (four legs) — a clever registration of two views. The front is nearly in the round; the side is cut into the stone block.

**Vocabulary:** inscribe · lamassu · gypsum
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 1.4
