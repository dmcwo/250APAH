## #145 — Woman, I

**Willem de Kooning** (Abstract Expressionism) · 1950–1952 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Museum of Modern Art, New York · 192.7 × 147.3 cm

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/2a/Woman_I-Willem_de_Kooning.jpg" width="240" alt="Woman, I">

### Summary

De Kooning's signature **Abstract Expressionist** assault on the commercial female image.

- **Historical Context.** De Kooning was a **Dutch-American** painter and one of the rare abstract expressionists with a traditional academic training, close to **Jackson Pollock** and **Mark Rothko**. He worked on *Woman, I* for almost two years — layering and scraping away paint repeatedly — because he valued the process over the finished product.
- **Function.** A critical response to the flood of commercialized, sexualized imagery of women in postwar advertising and photography. The result is deliberately monstrous — what those images actually look like when their components are combined.
- **Content.** A large, confrontational female figure, part Madonna and part pin-up, reduced to exaggerated eyes, teeth, and breasts — the parts commercial imagery fixates on. Arms and legs dissolve in the surrounding chaos.
- **Form.** Thick layers of oil in quick slashing brushstrokes (despite the two-year gestation). **Garish** hot pinks, oranges, and yellows set against dull neutrals, with a silver band down the right side. No stable spatial depth.

**Vocabulary:** expressionism · Abstract Expressionism
