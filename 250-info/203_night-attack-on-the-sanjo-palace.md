## #203 — Night Attack on the Sanjo Palace

**Unknown Japanese artist, Kamakura period** · Second half of the 13th century C.E. · Handscroll; ink and color on paper · Museum of Fine Arts, Boston · 18¹⁄₁₆ × 304¹⁵⁄₁₆ in

### Summary

A vivid **Kamakura-period emaki** (handscroll) depicting the 1159 Heiji Rebellion, a foundational event in the samurai ascent to power.

- **Historical Context.** Painted roughly a century after the event, during the **Kamakura period** when military rule had replaced aristocratic government. The scroll depicts the 1159 **Heiji Disturbance** in Kyoto, where Fujiwara Nobuyori and Minamoto Yoshitomo abducted the retired Emperor Go-Shirakawa and burned the Sanjo Palace. An exemplar of the Yamato-e style and otoko-e ("men's pictures") warrior subject, it became a prized possession of nobles by the 15th century.
- **Function.** Narrative entertainment and commemoration — read by unrolling right to left, telling a moving story of chaos, violence, and the triumph of the conspirators.
- **Content.** From right to left: Nobuyori forcing the retired emperor into an ox-cart; panicked women and bystanders fleeing the burning palace; warriors hacking and shooting Taira loyalists; finally, at far left, order restored as the warriors surround the imperial cart.
- **Form.** Vivid red flames and black smoke dominate the central section; figures are outlined in black and overlap chaotically. The long horizontal format allows the viewer to experience the event as continuous time. Parts of the scene are seen from a high, oblique viewpoint (*fukinuki yatai*, "blown-off roof").

**Vocabulary:** otoko-e · Kamakura · emaki
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.1, 2.2
