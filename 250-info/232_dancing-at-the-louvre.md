## #232 — Dancing at the Louvre, from The French Collection (Part I, #1)

**Faith Ringgold (USA)** · 1991 C.E. · Acrylic on canvas, tie-dyed, pieced fabric border · New York · 73.5 × 80 in

### Summary

A **story-quilt** reimagining art history from a Black woman's perspective — a mother and children dance joyously in front of Leonardo's Mona Lisa.

- **Historical Context.** **Faith Ringgold** (1930–2024), Harlem-born painter and activist, began making "story quilts" in the 1980s after encountering Nepali cloth-bordered paintings at the Rijksmuseum. Quilting connected her to African-American women's traditions — especially the legacy of **enslaved quilters** — and to her own mother, fashion designer Willi Posey. *Dancing at the Louvre* is the first of twelve quilts in **The French Collection**, a narrative series following fictional Willia Marie Simone, a young Black American artist who moves to 1920s Paris.
- **Function.** A **counter-history** of modern art. By inserting Black women and children into the Louvre — joyously dancing before Leonardo da Vinci's *Mona Lisa*, *Virgin of the Rocks*, and *Virgin and Child with St. Anne* — Ringgold challenges the exclusion of African-American and women artists from the Western canon.
- **Content.** Willia Marie and her friend Marcia with three small girls hold hands and dance in a gallery of the Louvre. A narrative text, handwritten in the cloth borders, tells the story. Leonardo's paintings recede to the background.
- **Form.** **Mixed-media quilt**: a central acrylic painting on canvas, bordered by strips of tie-dyed and pieced fabric, backed with plain cotton and batted in traditional quilt fashion. The hand-written text was inscribed in black marker on the cloth border.

**Vocabulary:** quilt · story quilt
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.3, 2.1
