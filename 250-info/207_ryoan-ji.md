## #207 — Ryoan-ji

**Japanese (Muromachi period; patron: Hosokawa Katsumoto)** · c. 1480 C.E.; current garden design c. 18th c. · Rock garden, stone; wood architecture · Kyoto, Japan · Rock garden ~2,600 sq ft

### Summary

The most famous **Zen dry-landscape garden** in Japan — fifteen stones set in raked gravel as a meditation on emptiness.

- **Historical Context.** Part of a large Rinzai **Zen Buddhist** temple complex in Kyoto, built by **Hosokawa Katsumoto**, a shogunal deputy, in 1450 and rebuilt after fire. Zen traveled from China to Japan in the 12th century and fused with native Shinto reverence for nature and Chinese monochrome ink-painting aesthetics. The dry-landscape (*karesansui*) garden's designer is unknown.
- **Function.** A **visual aid for meditation**. Practitioners sit on the veranda of the abbot's quarters (*hojo*) and contemplate the stones in silence — the garden encourages introspection and the realization of Zen's core concept of emptiness.
- **Content.** Fifteen carefully placed stones in five groups, partially wrapped in moss, set in a bed of raked white gravel. An earthen wall encloses the space. The stones have been interpreted as mountain peaks in mist, tigers crossing a river, or islands in a sea — their meaning is deliberately open. From any vantage point, only **14 of the 15 stones** are visible, implying enlightenment is still to come.
- **Form.** Austere minimalism: three materials (stone, gravel, moss), one color family, strict rectangular enclosure. The raked gravel is maintained daily. Low oil-boiled earth wall ages with patina.

**Vocabulary:** Zen Buddhism · wabi / sabi · hojo · karesansui
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3
