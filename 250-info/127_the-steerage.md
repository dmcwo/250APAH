## #127 — The Steerage

**Alfred Stieglitz** · 1907 C.E. · Photogravure · J. Paul Getty Museum (one of five known versions) · 13 1/8 × 10 3/8 in.

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Alfred_Stieglitz_-_The_Steerage_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-Alfred_Stieglitz_-_The_Steerage_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" width="240" alt="The Steerage">

### Summary

Stieglitz's landmark photograph arguing that **photography belongs on the wall with fine art**.

- **Historical Context.** Taken on a 1907 transatlantic voyage during the peak of European immigration to the U.S., before the **Emergency Quota Act of 1921** cut that flow. Stieglitz, the son of a German immigrant, moved in avant-garde circles and published the work in his magazine *291* alongside Cubist drawings by Picasso — pitching photography into the wider **modernist** turn toward abstract form.
- **Function.** Primarily to elevate photography to the status of fine art; secondarily a quiet commentary on **class stratification**. Picasso reportedly admired its "collagelike dispersal of forms and shifting depths."
- **Content.** Two tiers on a ship bound New York → Europe: well-dressed first-class passengers above, shabbier steerage passengers (returnees from Ellis Island) below, a white **drawbridge** slashing a dividing line between them. A man in a round straw hat looks down from above.
- **Form.** Silver photogravure print built from geometric shapes — the round hat, the triangular mast, the chained drawbridge, the crossed suspenders on a man's back. Stieglitz said he saw "shapes related to each other… a picture of shapes and underlying that the feeling I had about life."

**Vocabulary:** photogravure · intaglio · modernism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.2
