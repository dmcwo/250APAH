## #139 — Fallingwater

**Frank Lloyd Wright** (Prairie Style / American modernism) · 1936–1939 C.E. · Reinforced concrete, sandstone, steel, and glass · Bear Run, Pennsylvania · 5,330 sq ft

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Fallingwater3.jpg/330px-Fallingwater3.jpg" width="240" alt="Fallingwater">

### Summary

Wright's career-defining **cantilevered house** built directly over a waterfall.

- **Historical Context.** Designed mid-Depression when architects like Wright were struggling; commissioned by Pittsburgh department-store owner Edgar J. Kaufmann after his son discovered Wright's work. The house extends Wright's earlier **Prairie School** (1900–1917) into the 1930s, embracing harmony with site, overhanging eaves, horizontality, and organic materials. Became a National Historic Landmark; given to the Western Pennsylvania Conservancy in 1963 and opened to the public.
- **Function.** A family weekend retreat for the Kaufmanns over Bear Run. Now a public museum and tourable landmark.
- **Content.** A three-level residence cantilevered directly over the stream and its waterfall. Ochre-colored concrete terraces extend outward; interior rooms open to the landscape; native **sandstone** walls anchor the vertical core.
- **Form.** Three-dimensional architecture built from **reinforced-concrete cantilevers**, sandstone walls, steel, and glass. Strong horizontal lines intersect the waterfall so that the house seems to generate the water itself. Ochre concrete matches local sandstone, binding the structure to its site; smooth concrete contrasts with rough stone.

**Vocabulary:** cantilever · terrace · mullion · Prairie Style
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.2, 1.4, 2.1
