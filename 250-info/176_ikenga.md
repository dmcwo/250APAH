## #176 — Ikenga (shrine figure)

**Igbo peoples** (Nigeria) · c. 19th–20th century C.E. · Wood · sizes vary (6 in to 3 ft)

### Summary

A personal **shrine figure** embodying an Igbo man's personal god of achievement.

- **Historical Context.** Made by the **Igbo** of Nigeria. Each man received an Ikenga upon reaching adulthood; the figure was consecrated by a kinsman before use and could be joined by additional ones as the owner gained rank. Ikenga translates as "strong right arm."
- **Function.** Serves as the owner's **personal god (chi)** of success and status. Honors the right hand, which wields tools and weapons. Each figure encodes its owner's morality, prosperity, genealogy, and social rank. Kept in the household until the owner's death, then usually destroyed; rarely reused.
- **Content.** A hand-carved human figure with prominent **horns** (signaling power) and the right hand holding a tool or weapon. Warriors look militaristic; leaders look tall and imposing; the details personalize the figure to its owner.
- **Form.** **Subtractive wood carving**, usually symmetrical with two legs, a cylindrical torso, and upright horns. Varies from naturalistic to heavily stylized depending on the owner's story.

**Vocabulary:** Ikenga · Chi · ritual · kinsmen
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 3.5
