## #70 — Palazzo Rucellai

**Leon Battista Alberti (architect)** · c. 1450 C.E. · Stone, masonry · Florence, Italy

### Summary

The **Palazzo Rucellai** by **Leon Battista Alberti** — the first Florentine palace façade to import the **stacked classical orders** of the Roman **Colosseum** to private domestic architecture, translating imperial Rome into early-Renaissance bourgeois grandeur.

- **Historical Context.** Commissioned by the wealthy Florentine merchant **Giovanni Rucellai**, rival-allied to the Medici, as a demonstration of family prestige. Alberti had visited Rome in 1430 and was inspired by ancient Roman buildings, particularly the **Colosseum**; he wrote the Renaissance's foundational architectural treatise, *De re aedificatoria*.
- **Function.** An urban **private palazzo** for the Rucellai family. Like other Florentine palaces, it served as residence, business headquarters, and public face of the family.
- **Content.** A three-story façade organized into three tiers by **horizontal entablatures**. Windows are round-arched with bifora (twin) lights; family emblems (the Rucellai sails) punctuate the frieze; the bottom floor features large **rusticated stone** and square windows to express strength at street level.
- **Form.** **Stone masonry** with finely cut blocks. Each story carries a different superimposed **classical order**: **Doric/Tuscan** below, **Ionic** in the middle, **Corinthian** above — a direct quotation of the Colosseum's order stacking, applied to domestic architecture for the first time.
