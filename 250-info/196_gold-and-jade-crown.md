## #196 — Gold and Jade Crown

**Silla Kingdom (Korea)** · Second half of 5th–6th century C.E. · Gold with jade (gogok) · Excavated Hwangnam Daechong, Gyeongju; National Museum of Korea · 27.3 cm tall

### Summary

A tree-and-antler gold crown from a **Silla royal tomb**, uniting shamanic cosmology with the metallurgical traditions of the Eurasian steppes.

- **Historical Context.** Made during Korea's **Three Kingdoms Period** (57 B.C.E.–668 C.E.), when the small southeastern Silla kingdom was expanding its diplomatic reach; Silla eventually unified the peninsula in 668 with Tang Chinese assistance. Silla's capital Seorabeol (modern Gyeongju) was also known as **Geumseong**, "city of gold." Unlike Baekje and Goguryeo, which looked primarily to China, Silla absorbed **nomadic Eurasian steppe** influence visible in its goldwork. Excavated from Hwangnam Daechong, the "Great Tomb of Hwangnam," a double royal burial.
- **Function.** Funerary regalia marking royal status in the afterlife; increasingly believed to have also served in **shamanic rituals** during life, before Buddhism became the state religion in the 6th century.
- **Content.** Three upright tree-branch uprights (axis mundi / sacred tree from Gyeongju legend), two side **antler** uprights (steppe reindeer imagery), hung with flat gold spangles and comma-shaped jade **gogok** (fertility "fruits"). Six long pendants dangle from the band.
- **Form.** Sheet gold cut and bent into upright elements pierced, hammered, and riveted together; gogok of jade threaded with gold wire. Strongly vertical and symmetrical.

**Vocabulary:** Silla Kingdom · Three Kingdoms Period · shamanism · axis mundi · gogok · daegwan
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 2.2, 3.3
