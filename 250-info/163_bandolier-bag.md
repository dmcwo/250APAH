## #163 — Bandolier bag

**Lenape (Delaware tribe, Eastern Woodlands)** · c. 1850 C.E. · Beadwork on leather · 68 × 47 cm

### Summary

An elaborately **beaded ceremonial pouch** that took the form of a European soldier's cartridge bag.

- **Historical Context.** Made by the **Lenape** (Delaware) people of the Eastern Woodlands, a confederation of three clans who traced descent through the female line. The shoulder-pouch form originated as the European soldier's **bandolier** for rifle cartridges; after contact, Native tribes adapted both form and material. Glass beads and silk ribbons reached tribal hands through the **fur trade**.
- **Function.** Ceremonial rather than utilitarian. Worn as part of men's regalia at dances and ritual gatherings. Only elite or elder men wore them; a display of prestige.
- **Content.** Symmetrical, abstracted beadwork designs. The contrasting colors (often blue and red against white) may correspond to the **four cardinal directions** and the sky-and-underworld cosmology. Plains-region bandolier bags tend toward floral patterns; Eastern Woodlands ones lean more geometric.
- **Form.** Glass beads stitched to leather or trade cloth, with silk ribbons and fringed tassels hanging from the bottom edges. Small beads create tight geometric patterns; ribbons and fringe produce motion as the wearer moves.

**Vocabulary:** beadwork · embroidery · bandolier · fringe · indigenous adaptation
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 2.2
