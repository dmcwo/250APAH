## #243 — Darkytown Rebellion

**Kara Walker (USA)** · 2001 C.E. · Cut paper silhouettes and projection on walls · Collection Musée d'Art Moderne Grand-Duc Jean, Luxembourg · 14 × 37 ft

### Summary

A room-sized tableau of **black paper silhouettes** of antebellum Southern violence, bathed in colored light — Walker's confrontational excavation of American racial memory.

- **Historical Context.** **Kara Walker** (b. 1969 in Stockton, CA) moved to Georgia at age 13 and experienced Southern racism directly. She is known for mural-scale cut-paper silhouettes — a genre associated with genteel 19th-century women's craft — used to depict brutal, grotesque scenes of slavery, rape, lynching, and rebellion. Her work has drawn sharp controversy from critics who argue she reinscribes racial stereotypes and from supporters who value her confrontation of suppressed histories. *Darkytown* extends the silhouette tradition by adding **colored overhead projectors** whose beams are interrupted by visitors' bodies, implicating the viewer in the scene.
- **Function.** An **immersive history lesson** forcing viewers to complete and interpret violent and sexually charged scenes that the silhouette reduces to stereotype; the colored projections literally cast the viewer's shadow into the tableau.
- **Content.** Figures of men, women, and children in 19th-century dress: a man with a severed leg, a woman giving birth, others brandishing weapons or falling wounded. References to racist 19th-century minstrel-show tropes (the title alludes to an 1899 "coon song"). Walker never names specific events; her scenes are composites.
- **Form.** Black cut-paper silhouettes adhered with wax to white gallery walls. Colored gel projectors wash the wall in lurid reds, greens, and blues; the viewer's shadow joins the scene.

**Vocabulary:** racism · stereotypes · silhouettes · shadows · narrative · controversy · concealment · historical representation
