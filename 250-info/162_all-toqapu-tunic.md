## #162 — All-T'oqapu tunic

**Inka** · 1450–1540 C.E. · Camelid fiber and cotton · Museo Chileno de Arte Precolombino, Santiago, Chile · 77.2 × 90.2 cm

### Summary

A luxury Inka **tunic** densely covered in **t'oqapu** geometric blocks — wearable iconography of the empire.

- **Historical Context.** Produced in the Inka empire at its height, made by the **acllas** (chosen women) who specialized in textile weaving and ritual duties. Inka textiles were more valuable than gold; fine cloth (**qompi**) was reserved for royalty and religious offering. A tunic covered entirely in t'oqapu — likely worn only by the emperor himself.
- **Function.** A status symbol and possibly a ritual offering. Cloth could be burned as tribute to the sun god **Inti**. Once cut from the loom, the textile was believed to lose its spiritual essence (**camac**).
- **Content.** Over 100 squares filled with distinct **t'oqapu** geometric patterns, each symbol representing a family, ethnic group, clan, or unit of the empire. The denser the t'oqapu, the higher the status of the wearer.
- **Form.** Fine rectangular **qompi** cloth with neck and arm openings. Warm reds, ochres, blacks, and yellows — the red cochineal dye came from insects and was expensive. Weave is extraordinarily fine and the ink embedded throughout the fiber.

**Vocabulary:** t'oqapu · qompi · acllas · iconography
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.2
