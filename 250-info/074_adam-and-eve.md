## #74 — Adam and Eve

**Albrecht Dürer** · 1504 C.E. · Engraving · Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York · 9.75 × 7.56 in

### Summary

Dürer's **printed manifesto** on ideal human proportion, fusing Northern engraving with Italian classicism.

- **Historical Context.** Produced in Nuremberg by the leading Northern Renaissance printmaker. Dürer was obsessed with **classical canon** and bodily proportion, and had traveled to Italy to study Greco-Roman sculpture. The work predates the Fall of Man; the balance of the Four Humors (melancholic/phlegmatic/sanguine/choleric) is still intact.
- **Function.** An engraving meant for wide distribution, doubling as a theological meditation and as a display of Dürer's learning. It was the only work on which he inscribed his **full name and date** on a placard.
- **Content.** Adam holds a branch of the Tree of Life; Eve holds a branch of the Tree of Knowledge, with the **snake** offering an apple. Four animals — **elk (melancholic), ox (phlegmatic), rabbit (sanguine), cat (choleric)** — embody the humors; the cat-and-mouse pairing foreshadows the predatory tension introduced by the Fall. A parrot may represent Ave/Eve wordplay.
- **Form.** Deeply incised copper-plate lines yield rich tonal range and crisp detail. Both figures stand in **contrapposto** based on classical sculpture (Apollo Belvedere / Medici Venus); Dürer prioritizes geometric ideal over Northern naturalism.

**Vocabulary:** melancholic · phlegmatic · sanguine · choleric · engraving · contrapposto · Four Humors
