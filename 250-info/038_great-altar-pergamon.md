## #38 — Great Altar of Zeus and Athena at Pergamon

**Hellenistic Greek** · c. 175 B.C.E. · Marble (architecture and sculpture) · Asia Minor (present-day Turkey) · ~115 × 110 ft

### Summary

An enormous **Hellenistic altar** whose wrap-around **Gigantomachy frieze** — the Olympian gods battling the Giants — turns the high-baroque drama and pathos of late Greek sculpture into a metaphor for the **Attalid** dynasty's victory.

- **Historical Context.** Built under **Eumenes II** of the Attalid dynasty in **Pergamon**, a former Alexandrian successor kingdom that became a major center of Hellenistic art. The altar commemorates the Attalid victory over Gallic (Galatian) invaders and Seleucid rivals.
- **Function.** An **altar** (not a temple), approached up a grand flight of stairs. A tribute to Zeus and Athena and a triumphal statement of Attalid political supremacy.
- **Content.** The continuous frieze depicts the **Gigantomachy** — the Olympians defeating the earth-born **Giants**. In the frieze shown in the image set, **Athena** drags **Alcyoneus** (a Giant) from the earth while **Gaea** (earth mother) looks up in anguish; a winged **Nike** crowns Athena.
- **Form.** Ionic colonnade enclosing a square altar platform on a high podium. The nearly sculpted-in-the-round **high relief** frieze shows **straining muscles, twisting bodies, wind-whipped drapery, and vivid pathos** — the full Hellenistic baroque idiom.

**Vocabulary:** frieze · colonnade · peristyle · Ionic columns
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.1, 3.2
