## #202 — Shiva as Lord of Dance (Nataraja)

**Chola Dynasty, India (Tamil Nadu)** · c. 11th century C.E. · Cast bronze · Tamil Nadu, India · 68.3 × 56.5 cm

### Summary

The canonical Chola-period bronze **Nataraja**, the dancing Shiva whirling the universe through cycles of creation and destruction.

- **Historical Context.** Cast during the height of the **Chola Dynasty** (c. 850–1250 C.E.) in southern India, whose rulers were fervent Shaivites and used bronze icons as instruments of state religion. Tamil religious poetry of the *Nayanars* saints shaped the iconography. Made by the lost-wax process, these sculptures were produced in royal workshops for temple festivals.
- **Function.** A **processional icon** carried from the inner shrine through city streets during festivals, dressed in real garments and bathed in offerings. Represents the cosmic dance of *Ananda Tandava* that destroys the universe and regenerates it.
- **Content.** Shiva dances inside a ring of flames (*prabha-mandala*) standing on the dwarf demon Apasmara (ignorance). His four arms hold the drum of creation (*damaru*), the flame of destruction, and strike protective and teaching mudras. His matted hair whips outward, bearing the goddess Ganga and a crescent moon.
- **Form.** Cast in solid bronze via lost-wax method. Idealized, elongated, lithe body frozen mid-movement; every attribute and gesture is codified iconography. Intended to be viewed in the round.

**Vocabulary:** Shiva · Nataraja · mudra · naturalistic · individualistic
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.1, 3.2
