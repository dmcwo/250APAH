## #244 — The Swing (after Fragonard)

**Yinka Shonibare (British-Nigerian)** · 2001 C.E. · Life-size fiberglass mannequin, Dutch wax printed cotton textile, swing, artificial foliage · Tate, London · 10.8′ × 11.5′ × 7.2′

### Summary

A life-sized, **headless remake of Fragonard's Rococo painting** in African-print fabric — Yinka Shonibare's postcolonial confrontation with European aristocracy and the entangled histories of trade and identity.

- **Historical Context.** **Yinka Shonibare** (b. 1962 in London, raised in Lagos, returned to London) self-identifies as a "**post-colonial hybrid**." He is best known for using **Dutch wax printed cotton** — a fabric whose patterns originated in Indonesian batik, were appropriated by Dutch manufacturers, produced in European factories, and marketed to West Africa, where they became synonymous with African identity — as a metaphor for layered, imposed cultural authenticity. This work directly remakes **Jean-Honoré Fragonard's 1767 painting** *The Swing* (image-set #101). Fragonard's painting depicts an aristocrat peeking beneath the dress of a swinging lady; Shonibare's revision removes both her head (evoking the **Reign of Terror** a generation later) and the voyeur (making the viewer complicit).
- **Function.** **Postcolonial institutional critique**. Displayed in London museums, it forces European audiences to see their cultural heritage through the lens of colonial exploitation.
- **Content.** A life-sized mannequin in Fragonard's pose mid-swing — shoe flying off, petticoats fluttering — but headless and dressed in vivid Dutch wax prints instead of pale silk. Hung from a real swing amid artificial dark-green foliage.
- **Form.** 3-D reconstruction of a 2-D painting; the viewer circles the mannequin, physically stepping into the role once occupied by the hidden lover. Bright pattern pops against dark foliage and blank white gallery wall.

**Vocabulary:** Postmodernism · Rococo · installation art · Dutch wax · hybridity
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 2.1
