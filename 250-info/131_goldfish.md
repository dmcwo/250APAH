## #131 — Goldfish

**Henri Matisse** · 1912 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Pushkin State Museum, Moscow · 140 × 98 cm

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Goldfish_Matisse.jpg/250px-Goldfish_Matisse.jpg" width="240" alt="Goldfish">

### Summary

One of Matisse's signature **Fauvist** compositions, painted just after his trips to **Morocco**.

- **Historical Context.** Matisse (French, 1869–1954) painted this in 1912 outside Paris, newly returned from **Morocco**, where he had been struck by the relaxed, contemplative daily life he saw around goldfish bowls. The work sits firmly inside **Fauvism** ("wild beasts"), the early-20th-century movement Matisse helped lead, which prized bold, unnaturalistic color and loose brushwork over realistic description.
- **Function.** To evoke contemplation. Goldfish became, for Matisse, a symbol of the tranquil, meditative state he had observed in Morocco — a subject for quiet looking rather than narrative.
- **Content.** A cylindrical glass bowl of goldfish sits on a small garden table, surrounded by lush pink flowers and green foliage. The fish's reflections in the water glass are nearly abstract, only loosely echoing the bodies above.
- **Form.** Oil on canvas; saturated pink, green, red, and orange; flat patterned backgrounds that compress depth. The fish are the only sharply rendered subject — everything else is simplified into broad color planes typical of Fauvism.

**Vocabulary:** Fauvism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 2.3
