## #220 — Tamati Waka Nene

**Gottfried Lindauer (Bohemian-born, working in New Zealand)** · 1890 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Auckland Art Gallery, Auckland, New Zealand · 101.1 × 82.4 cm

### Summary

A **posthumous European-style portrait** of the Ngāpuhi chief Tamati Waka Nene that fuses Māori identity markers with Victorian oil painting.

- **Historical Context.** **Gottfried Lindauer** (1839–1926), a Czech-born painter patronized by dealer Henry Partridge, produced scores of Māori portraits based on photographs and earlier likenesses. **Tamati Waka Nene** (c. 1780s–1871) was a Ngāpuhi rangatira who fought as a British ally in the 1845–46 Flagstaff War and helped broker the Treaty of Waitangi. He had converted to the Wesleyan faith in the 1830s and supported European trade. This portrait was painted from photographs nearly two decades after his death.
- **Function.** In Māori culture, portraits of ancestors act as vessels for the subject's **spirit and mana**, treated as living presences by descendants. Simultaneously, the painting served Pākehā (European New Zealander) collectors' taste for "ethnographic" portraits.
- **Content.** Nene is shown in three-quarter view holding a ceremonial **tewhatewha** staff hung with feathers and pressing a bone dagger to his lips. He wears a **kiwi feather cloak**, a large greenstone earring, and full facial **tā moko** tattoos — each a signifier of chiefly rank.
- **Form.** Conventional European oil technique: modeled volumes, **atmospheric perspective**, rich cool palette. Traditional Māori regalia is rendered with careful descriptive realism.

**Vocabulary:** portrait · tattooing (tā moko) · mana · atmospheric perspective · posthumous · rangatira
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.1
