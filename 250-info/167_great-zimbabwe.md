## #167 — Conical tower and circular wall of Great Zimbabwe

**Shona peoples** · c. 1000–1400 C.E. · Coursed granite blocks · Southeastern Zimbabwe · walls ~820 ft × 32 ft; tower ~30 ft × 18 ft

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Conical_Tower_-_Great_Enclosure_III_%2833736918448%29.jpg/250px-Conical_Tower_-_Great_Enclosure_III_%2833736918448%29.jpg" width="240" alt="Conical tower of Great Zimbabwe">

### Summary

The largest stone ruins in sub-Saharan Africa and **royal center of the Shona**.

- **Historical Context.** Capital of a wealthy inland Shona kingdom that may have held 18,000 residents and produced up to two-fifths of the world's gold by taxing trade with Swahili port cities on Africa's east coast. Declined in the 15th century from soil exhaustion, gold depletion, and trade shifts. Misattributed by 18th- and 19th-century European explorers to the **Queen of Sheba** rather than to its African builders. After independence, its name was chosen for the modern nation.
- **Function.** **Royal center** — the walled Great Enclosure housed the ruler and elite; the **Conical Tower** may have functioned as a granary whose grain symbolized the king as "custodian of the harvest." Also a trading hub and possibly a religious site tied to the supreme god **Mwari**.
- **Content.** Three complexes: the **Hill Ruin** (sacred cave, 30-ft walls, monoliths), the **Great Enclosure** (enormous walls, turrets, monoliths, and a narrow inner passage to the **Conical Tower**), and the **Valley Ruins** with evidence of long-distance trade reaching as far as Southeast Asia.
- **Form.** **Ashlar granite masonry** with no mortar — stones cut from nearby hills and fitted so precisely that gravity alone holds the walls. Some wall sections carry geometric chevron patterns.

**Vocabulary:** ashlar masonry · Shona · Mwari · Great Enclosure
