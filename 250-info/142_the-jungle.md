## #142 — The Jungle

**Wifredo Lam** (Cuban Surrealism / Cubism) · 1943 C.E. · Gouache on paper mounted on canvas · Museum of Modern Art, New York · 94¼ × 90½ in.

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/21/%27The_Jungle%27%2C_gouache_on_paper_painting_by_Wifredo_Lam%2C_1943%2C_Museum_of_Modern_Art.jpg/250px-%27The_Jungle%27%2C_gouache_on_paper_painting_by_Wifredo_Lam%2C_1943%2C_Museum_of_Modern_Art.jpg" width="240" alt="The Jungle">

### Summary

A **Cuban Surrealist–Cubist** indictment of colonialism and the afterlife of slavery.

- **Historical Context.** **Wifredo Lam** (Cuban, b. 1902) was of Afro-Cuban, Chinese, and European descent. Studying in Spain and Paris, he met the **Cubists** and **Surrealists** (including Picasso and Breton). After returning to Cuba he saw the poverty and oppression of Afro-Cubans firsthand, the legacy of sugar-plantation slavery and of the US-backed Batista regime. The painting was made in 1943, with WWII still ongoing.
- **Function.** A protest and reclamation. Lam wanted to expose the ongoing suffering of Black Cubans and the destructive legacy of colonialism and cash-crop economy — to push back against exoticized tropical imagery.
- **Content.** A dense jungle of sugarcane is occupied by four tall figures with elongated bodies, African-mask-like faces, bird- or animal-like hands and feet, and round buttocks. The figures are part human, part vegetal, part spirit; they appear to be harvesting their own crops, merging with the cane.
- **Form.** Gouache on paper mounted on canvas. Composition is denser at top, more open below; warm tones on the left contrast with cooler tones on the right. Geometric, Cubist breakdown of limbs combines with Surrealist hybrid forms; African mask vocabulary (partly via Picasso) drives the faces.

**Vocabulary:** gouache · Cubism · Surrealism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 3.3
