## #146 — Seagram Building

**Ludwig Mies van der Rohe and Philip Johnson** (International Style / Modernism) · 1954–1958 C.E. · Steel frame with glass curtain wall and bronze · New York City · 516 ft

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Seagram_Building_%2835098307116%29.jpg/330px-Seagram_Building_%2835098307116%29.jpg" width="240" alt="Seagram Building">

### Summary

The landmark **International-Style skyscraper** of postwar corporate America.

- **Historical Context.** Built during the postwar **1950s boom** in New York; at the time the most expensive skyscraper ever erected. **Mies van der Rohe** (German-born, emigrated to the U.S. before WWII) carried the European modernist creed into American corporate architecture — "structure is spiritual," "less is more." Co-designed with Philip Johnson.
- **Function.** Headquarters for the Canadian distiller **Joseph E. Seagram & Sons**; now owned by RFR Holdings. A monument to multinational corporate prestige.
- **Content.** A sheer bronze-and-glass tower set back on a granite plaza with twin reflecting pools on Park Avenue. The set-back created a new urban typology: the **plaza skyscraper**.
- **Form.** Steel frame encased in concrete for the building code. On the exterior, non-structural **bronze-toned I-beams** are welded to the curtain wall to suggest the building's skeleton — a visual honesty about structure. No decorative ornament. Interior lobby and restaurants ("The Four Seasons") lavishly finished in bronze, travertine, and marble.

**Vocabulary:** functionalist · corporate modernism · International Style · curtain wall
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.2, 3.5
