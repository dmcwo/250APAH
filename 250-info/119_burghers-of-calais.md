## #119 — The Burghers of Calais

**Auguste Rodin** · 1884–1895 C.E. · Bronze · Place du Soldat Inconnu, Calais, France · 82½ × 94 × 95 in

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Statue_bourgeois_calais_rodin.jpg/250px-Statue_bourgeois_calais_rodin.jpg" width="240" alt="The Burghers of Calais">

### Summary

A commemorative civic bronze that rejects heroic convention to honor six citizens' willingness to die for their city.

- **Historical Context.** During the **Hundred Years' War**, Calais was besieged by **Edward III of England**; after eleven months, the city decided to surrender. Terms required six of its most prominent leaders to come out barefoot in sackcloth, holding the keys to the city, to be executed. When they appeared, Edward III's wife **Philippa** implored him to spare them, and he did.
- **Function.** Commemorative: acknowledging the sacrifice the six were willing to make for their city. The work is meant to be seen up close and personally — Rodin intentionally set the figures low to the ground so they feel human and real.
- **Content.** Rodin was commissioned to commemorate the six citizens (or **burghers**) who offered to sacrifice their lives for Calais. The commissioners had wanted a single heroic figure to represent the group, but Rodin deemed it more appropriate to represent all six, depicting them **as they were leaving the city** heading toward execution, not as heroic figures after their reprieve.
- **Form.** The six burghers are arranged in a rough circle, allowing viewing from multiple angles. They are about life size and set as a group low to the ground, approachable and personal. Each face conveys a different emotion, exhibiting the feelings the men must have felt walking toward what they believed was their death.

### Compare-and-contrast exercise (LO 3.5)

**Prompt:** How did context affect artistic decisions the artist made?

> The statues were meant to commemorate the sacrifice the Burghers were willing to make, so Rodin made the sculpture depict the strong emotions the men must have been feeling at the time. The six men stand together as a group, ready to go to their fate. Rodin captured the moment and gave it new life, allowing people to understand what happened in Calais during the Hundred Years' War and relate to it as well as realize the gravity of it.
