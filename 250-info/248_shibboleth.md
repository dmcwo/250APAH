## #248 — Shibboleth

**Doris Salcedo (Colombian)** · 2007–2008 C.E. · Installation: concrete and steel-reinforced fissure in the floor · Tate Modern Turbine Hall, London (permanent scar remains) · 548 ft long, variable width and depth

### Summary

A jagged **crack running 548 feet through the floor of the Tate Modern** — a memorial to the exclusion of immigrants and the poor from Western modernity.

- **Historical Context.** **Doris Salcedo** (b. 1958 in Bogotá), shaped by the violence of Colombia's civil war, builds memorials to the disappeared and marginalized. Commissioned for the Tate Modern's **Unilever Series** in the Turbine Hall, *Shibboleth* opened in October 2007. Salcedo secretly cast the fissure from a mold over a year; the piece broke the ground of one of the world's most powerful art institutions. The title is a biblical word (Judges 12:6) for a shibboleth — a word used by one group to identify outsiders and kill them. Salcedo's subject: the **racialized exclusion** underpinning European modernity.
- **Function.** A **political/philosophical installation**. Visitors walked along, straddled, and peered into the fissure, feeling the instability of the ground beneath them. After the exhibition, the Tate filled the crack, but the repair line remains — a permanent scar.
- **Content.** A fissure begins as a hairline at one end and gradually widens and deepens as it crosses the hall. Steel mesh is visible inside the crack — a surrogate for borders, fences, walls.
- **Form.** **Subtractive sculpture / installation** — cast concrete inserted into the existing floor, using industrial fabrication. Entirely monochromatic gray; no applied color; only the wall of the museum and the crack itself.

**Vocabulary:** shibboleth · Spazialismo · transgressive
