# AP Art History — Theme: Nature & the Body

**AP Definition:** Considers representations of the natural world and the human body, including symbolism, idealization, anatomy, landscape, and relationships between humans and nature.

---

| Artwork ID | Artwork Name | Connection Level | Thematic Connection |
|---|---|---|---|
| 34 | Doryphoros (Spear Bearer) | essential | The Doryphoros is the definitive example of Classical Greek idealization of the male body: Polykleitos's Canon establishes a mathematical system of proportional relationships that produces the ideal body as an intellectual construction rather than an observed fact. The contrapposto pose, the balance of tension and relaxation, and the sense of arrested motion make this the foundational statement of the Western tradition's project of idealizing the human form — a project that dominates the AP 250 from Classical Greece through the Renaissance. |
| 72 | Birth of Venus | essential | Botticelli's Venus — nude, idealized, weightless, born from the sea on a shell — is the Renaissance's definitive statement on the female body as embodiment of ideal beauty, Nature's most perfect form, and the Neoplatonic fusion of earthly and divine love. The landscape (sea, wind, flowers, shore) is organized entirely around the central body, which arrives from Nature as Nature's gift. The painting also belongs to the long history of the male-authored female nude — who this idealization serves and what it costs is a question the AP exam expects students to think about. |
| 109 | The Oxbow | essential | Cole's panoramic view from Mount Holyoke after a thunderstorm — the dark, storm-blasted wilderness on the left, the sunlit, cultivated Connecticut River valley on the right — is the foundational statement of American landscape painting and its ideological entanglements: the question of whether the wilderness should be preserved or cultivated, whether nature is sublime threat or pastoral resource, structured American environmental and political debate throughout the nineteenth century. Cole's tiny self-portrait in the landscape makes the painter a figure within the nature he represents, complicating the viewer's relationship to the scene. |
| 2 | Great Hall of the Bulls | top-pick | The Lascaux paintings are among the earliest and most complex representations of animals in art: the images of aurochs, horses, deer, and felines — using the contours of the cave walls to suggest three-dimensionality — demonstrate a detailed observational knowledge of animal bodies and movement that suggests sustained, close attention to the natural world. Whether the images served hunting magic, shamanic ritual, or other purposes, they establish that the representation of nature is among humanity's oldest artistic impulses. |
| 27 | Anavysos Kouros | top-pick | The Archaic kouros tradition documents the systematic study of the human body as an artistic problem: the broad shoulders, the frontal symmetry, the step forward, and the increasingly naturalistic surface detail across the Archaic period reflect a culture committed to solving the problem of how to represent the body in three dimensions. The comparison between an early kouros and a late kouros (like the Kritios Boy) tracks one of art history's most clearly documented progressions toward bodily naturalism. |
| 75 | Sistine Chapel ceiling | top-pick | Michelangelo's ceiling is the supreme achievement of the Renaissance study of human anatomy as the foundation of artistic expression: every prophet, sibyl, nude ignudo, and narrative figure demonstrates the kind of deep anatomical knowledge (gained through dissection) that the Renaissance believed made the body's physical forms capable of expressing the full range of human spiritual and emotional states. The Creation of Adam is the paradigmatic image of the body as vessel of the divine. |
| 80 | Venus of Urbino | top-pick | Titian's reclining nude — the first in a long tradition of this specific pose — makes the female body the site where the idealization of nature (the nude as timeless ideal) and the specificity of social circumstance (a real woman in a Venetian interior) coexist in productive tension. The comparison with Manet's Olympia (which directly quotes and challenges this tradition) is one of the AP exam's most useful formal-thematic pairings. |
| 107 | La Grande Odalisque | top-pick | Ingres's anatomically impossible Odalisque — her spine extended by at least three vertebrae to create a sinuously elongated back, her body a patchwork of Classical ideal parts assembled without anatomical consistency — represents the Neoclassical tradition's willingness to distort the observed body to achieve ideal formal effect. The tension between idealization and anatomy, between observed nature and constructed beauty, is the painting's central argument. |
| 118 | Valley of Mexico from the Hillside of Santa Isabel | top-pick | Velasco's panoramic landscape — the Valley of Mexico seen from an elevated viewpoint, the volcanic peaks of Iztaccíhuatl and Popocatépetl in the distance — is a statement of Mexican national landscape painting: nature as the ground of national identity, the specific topography of the Mexican landscape as the visual material for constructing a post-colonial sense of place. The painting engages the same questions as Cole's Oxbow but in a Latin American context where the relationship between nature, cultivation, and colonial history has entirely different stakes. |
| 120 | The Starry Night | top-pick | Van Gogh's nocturnal landscape — the sky alive with swirling, flaming stars, the village below dark and still — represents the Post-Impressionist project of using nature not as a subject for objective description but as a medium for expressing subjective psychological and spiritual states. The brushwork that makes the stars pulse and the cypresses flame with an inner energy makes visible the Expressionist argument that the artist's experience of nature is more real than its external appearance. |
| 201 | Travelers among Mountains and Streams | top-pick | Fan Kuan's monumental Song Dynasty ink landscape is the canonical statement of Chinese landscape painting and its cosmological program: the vast, mist-shrouded mountains dwarf the tiny travelers in the foreground, expressing the Daoist understanding that the human being is a small, temporary presence within the enduring natural order. The Chinese genre of shanshui (mountain-water) painting represents nature not as background or scenery but as the primary subject and the site of spiritual meaning. |
| 211 | Under the Wave off Kanagawa (Great Wave) | top-pick | Hokusai's woodblock print — the enormous wave dwarfing the fishing boats, Mount Fuji tiny in the background — captures the sublime relationship between human bodies and natural force: the wave simultaneously threatens to destroy and is an object of aesthetic wonder. The print also demonstrates the Japanese woodblock technique's capacity to represent natural movement through pattern, the stylized foam of the wave-tips becoming the work's most recognizable formal element. |
| 10 | Tlatilco female figurine | related | The Tlatilco figurines' exaggerated breasts, hips, and thighs represent female bodies as concentrations of natural reproductive power. Whether they were fertility objects, funerary offerings, or status markers, they document a consistent human impulse to represent the body not as it is but as what it means — the body as a symbol of natural force rather than an observed individual. |
| 18 | King Menkaura and queen | related | Egyptian dyad sculpture presents the human body as an idealized, permanent form: the striding pharaoh, the flat-footed frontal stance, the idealized musculature, and the smooth surface untouched by age or individuality express the Egyptian belief that the royal body participates in divine nature and therefore should be represented in its ideal, eternal form rather than its specific, mortal actuality. |
| 37 | Winged Victory of Samothrace | related | The Hellenistic Nike — her wings spread, her drapery wind-pressed against her body, her torso twisting forward in the posture of landing — represents the body in its most energetic engagement with nature: wind, movement, the act of alighting on a ship's prow. The wet drapery technique that reveals the body beneath the cloth and the dynamic contrapposto carry forward the Classical project of bodily idealization into a new register of movement and environmental relationship. |
| 38 | Great Altar of Zeus at Pergamon | related | The Gigantomachy frieze — divine and human bodies in violent combat, each figure exhibiting extreme muscular exertion, pain, and power — represents the Hellenistic push toward the body as a site of emotional and physical extremity. The serpent legs of the Giants (half-human, half-natural) and the divine bodies locked in struggle with them blur the boundary between human form and natural force. |
| 41 | Seated Boxer | related | The battered bronze boxer — his body the record of accumulated physical punishment rather than athletic ideal — represents the Hellenistic turn toward the body as it is rather than as it should be: the broken nose, the cauliflower ears, the cuts, and the exhausted posture are detailed with the same attention that Classical sculpture gave to ideal proportions. Nature (specifically the wear of nature on the body) becomes the subject. |
| 74 | Adam and Eve (Dürer) | related | Dürer's engraving places the idealized bodies of Adam and Eve — proportioned using Classical models Dürer researched in Italy — in a dense Northern European forest where the animals (cat, rabbit, elk, parrot, mouse) carry precise symbolic meanings about the humors and temperaments disrupted by the Fall. The bodies are ideal Nature before the Fall; the surrounding natural world is already fallen Nature, full of ominous symbol. |
| 96 | Fruit and Insects (Ruysch) | related | Ruysch's Dutch still life is simultaneously a scientific document and a meditation on natural transience: the insects, the dew, the fallen petals, and the overripe fruit are painted with botanical and entomological precision while composing a traditional vanitas argument about the brevity of natural beauty. The genre of flower painting emerged in a culture with strong interests in botanical science and global trade in natural specimens. |
| 123 | Where Do We Come From? What Are We? Where Are We Going? | related | Gauguin's Tahitian painting poses the universal questions of human existence through bodies immersed in a tropical natural landscape: the figures move from infancy to age across a continuous frieze, their brown bodies in direct physical relationship with the earth, fruit, and animals of a "primitive" Eden that Gauguin projects onto Polynesia. The painting's complex and troubling relationship to colonial exoticism makes it a rich site for discussing the politics of how the Western tradition has represented "nature" and "natural" bodies. |
| 128 | The Kiss (Klimt) | related | Klimt's golden couple — their bodies dissolved into flat gold mosaic pattern from the waist down, merged with the flowered hillside on which they kneel — represents the body in a moment of dissolution into nature: the boundary between the human figures and the natural ground literally disappears in the golden cloak, making love a state of merger with the natural and the decorative simultaneously. |
| 131 | Goldfish (Matisse) | related | Matisse's painting — goldfish in a bowl, seen simultaneously from above and through the glass, surrounded by interior furniture and garden glimpses — represents the Fauvist project of using color and simplified form to express the sensory pleasure of experiencing the natural world rather than to describe it accurately. Nature is a source of joy and chromatic intensity, not scientific data. |
| 151 | Spiral Jetty | related | Smithson's earthwork coiled into the Great Salt Lake directly engages the geological and ecological nature of a specific site: the pink alkaline water, the salt crystals that have grown over the black basalt rocks since 1970, and the industrial ruins on the surrounding shore are all part of the work. Spiral Jetty makes human intervention in the natural landscape — and nature's ongoing transformation of that intervention — the subject of art. |
| 234 | Earth's Creation | related | Emily Kame Kngwarreye's dense dot-painting refers to the Dreamtime narrative of the Anmatyerr people's relationship to the land: the work is not a representation of landscape but a map of the artist's knowledge of country — the plants, seeds, and waterholes of the land she is custodian for. Nature here is not scenery but kinship, a set of relationships between people and place that constitute identity and spiritual obligation. |
| 239 | The Crossing | related | Bill Viola's two-channel video — a body consumed by water on one screen, by fire on the other — uses the elemental forces of nature (the two ancient opposites) as agents of both destruction and purification. The work draws on mystical traditions across cultures that understand the natural elements as forces capable of transformation, and the body as the site where that transformation occurs. |
| 242 | Lying with the Wolf | related | Kiki Smith's drawing of a naked woman lying companionably with a wolf in a forest setting reimagines the body's relationship to nature and to the animals that fairy tales cast as threats: the woman is relaxed, not prey. The work reclaims a connection between the female body and the natural world that the predator-prey narrative of Western folklore has structured as dangerous, offering instead an image of peaceful coexistence between the human body and wild nature. |
