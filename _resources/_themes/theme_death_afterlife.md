# AP Art History — Theme: Death & Afterlife

**AP Definition:** Studies visual expressions related to mortality, funerary practices, ancestor veneration, tomb architecture, and beliefs about the afterlife.

---

| Artwork ID | Artwork Name | Connection Level | Thematic Connection |
|---|---|---|---|
| 17 | Great Pyramids and Great Sphinx | essential | The Giza complex is history's most monumental funerary architecture, and the AP exam's anchor example for the relationship between death, royal power, and architectural permanence. Each pyramid is a resurrection machine — its form (the ben-ben, primordial mound of creation), its orientation (toward circumpolar stars that never set), and its interior sequence (passage to the burial chamber) all serve Egyptian beliefs about the pharaoh's transformation into a divine being and eternal co-pilot of the sun. |
| 24 | Last Judgment of Hu-Nefer | essential | The Book of the Dead papyrus is the most explicit visual statement of Egyptian funerary belief in the AP 250: the weighing of the heart against Ma'at's feather, the assembly of divine judges, Anubis guiding the deceased, and Ammit waiting to devour the unworthy. The papyrus was not decorative but functional — a ritual guide buried with the mummy to navigate the underworld — making it one of the AP exam's clearest examples of art as direct participant in funerary ritual. |
| 193 | Terra cotta warriors | essential | The First Emperor of China's buried army — thousands of individualized, life-size terra cotta soldiers, horses, and officials arrayed in military formation beneath a mountain-shaped burial mound — is the most spectacular example in the AP 250 of art created entirely to serve the dead in the afterlife. The logic that the emperor would need a functional army, administrative staff, and entertainers in death demonstrates how beliefs about the afterlife can drive the creation of art on a scale that rivals or exceeds anything produced for the living. |
| 23 | Tutankhamun's innermost coffin | top-pick | The solid-gold innermost coffin of Tutankhamun — the pharaoh depicted as Osiris, god of the dead — is the physical center of the most lavishly documented royal funerary ensemble in history. The nesting coffins, the canopic shrine, the gilded shrines, the mummy mask, and the tomb's painted chambers together constitute a complete visual theology of Egyptian beliefs about royal death, transformation, and eternal life. |
| 47 | Ludovisi Battle Sarcophagus | top-pick | The Late Imperial Roman sarcophagus — its entire surface packed with a writhing mass of Roman and barbarian soldiers in violent combat — represents a shift in Roman funerary practice from cremation to inhumation and from portrait commemoration to narrative glorification. The deceased general is depicted triumphant at the center, his enemies surrounding him in defeated mass — death becomes the occasion for the ultimate statement of military identity and virtue. |
| 29 | Sarcophagus of the Spouses | top-pick | The Etruscan terracotta sarcophagus depicting a husband and wife reclining together at the banquet of the afterlife is the AP exam's primary example of Etruscan funerary art and its distinctive conception of death: the afterlife as an extension of elite banquet culture, the couple animated and intimate rather than passive and idealized, and the female figure depicted as an equal participant — all contrasting sharply with Greek and Roman funerary conventions. |
| 194 | Funeral banner of Lady Dai | top-pick | The Han Dynasty silk funeral banner — its three-tiered composition moving from the underworld below through the earthly realm to a heavenly paradise above, the deceased Lady Dai depicted at center — is one of the most complete visual statements of Chinese Han Dynasty cosmology and funerary belief in any medium. The banner was placed over the innermost coffin to guide the soul's ascent and marks an early moment in the Chinese visual tradition of mapping the afterlife. |
| 48 | Catacomb of Priscilla | top-pick | The Roman Christian catacombs are simultaneously a burial ground and the visual record of early Christian funerary theology: the orant figures (praying souls), the portraits of the deceased, the chi-rho symbols, and the earliest known image of the Virgin and Child all map emerging Christian beliefs about resurrection, the intercession of saints, and the continuity of community between the living and the dead. The catacomb's underground nature — close to the martyrs' relics — made burial there a spiritual act as well as a practical one. |
| 63 | Arena (Scrovegni) Chapel | top-pick | The Arena Chapel was commissioned by Enrico Scrovegni as an act of funerary piety and personal atonement: he built and decorated it to atone for his father's usury (depicted in Dante's Inferno) and eventually arranged to be buried within it. The chapel's complete fresco program — culminating in Giotto's monumental Last Judgment on the west wall — makes the building itself a funerary monument designed to secure the patron's afterlife through art as intercessory device. |
| 209 | Taj Mahal | top-pick | The Taj Mahal is simultaneously the world's most celebrated funerary monument and an act of Islamic theological argument: the white marble mausoleum for Mumtaz Mahal (and later Shah Jahan himself) is surrounded by the chaharbagh (paradise garden), inscribed throughout with Qur'anic verses describing paradise, and oriented so the setting sun turns the marble amber on the side facing Mecca. Death here is treated as the threshold to paradise, and the monument is an architectural argument for the Qur'anic conception of the afterlife. |
| 8 | Stonehenge | related | Stonehenge was used as a burial site across many centuries of its use, and its astronomical alignments with solstice events suggest a monument where the movement of heavenly bodies — and their relationship to death and rebirth — organized communal ritual. As a prehistoric example, it demonstrates that monumental architecture in service of death and afterlife beliefs is among humanity's earliest and most persistent impulses. |
| 32 | Tomb of the Triclinium | related | The Etruscan tomb — its name referring to the three-couch dining arrangement depicted in the frescoes — imagines the afterlife as a continuation of elite banquet culture: musicians play, dancers perform, and revelers feast in vivid, movement-filled frescoes. The paintings' subjects are not death but its antidote — pleasure, motion, and celebration projected into the underground space where the body rests. |
| 36 | Grave stele of Hegeso | related | The Classical Athenian grave stele — a woman selecting jewelry from a servant's box, rendered in shallow marble relief — is a funerary monument to a domestic female life commemorated in the terms of that life's most intimate, private rituals. The stele marks the transition from archaic kouros-type grave markers to Classical narrative commemoration, and the woman's act of selecting jewelry in the presence of her servant captures a moment of ordinary life made permanent by death. |
| 64 | Golden Haggadah | related | While primarily a Passover liturgical manuscript, the Golden Haggadah's depiction of the Plagues of Egypt — death of the firstborn, the Angel of Death passing over marked houses — engages the theme of death as divine instrument and the protection of a community marked for survival. The ritual of Passover itself is a communal act of memory that keeps the dead (those who did not survive Egypt) present in the liturgy of the living. |
| 156 | Great Serpent Mound | related | The Great Serpent Mound — an earthwork effigy of a serpent with an oval feature in its mouth, aligned with astronomical events — is associated with burial mounds in the surrounding landscape, though the effigy mound itself contains no burials. As an earthwork associated with death and cosmological belief, it demonstrates how Indigenous North American communities created monumental landscapes that organized their relationships with the dead and the cosmos. |
| 167 | Conical tower and circular wall of Great Zimbabwe | related | Great Zimbabwe's enclosure complex served as both a royal residence and, ultimately, a funerary precinct: the conical tower's solid form (no interior chamber, no practical function) has been interpreted as a symbolic structure associated with royal ancestral power. Shona beliefs about ancestor veneration and the ongoing power of royal lineages over the living community gave the site a funerary-political significance that outlasted the kingdom itself. |
| 172 | Power figure (Nkisi n'kondi) | related | The Kongo nkisi figure is a mediator between the living and the dead: the medicines loaded in its cavity connect it to ancestral spiritual power, and its deployment in rituals of healing, oath-taking, and justice draws on the authority of the dead to compel and protect the living. The accumulation of iron nails over time — each marking a specific ritual transaction — makes the figure a physical archive of the community's ongoing relationship with ancestral power. |
| 179 | Reliquary figure (byeri) | related | Fang byeri figures are designed explicitly to guard and mediate the power of ancestral relics: placed atop containers of skull and bone material from important ancestors, they make the dead continuously present and protective for the living family. The figure is not a portrait of the ancestor but a guardian of the ancestor's remaining physical presence — a distinction that reflects a specific cosmology of how the dead remain active in the world of the living. |
| 214 | Moai on platform (ahu) | related | The moai of Rapa Nui — with their backs to the sea, their eyes (when fitted with coral inlays) gazing inland toward the living community — are monuments to ancestral chiefs whose mana (spiritual power) was understood to flow into the community through the stone figures. The ahu platforms are burial sites; the moai above them make the chiefs' afterlife power spatially and visually present to the community they continue to protect. |
| 222 | Malagan display and mask | related | Malagan objects — intricate carved and painted figures produced in large numbers in New Ireland for memorial ceremonies held months or years after a death — are made to be displayed briefly in ritual and then discarded or destroyed. Their value is not in permanence but in the ritual process of their production (which transmits rights and cultural knowledge) and display. The Malagan tradition directly challenges Western assumptions about funerary art as built for permanence, demonstrating that the ritual event rather than the object is the locus of cultural meaning. |
| 225 | Vietnam Veterans Memorial | related | Maya Lin's granite wall — inscribed with the names of 58,279 Americans who died in Vietnam, set into the earth so that visitors descend into it — is a contemporary funerary monument that refuses the heroic conventions of military commemoration. The reflective surface mirrors the living visitor beside the names of the dead, collapsing the distinction between commemoration and encounter. The debate surrounding its design (perceived as a "black gash of shame" vs. a revolutionary act of empathetic commemoration) makes it a case study in how contemporary societies negotiate what death, sacrifice, and memory require of public art. |
