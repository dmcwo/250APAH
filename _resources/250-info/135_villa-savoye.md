## #135 — Villa Savoye

**Le Corbusier** (International Style / Modernism) · 1929 C.E. · Steel and reinforced concrete · Poissy-sur-Seine, France

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/3c/VillaSavoye.jpg/250px-VillaSavoye.jpg" width="240" alt="Villa Savoye">

### Summary

The textbook demonstration of Le Corbusier's **Five Points of a New Architecture**.

- **Historical Context.** Designed by Swiss-French architect **Le Corbusier** (Charles-Édouard Jeanneret-Gris) in 1929 in the village of **Poissy**, just outside Paris. Sits at the center of the **International Style** of modernism, which stripped architecture of ornament and demanded that form follow function — a machine-age aesthetic abstracted from its landscape.
- **Function.** A private weekend country house for the Savoye family; simultaneously a built manifesto for Le Corbusier's **Five Points**: pilotis, roof garden, free plan, horizontal ribbon windows, and free façade.
- **Content.** Two floors on stilts. Ground floor holds the entrance hall, garage, and servants' rooms, laid out on the turning radius of a car. The main floor above has the bedrooms and a long ramp rising to the **roof garden**. Continuous **ribbon windows** run around the exterior.
- **Form.** Reinforced concrete and steel; a white cube lifted off the earth on slender **pilotis**. Walls are non-load-bearing, freeing the façade for aesthetics alone. Geometric, machine-like precision with no decorative relief.

**Vocabulary:** International Style · Modernist · Five Points · pilotis
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.2

> Both the Villa Savoye and the Seagram Buildings are exemplars of the modernist style, which emphasized functional utilitarianism, and also detachment from the environment to a more abstract form. They are also both in the international style, which can be seen by the uniformity in both, which was emphasized in the Seagram Building with the windows only being able to be in the open, closed, or half open position, so to minimize the disorder from idiosyncratic windown opening tendencies.
