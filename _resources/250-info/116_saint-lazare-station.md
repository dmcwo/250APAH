## #116 — The Saint-Lazare Station

**Claude Monet** · 1877 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Musée d'Orsay, Paris · 21.4 × 28.9 in

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/La_Gare_Saint-Lazare_-_Claude_Monet.jpg/330px-La_Gare_Saint-Lazare_-_Claude_Monet.jpg" width="240" alt="The Saint-Lazare Station">

### Summary

An Impressionist study of a bustling Parisian train station as a site of **modern life, light, and atmosphere**.

- **Historical Context.** Monet was a founding artist of **Impressionism**, which emphasized small brushstrokes, accurate depictions of lighting, and the passage of time — and was at the time dismissed as frivolous compared to the chiaroscuro of traditional European painting. The Gare Saint-Lazare was a symbol of modern Paris, a site favored by forward-looking artists (**Manet** also depicted it). The series was made the year after Monet's wife **Camille** was diagnosed with tuberculosis. Critics coined the name "Impressionists" partly from the paintings' unfinished look.
- **Function.** The set of paintings of the Gare Saint-Lazare were painted as a **study of light throughout the day** on a single subject, the train station.
- **Content.** Trains, workers, and everyday passengers at the Gare Saint-Lazare, with **billowing smoke** from the trains covering most of the scene. Different views of the station can be identified precisely by where Monet stood. Smoke, skies, and lighting take on blue hues ranging to yellow depending on time of day and weather — the smoke, light, and color become the real focus.
- **Form.** Small, quick brushstrokes and a wide variance in color to portray lighting with depth. Oil on canvas. Objects and figures are not drawn in great detail — Monet focuses on general shape and lighting, yielding near-abstract human figures.

**Vocabulary:** Impressionism · plein air
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.1
