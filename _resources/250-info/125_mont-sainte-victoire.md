## #125 — Mont Sainte-Victoire

**Paul Cézanne** · 1902–1904 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Aix-en-Provence, France · 73 × 91.9 cm

### Summary

One of Cézanne's eleven **series** paintings of the mountain near his hometown — a structural study that became the key bridge from Impressionism to **Cubism**.

- **Historical Context.** Cézanne was born in **Aix-en-Provence** (1839) and felt closely tied to its landscape. He worked mostly outside the Paris art scene and was not widely recognized until late in life. The mountain, known as the "Mountain of Holy Victory," commemorates Provence's ancient Roman inhabitants defeating invaders. Later classified under **Post-Impressionism** — artists who responded to Impressionism with simplified forms, abstraction, and emotional/symbolic content.
- **Function.** Cézanne aimed to "**make Impressionism something solid and durable, like the art of the museums**" — analyzing the underlying structure of his subject rather than recording fleeting appearance. None of his mountain paintings look the same; each tests a different structural solution.
- **Content.** The imposing **Mont Sainte-Victoire** dominates the distance. The composition divides into three bands: blue mountain, yellow plain, foreground trees. A few houses are vaguely visible below; no figures, no narrative.
- **Form.** Patches of **undiluted color** and geometric construction build the forms. Warmer/darker foreground colors against cooler background colors create depth even as the paint remains emphatically surface-bound. Rough, visible brushstrokes; the mountain is grounded and firmly built from color planes.

**Vocabulary:** Post-Impressionism · series · structural analysis
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.4, 3.2
