## #98 — The Tête à Tête, from Marriage à la Mode

**William Hogarth** · c. 1743 C.E. · Oil on canvas · National Gallery, London · 27.5 × 35.7 in

### Summary

A biting **satire of aristocratic marriage** produced for the expanding English middle class as engraved prints.

- **Historical Context.** Painted in mid-18th-century London during the **Industrial Revolution**, when the merchant bourgeoisie was rising and the aristocracy declining. Hogarth pioneered the **"modern moral subject**," modeling paintings for affordable engraved prints he sold at one shilling. *Marriage à la Mode* is a six-painting series about arranged marriages.
- **Function.** Painted as a **model for prints** so ordinary buyers could own the series. A satirical narrative skewering greed, arranged marriages, venereal disease, and aristocratic decadence.
- **Content.** The second scene of the cycle. **Viscount Squanderfield** slouches exhausted after a night out; his wife stretches coyly after her own entertainment; a steward throws up his hands at unpaid bills. A dog sniffs a **lady's bonnet** in the Viscount's pocket. Saints stare from paintings in the adjoining room, contrasting with the erotic canvas shrouded by a green curtain.
- **Form.** Oil on canvas, packed with **symbolic detail** (broken Greek head for impotence; mismatched Rococo-Chinoiserie-Palladian clock ridiculing fashionable taste). Three figures distributed across the canvas; receding rooms create perspectival depth that fits the satirist's narrative richness.

**Vocabulary:** satire · realism · "à la Mode"
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3

**Theme:** Paintings within paintings

> Both Las Meninas and The Tete a Tete are paintings depicting paintings. Their purposes are different, however. In The Tete a Tete, the paintings serve to contrast a past morality with the current debauchery of the couple. In Las Meninas, the angle of the figures and the easel are enigmatic, confusing the viewer about who is painting what, and yet there are even more paintings by Rubens on the walls.

#### → #68 — The Arnolfini Portrait (Jan van Eyck, 1434)

**Theme:** Small symbols within details, marriage

> Both the Arnolfini Portrait and The Tete a Tete are noticeable for their use of symbolism in each detail. In the Arnolfini Portrait, even such things as the oranges on the windowsill demonstrate wealth. In The Tete a Tete, the broken nose on the Classical Greek head can represent impotence. Both works also depict the union of a man and woman. However, The Tete a Tete is satirical and their relationship is a complete mess in comparison to the gentle joining of hands in The Arnolfini Portrait.

#### → #101 — The Swing (Jean-Honoré Fragonard, 1767)

**Theme:** Infidelity

> The Swing is quite a naughty painting. The lady is being watched by her lover on the left, while being pushed by an older man who is unaware of the lover. Both The Tete a Tete and The Swing depict an unfaithful woman. However, The Swing seems more romantic in the idea as the angel sculpture holds its finger as if to keep the lover a secret. The Swing values pleasure and base human aspirations.
