## #214 — Moai on Platform (ahu)

**Rapa Nui people** · c. 1100–1600 C.E. · Volcanic tuff figures on basalt base · Rapa Nui (Easter Island) · Average ~7.94 × 3.15 × 1.54 ft

### Summary

Monumental ancestral portrait figures set on ceremonial **ahu platforms** — the defining art of Polynesian Rapa Nui.

- **Historical Context.** Carved by the Rapa Nui, Polynesians who settled the remote volcanic island perhaps c. 800–1200 C.E. Nearly 1,000 **moai** were produced, largely at the **Rano Raraku** quarry, and transported (probably "walked" upright with ropes) to coastal ahu. Production declined after c. 1600 with deforestation, clan warfare, and eventual European contact. Many moai were toppled during intraclan conflicts and, later, after Christianization; a number have been re-erected since the 20th century.
- **Function.** Ancestor effigies generating **mana** (spiritual power) for the community. Moai face inland with their backs to the sea, guarding and blessing the living descendants.
- **Content.** Over-large heads on blocky torsos with arms at the sides and hands resting on the belly. Elongated earlobes, heavy brows, pursed lips, prominent nose. Some bear red scoria **pukao** (topknots) and once had inset coral-and-obsidian eyes (inserted only during ritual "activation").
- **Form.** Carved from soft volcanic **tuff** using basalt picks. Austere geometric style with minimal detail. Set on rectangular ahu platforms of cut basalt blocks.

**Vocabulary:** moai · ahu · mana · tuff
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.1, 3.3
