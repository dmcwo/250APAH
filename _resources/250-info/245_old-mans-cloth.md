## #245 — Old Man's Cloth

**El Anatsui (Ghanaian, working in Nigeria)** · 2003 C.E. · Aluminum (flattened liquor-bottle caps and labels) and copper wire · Harn Museum of Art, Gainesville · 16′ × 17′1″

### Summary

A shimmering "metal cloth" of **flattened liquor-bottle caps** — El Anatsui's signature contemporary reimagining of West African Kente cloth and the transatlantic slave trade.

- **Historical Context.** **El Anatsui** (b. 1944 in Ghana) studied in the 1960s during the height of African **decolonization**; he became a central figure at the University of Nigeria, Nsukka, and a leader of the pan-African **Sankofa** revival of traditional forms. His metal textiles of the early 2000s — made from bottle caps collected near his studio in Nsukka — reference the **triangular trade** in which European liquor was exchanged for enslaved Africans, while simultaneously evoking West African **Kente** cloth.
- **Function.** A contemporary display work made for museums and biennials. Conceptually, a **commemorative textile** — folding the history of the slave trade, colonial consumerism, and Akan craft into a single shimmering surface.
- **Content.** Thousands of flattened aluminum tops and labels from schnapps and other liquor bottles, stitched with copper wire into interlocking rectangular blocks of gold, red, black, and silver — mimicking the color and weave of Kente. Draped over the wall, its folds catch the light.
- **Form.** Enormous, flexible metal textile. Made in **communal workshop** with apprentices, echoing Akan cloth-making traditions. Installs differently each time, never twice in exactly the same folds.

**Vocabulary:** decolonization · Sankofa · sunsum · Kente
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3
