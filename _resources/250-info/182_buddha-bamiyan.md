## #182 — Buddha, Bamiyan

**Gandharan / Bamiyan culture** · c. 400–800 C.E. (destroyed 2001) · Cut rock with plaster and polychrome paint · Bamiyan Valley, Afghanistan · one 175 ft, other 115 ft

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Buddha_of_Bamiyan.jpg/250px-Buddha_of_Bamiyan.jpg" width="240" alt="Buddha of Bamiyan">

### Summary

Two **colossal standing Buddhas** at a key waypoint on the Silk Road — destroyed by the Taliban in 2001.

- **Historical Context.** Carved between the 4th and 8th centuries at the western end of the **Silk Road**, Bamiyan was a major stop for Buddhist pilgrims and traders as Buddhism spread across Asia. The two Buddhas represented the largest standing Buddha figures in the world. In **2001 the Taliban destroyed them** as idolatrous, an act of deliberate iconoclasm; only the niches and outlines remain.
- **Function.** Public pilgrimage images that demonstrated the reach and prestige of Buddhism. Surrounding caves housed monks and Buddhist wall paintings; pilgrims could walk up through passages to the Buddhas' shoulders.
- **Content.** Two colossal standing Buddhas — one (the Western, "Salsal") traditionally identified as **Vairocana** (the universal Buddha), the other (the Eastern, "Shahmama") as **Sakyamuni** (the historical Buddha). Frontal, symmetrical, with a halo-shaped niche around each body.
- **Form.** **Rock-cut** figures carved from the sandstone cliff, finished in stucco and **polychrome paint**, originally gilded in places. Flowing drapery reflects **Hellenistic and Gandharan** influences (Roman toga) fused with Indian Buddhist iconography.

**Vocabulary:** Vairocana · Sakyamuni · iconoclasm
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.1, 2.2, 3.1
