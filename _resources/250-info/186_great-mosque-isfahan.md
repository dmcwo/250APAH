## #186 — Great Mosque (Masjid-e Jameh), Isfahan

**Nizam al-Mulk, Taj al-Mulk** (Persian, under Seljuq patronage) · founded c. 700 C.E.; expanded 11th c.; restored 14th, 19th, 20th centuries · Stone, brick, wood, plaster, glazed ceramic tile · Isfahan, Iran · 20,000+ sq m

### Summary

A thousand-year **layered mosque** showing every major phase of Persian Islamic architecture.

- **Historical Context.** Originally founded under the **Abbasid** dynasty in present-day Iran. The 11th-century **Seljuq** expansion produced the two domed chambers — the south dome (over the **mihrab**) and the north dome — built a year apart by two rival architects, **Nizam al-Mulk** and **Taj al-Mulk**. Successive Mongol, Timurid, and **Safavid** patrons continued to add to it through the 20th century, so the complex records a thousand years of Persian architectural evolution.
- **Function.** A **congregational (Friday) mosque** with ongoing religious, educational (madrasa/library), and civic functions — still in use today, now also a tourist hub and UNESCO World Heritage Site.
- **Content.** A **four-iwan** hypostyle plan centered on a large open courtyard (**sahn**) with a fountain for ablutions. The south iwan leads to the mihrab and qibla; others open onto libraries, schools, and treasuries. Tilework in brilliant **turquoise** blue throughout. The muqarnas-vaulted ceilings, domes, and iwans are among the most celebrated examples of Persian-Islamic architecture.
- **Form.** **Hypostyle** plan with **four iwans** and two brick **domes**; glazed ceramic tile exteriors and **muqarnas** on many interior surfaces; vegetal **arabesque** and **calligraphic** decoration in keeping with Islamic aniconism.

**Vocabulary:** qibla · mihrab · minaret · muqarnas · arabesque · minbar · sahn · calligraphy · tessellations · iwan
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.2
