## #109 — The Oxbow (View from Mount Holyoke, Northampton, Massachusetts, after a Thunderstorm)

**Thomas Cole** · 1836 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York · 51.5 × 76 in

### Summary

A foundational **Hudson River School** landscape dramatizing America's choice between **wilderness and civilization**.

- **Historical Context.** Painted by **Thomas Cole**, founder of the **Hudson River School** of American landscape painting, during a pause from his allegorical *Course of Empire* series (commissioned by patron **Luman Reed**). Painted at the height of **Manifest Destiny**, the belief in America's divinely ordained westward expansion.
- **Function.** A **Romantic landscape** meant for display at the National Academy of Design's annual exhibition. Reed's commission let Cole return to what he did best while working out national-identity themes in miniature.
- **Content.** A diagonal composition splits the canvas in two: the **left** shows storm-swept **wilderness** — dark clouds, a lightning-struck tree — while the **right** reveals a sunlit, cultivated **Connecticut River valley** wrapped around the iconic oxbow bend. A tiny **self-portrait** of Cole, painting at his easel, sits in the foreground of the wilderness side.
- **Form.** Oil on canvas; dramatic **tonal contrast** between storm and sun; atmospheric perspective pulls the eye deep into the valley. The curving river, the diagonal cloud line, and the lone tree split anchor a careful moral geometry.

**Vocabulary:** Hudson River School · Manifest Destiny · Romanticism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3
