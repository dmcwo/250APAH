## #143 — Dream of a Sunday Afternoon in the Alameda Park (*Sueño de una tarde dominical en la Alameda Central*)

**Diego Rivera** (Mexican Muralism) · 1947–1948 C.E. · Fresco · Museo Mural Diego Rivera, Mexico City · 4.8 × 15 m

### Summary

A **history of Mexico** told as a dream in the Alameda Central park.

- **Historical Context.** Painted 1947–1948 originally for the **Hotel del Prado** in Mexico City. After the 1985 earthquake damaged the hotel, the fresco was moved to a purpose-built museum beside the **Alameda Central**, Mexico City's first public park and former Aztec marketplace. Spans three major eras: **Spanish conquest**, **Porfirio Díaz dictatorship**, and **Mexican Revolution**.
- **Function.** A public celebration and critique of Mexican identity — Rivera honors indigenous and popular culture while attacking the colonial Church, the dictatorship, and class oppression. Also a personal autobiography: Rivera paints himself as a child at center, holding hands with **La Catrina**, with **Frida Kahlo** behind him holding a yin-yang.
- **Content.** More than 400 historical figures: **Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz** in nun's habit; **Benito Juárez**, five-term indigenous-origin president; **Ignacio Ramírez** holding a paper reading "God does not exist"; Santa Anna handing keys of Mexico to General Winfield Scott; Emperor Maximilian and Empress Carlota; José Martí tipping his hat; Porfirio Díaz dozing with his medals; a police officer evicting a poor family from the park.
- **Form.** Enormous horror-vacui fresco — packed with figures edge to edge — vibrant saturated color, heroic scale.

**Vocabulary:** La Catrina · Porfirio Díaz · Sor Juana · fresco
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.3, 3.2
