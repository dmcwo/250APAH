## #178 — Aka elephant mask

**Bamileke peoples** (Cameroon, western grassfields region) · c. 19th–20th century C.E. · Wood, woven raffia, cloth, and beads · 57¾ × 20½ × 11½ in.

### Summary

A **royal masquerade** mask worn by elite members of the Kuosi society to embody the spirit of the elephant.

- **Historical Context.** Made by the **Bamileke** of the Cameroon grasslands. Elephants and beadwork were reserved symbols of political power; only wealthy warriors and high-status elites of the **Kuosi society** could wear the mask. Rooted in the broadly African belief that ancestral spirits persist and can inhabit masked dancers.
- **Function.** Worn at **royal court ceremonies** and performances to transform the dancer into the elephant-spirit, conveying strength, wealth, and power. Economic and social signal of elite status.
- **Content.** An elongated cloth panel at the front represents the elephant's **trunk**; two large circular panels at the sides are its **ears**; beaded geometric patterns in black, white, red, and blue cover the whole. A humanoid face sits above the trunk — the fluidity between human and animal spirit.
- **Form.** Wood-and-textile mask covered in elaborate **beaded patterns** and woven raffia. Long, draped form built for dancing rather than static display.

**Vocabulary:** Aka · raffia · Kuosi society
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4
