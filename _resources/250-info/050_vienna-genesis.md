## #50 — Rebecca and Eliezer at the Well and Jacob Wrestling the Angel, from the Vienna Genesis

**Early Byzantine Europe** · Early 6th century C.E. · Illuminated manuscript (tempera, gold, and silver on purple vellum) · Österreichische Nationalbibliothek, Vienna · 12.5 × 9.25 in

### Summary

The **oldest well-preserved illustrated biblical manuscript** — a Greek book of **Genesis** painted on **purple-dyed vellum** with silver ink, made in a Byzantine workshop for an elite patron.

- **Historical Context.** Produced in the **early 6th century** C.E., likely in **Constantinople** or Syria. Christianity dominated Byzantine art; literacy remained an elite privilege, so illustration was a vital way to communicate scripture to the broader lay audience.
- **Function.** An **illuminated codex** owned by an aristocrat or high church official, where rich pictures carried the narrative for less-literate viewers and declared the book's sanctity through costly materials.
- **Content.** Two scenes from Genesis: **Rebecca meets Eliezer at the well**, where she is chosen as Isaac's wife after offering water to his camels; and **Jacob wrestling the angel** at the ford of the Jabbok, where he is renamed Israel. Both use **continuous narrative** — the same figures appear several times in one frame as the story unfolds.
- **Form.** **Tempera, gold, and silver** on **purple-dyed vellum** (a color associated with imperial authority). Classical painterly techniques survive — **cast shadows, foreshortening, perspective** — alongside a beginning of the flatter Byzantine hieratic style. A silver line marks the bridge/pathway guiding the eye.

**Vocabulary:** vellum · Genesis · codex
