## #14 — Statues of Votive Figures, from the Square Temple at Eshnunna

**Sumerian** · c. 2700 B.C.E. · Gypsum inlaid with shell and black limestone · modern Tell Asmar, Iraq · ~11 5/8 in (largest male)

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Standing_male_worshiper_MET_DT850.jpg/250px-Standing_male_worshiper_MET_DT850.jpg" width="240" alt="Votive Figures from Tell Asmar">

### Summary

Twelve small Sumerian figures with **oversized inlaid eyes** — eternal stand-ins for their patrons, meant to pray forever in a god's presence.

- **Historical Context.** Found buried beneath the floor of a temple (possibly dedicated to the god **Abu**) at ancient **Eshnunna** (Tell Asmar, Iraq). Access to Sumerian shrines was restricted; elites likely commissioned these statuettes to place themselves permanently before the god.
- **Function.** **Votive substitutes**: the figures embody their patrons and offer perpetual prayer. Some hold small cups or sprigs of vegetation for libation.
- **Content.** Men in **belts and fringed skirts**, some bald, some bearded; women in long robes, varied hair. All stand **erect, hands clasped**, staring upward with wide eyes fixed on the cult image.
- **Form.** Geometric **gypsum** bodies — flattened torso, cylindrical lower body — carved for a **frontal view**. **Inlaid shell and black limestone eyes** with painted hair exaggerate the gaze.

**Vocabulary:** votive · inlay · incising · subtractive sculpture
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.4
