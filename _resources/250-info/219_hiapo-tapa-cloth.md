## #219 — Hiapo (Tapa)

**Polynesia (Niue)** · 1850–1900 C.E. · Tapa cloth (beaten and decorated mulberry bark) · Te Papa, New Zealand · 64.5 × 129 cm

### Summary

A densely patterned **Niuean barkcloth** — a cross-Polynesian textile tradition made by collaborating women and saturated with mana.

- **Historical Context.** Tapa (*hiapo* in Niuean) is made throughout Polynesia, each island developing distinctive motifs. Production is a communal **women's art**: bark of the paper mulberry is stripped, soaked, and beaten with wooden mallets into thin sheets, then painted with vegetal dyes. Niuean hiapo of this period show European contact — the introduction of the botanical drawing aesthetic by missionaries led to fine-line, naturalistic leaves and labeled names being integrated into traditional geometric grids.
- **Function.** Ceremonial gift exchange at weddings, funerals, and diplomatic presentations; bedding, clothing, and wall hangings. Tapa carried **mana** and signaled wealth and prestige.
- **Content.** Dense geometric bands and grids of triangles, circles, spirals, and concentric shapes; occasional human figures, plants, or written names mark Niuean interaction with missionaries. Fine-line drawing and decreasing-scale motifs are distinctive to Niue.
- **Form.** Beaten mulberry bark (*uto*) layered and felted into a malleable sheet. Hand-painted with mineral and vegetable dyes. Very thin, flexible, warm.

**Vocabulary:** dye · tapa · Niue · mana · mulberry
