## #75 — Sistine Chapel ceiling and altar wall frescoes

**Michelangelo** · Ceiling: c. 1508–1512 C.E.; Altar: c. 1536–1541 C.E. · Fresco · Vatican City · Ceiling 133′ × 46′; Altar wall 45′ × 40′

### Summary

The definitive **High Renaissance** monument, followed decades later by a darker, **proto-Mannerist** Last Judgment.

- **Historical Context.** **Pope Julius II** commissioned Michelangelo in 1508 to replace the ceiling's star-spangled decoration after cracking from adjacent excavations. Michelangelo, already a sculptor by training, persuaded the Pope to paint Old Testament scenes instead of the twelve apostles originally requested. The altar wall's *Last Judgment* was painted a generation later under Paul III, amid the **Protestant Reformation** and Counter-Reformation anxieties.
- **Function.** The Sistine Chapel hosts **papal conclaves**; the frescoes amplify the sacred authority of the space through biblical narrative and divine imagery.
- **Content.** The ceiling runs nine Genesis scenes (Separation of Light from Darkness → Drunkenness of Noah) flanked by prophets, sibyls, and *ignudi*. The altar wall's *Last Judgment* shows Christ at center making his verdict, the damned hauled to hell by **Charon** (after Dante) toward **Minos**.
- **Form.** Pared-down "**essential figures**" grow larger in later panels (e.g., Creation of Adam); the *ignudi* display **sprezzatura** — contorted yet at ease. Vivid color; muscular, sculptural anatomy. The altar wall shifts to a denser, more agitated, darker palette anticipating Mannerism.

**Vocabulary:** essential figures · fresco · sprezzatura · ignudi
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.1, 3.2
