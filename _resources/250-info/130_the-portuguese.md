## #130 — The Portuguese

**Georges Braque** · 1911 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Kunstmuseum Basel, Switzerland · 116.7 × 81.5 cm

### Summary

A keystone work of **Analytic Cubism**, made at the height of Braque's collaboration with Picasso.

- **Historical Context.** Painted in the summer of 1911 in **Céret**, Catalonia, during the intense Braque–Picasso partnership that produced **Analytic Cubism**. Braque built on **Cézanne**'s geometric reduction of form and pushed it further, deconstructing the object into faceted planes. The style paralleled the upheaval in physics from Newton to Einstein and Heisenberg.
- **Function.** A radical departure from Renaissance pictorial illusionism: instead of describing reality, the painting analyzes it — dissolving a single subject into multiple viewpoints, introducing stenciled letters and numbers as shards of reality inside the abstracted form.
- **Content.** A Portuguese musician Braque had seen in a Marseilles bar, rendered almost unrecognizably. Shapes replace forms; stenciled words and numbers float across the surface; the only clearly legible subject is the vague outline of a figure.
- **Form.** Reduced, near-**monochrome** palette of warm browns. Light and shadow are detached from any consistent source. Multiple viewpoints coexist on one plane — cylinders, cubes, and triangles dominate — and the canvas hovers between flatness and three-dimensional structure.

**Vocabulary:** Cubism · Analytical Cubism · multiple viewpoints · monochrome

### Compare-and-contrast exercise (LO 3.5)

#### Goldfish (Henri Matisse, 1912) — *in image set as #131*

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Goldfish_Matisse.jpg/250px-Goldfish_Matisse.jpg" width="180" alt="Goldfish">

**Prompt:** How was Braque's *The Portuguese* and the Cubist movement influenced by previous art movements?

> The primary influences on this work of art are Fauvism and the works of Paul Cezanne. Fauvism started the break from Impressionism and post-Impressionism by emphasizing painterly qualities and color over the last traces of representation and realism. For example, the use of wild brush strokes and striking colors would not have been employed inside *The Portuguese* if Matisse had not employed the style in works like *Goldfish*. Cezanne introduced the idea of using geometry to represent the form of the object. In his *Mont Sainte-Victoire*, he depicted the form of the mountain over an actual, realistic depiction of a mountain. The obvious geometrical intent gives way to abstraction, an idea completed by the works of Braque and Picasso.
