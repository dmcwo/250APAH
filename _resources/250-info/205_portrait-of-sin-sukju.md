## #205 — Portrait of Sin Sukju

**Korean (Joseon Dynasty)** · 15th century C.E. · Hanging scroll, ink and color on silk · Cheongwon, Korea · 167 × 109.5 cm

### Summary

A formal court portrait of a **Joseon meritorious subject**, honoring the scholar-statesman Sin Sukju for his loyalty to the king.

- **Historical Context.** **Sin Sukju** (1417–1475) was a Joseon scholar and politician who rose to the post of chief state councillor (prime minister). Portraits of meritorious subjects (*gongsin*) were painted as official rewards, preserved by descendants, and displayed in memorial halls. Joseon painters prized exacting physiognomic likeness — the face was thought to reveal inner character. Western modeling techniques entered Korean portraiture only in the 18th century via Jesuit intermediaries.
- **Function.** A state-sponsored **honor portrait** commemorating Sin Sukju's loyalty; it celebrated the sitter while reinforcing Confucian values of service and propriety.
- **Content.** Sin Sukju is shown three-quarter view, seated stiffly in a red chair, hands folded in his sleeves. He wears the green and dark blue robes of a civil official with an embroidered **rank badge** at the chest. His gaze meets the viewer; his features, including age lines, are rendered with unblinking precision.
- **Form.** Ink and mineral color on silk. Flat, decorative treatment of drapery contrasts with the finely modeled face. Angular folds of the robes; pure yellow background. No depth or atmospheric perspective.

**Vocabulary:** hanging scroll · hwaji · Joseon dynasty
