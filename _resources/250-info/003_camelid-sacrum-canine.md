## #3 — Camelid Sacrum in the Shape of a Canine

**Prehistoric Mesoamerican (unknown culture)** · c. 14,000–7,000 B.C.E. · Carved camelid sacrum bone · National Museum of Anthropology, Mexico City (found at Tequixquiac, Mexico) · ~6.8 × 6.7 in

### Summary

**One of the earliest known figurative artworks in the Americas**, reshaping a fossil bone to suggest a canine head.

- **Historical Context.** Unearthed in **1870 at Tequixquiac, Mexico**, during drainage works some 40 ft deep; the bone is the sacrum of an extinct camelid. No controlled archaeological context survives, but the object is among the oldest artworks yet recovered in the region. Later Mesoamerican cultures treated the sacrum as a "**sacred bone**" or "second skull," and the name itself derives from Latin *os sacrum*.
- **Function.** Unknown. Speculative purposes include ritual worship of animal spirits or use as a portable mask connecting the sacrum ("second skull") to the head. Possibly tied to hunting ritual.
- **Content.** A canine or other mammal head, rendered through the bone's natural paired hollows (read as eyes) plus humanly carved nostril holes and snout grooves.
- **Form.** **Subtractive sculpture in the round** in fossilized bone, no added pigment. The roughly triangular form is compact and symmetrical; the sculptor's minimal intervention exploits the sacrum's natural skull-like cavities.

**Vocabulary:** sacrum · camelid · prehistoric
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.3, 1.4
