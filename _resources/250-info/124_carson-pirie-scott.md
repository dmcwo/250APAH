## #124 — Carson, Pirie, Scott and Company Building

**Louis Sullivan (architect), Daniel Burnham** · 1899–1903 C.E. · Iron, steel, glass, and terra cotta · Chicago, Illinois · 30,490 ft² area, 207 ft tall

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Carson%2C_Pirie%2C_Scott_and_Company_Building_%28Sullivan_Center%29%2C_Chicago%2C_Illinois_%289179422705%29.jpg/330px-Carson%2C_Pirie%2C_Scott_and_Company_Building_%28Sullivan_Center%29%2C_Chicago%2C_Illinois_%289179422705%29.jpg" width="240" alt="Carson, Pirie, Scott and Company Building">

### Summary

An early Chicago skyscraper, originally the **Sullivan Center**, that embodies Sullivan's dictum "form ever follows function."

- **Historical Context.** The building was created after the **Chicago Great Fire of 1871**. In 1902, Bavarian partners David Mayer and Leopold Schlesinger settled on a twelve-story structure at State and Madison. It stood out for its **steel frame**, large windows, and terra cotta and bronze cladding — materials that made it effectively fire resistant. A multi-year renovation completed in 2006 at $68.9 million, and parent company Bon-Ton Stores closed the department store after the 2006 Christmas season; the building has since been leased in portions while remaining a landmark piece of architecture.
- **Function.** Multi-functional: described as the "ideal **tripartite skyscraper**." The first floor — bright, open, with easy public access — serves commerce; the upper floors are office space with uniform, repetitive windows. The ground floor is a **department store** with large windows and broad entrances intended to draw shoppers in. Height is heavily emphasized, and a distinctive **cornice** marks the top to set the building apart.
- **Content.** An innovative modern commercial building blending work and commerce. Sullivan based the design on his principle that a building's exterior must reflect the social purpose of the space: a light, accessible base level for retail; uniform upper floors for offices; and a cornice to give the structure individuality.
- **Form.** Composed of iron, steel, glass, bronze, and terra cotta. Glass is used throughout, emphasized on the ground floor. Iron gives the structure its rigidity, steel frames the whole, and bronze and terra cotta — sturdy, lightweight, fire-resistant — handle the decorative skin.
