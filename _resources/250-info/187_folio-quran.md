## #187 — Folio from a Qur'an

**Arab, Abbasid Caliphate** · 8th–9th century C.E. · Ink and gold on parchment · probably modern-day Iraq · 230 × 320 mm

### Summary

An early **Kufic-script Qur'an folio** — calligraphy as the highest Islamic art.

- **Historical Context.** One of the earliest surviving Qur'ans, produced in the **Abbasid Caliphate**. The Abbasids formalized Arabic into a standardized **Kufic script** to ensure that the sacred text could be read consistently across a geographically vast Muslim world. Because Islam prohibits figural imagery in religious contexts, **calligraphy** became the highest and most prestigious form of Islamic art.
- **Function.** A page from the holy book, used in pious recitation and as a display of wealth in elite homes. The format — wider than it is tall, with generous spacing and few lines per page — makes it easy to read at a distance and distinguishes it from more modest copies.
- **Content.** The beginning of a sura (here *Al-Ankabut*), introduced by an elaborate gold **sura heading**. Fewer than nine lines per page signal a wealthy patron. Arabic reads right-to-left, accounting for the landscape format.
- **Form.** **Kufic script** in dark ink on vellum; vowels marked by small red diacritical **dots** (a recent innovation at the time to prevent misreading). Consonants rendered in thick, stately lines. Spacing and layout mathematically planned before inking — spare, geometric, monumental.

**Vocabulary:** Islam · calligraphy · Qur'an · Kufic · recitation
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.1
