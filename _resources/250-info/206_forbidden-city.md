## #206 — Forbidden City

**Chinese (Ming Dynasty, commissioned by the Yongle Emperor)** · 1406–1420 C.E. · Stone masonry, marble, brick, wood, ceramic tile · Beijing, China · 178 acres (~720,000 m²)

### Summary

The imperial palace city of **Ming and Qing China** — a vast axial complex aligned to cosmic order, forbidden to commoners for nearly 500 years.

- **Historical Context.** After overthrowing the Mongol Yuan dynasty, the Ming restored Han Chinese rule; the **Yongle Emperor** moved the capital from Nanjing to Beijing and built the Forbidden City (1406–1420). It housed 24 emperors through the Ming and Qing dynasties until 1912. Construction mobilized roughly a million laborers and consumed vast quantities of Phoebe zhennan nanmu wood and specially quarried "golden bricks."
- **Function.** **Administrative center** and imperial residence. The outer court held ceremonial halls for state ritual and reception; the inner court was the private domain of the emperor, his family, and eunuch staff.
- **Content.** Roughly 980 buildings arranged on a strict north–south axis with the Meridian Gate (south) and Hall of Supreme Harmony at its heart. Yellow-glazed roof tiles (reserved for the emperor) and marble balustrades dominate. Dragon iconography symbolizes imperial divinity; pairs of guardian lions flank thresholds.
- **Form.** Post-and-beam timber architecture on raised stone platforms; hierarchical scaling of courtyards and halls; strict bilateral symmetry. Largest structure is the Hall of Supreme Harmony, the tallest in Beijing at the time.

**Vocabulary:** Forbidden · Ming Dynasty · eunuch
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.1, 3.3
