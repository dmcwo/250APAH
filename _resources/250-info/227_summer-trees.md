## #227 — Summer Trees

**Song Su-nam (South Korea)** · 1979 C.E. · Ink on paper · Korea · ~2 ft 1⅝ in tall

### Summary

A **contemporary Korean ink painting** that revives traditional East Asian brushwork as an assertion of cultural identity against 20th-century Westernization.

- **Historical Context.** Painted by **Song Su-nam** (1938–2013) during the height of South Korea's industrialization and political tension between globalized Western art and homegrown traditions. Song led the **Sumukwha** (Oriental Ink) movement in 1980s Korea, advocating for a revival of East Asian ink painting informed by modern abstraction and Zen-influenced spirituality.
- **Function.** **Aesthetic and nationalist**: to reassert the expressive power of the ink brush as an uniquely East Asian medium at a moment when Korean artists were expected to adopt Western idioms.
- **Content.** Vertical bands of varying ink density suggest a stand of trees in summer — without depicting specific trunks, branches, or leaves. A minimalist landscape in the tradition of *literati* painting, pared to essentials.
- **Form.** Grayscale tonal variation produced by layering wet ink on wet paper (bleeding, feathery edges) and wet ink on dry paper (crisp, spotted brush marks). Velvety blacks descend into silvery grays; no color; vertical format.

**Vocabulary:** abstraction · contemporary · Sumukwha / Oriental Ink Movement
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.2
