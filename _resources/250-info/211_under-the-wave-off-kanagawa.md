## #211 — Under the Wave off Kanagawa (The Great Wave), from *Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji*

**Katsushika Hokusai (Japan, Edo period)** · 1830–1833 C.E. · Polychrome woodblock print; ink and color on paper · Japan · 10⅛ × 14¹⁵⁄₁₆ in

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Tsunami_by_hokusai_19th_century.jpg/500px-Tsunami_by_hokusai_19th_century.jpg" width="240" alt="Under the Wave off Kanagawa">

### Summary

Japan's most iconic print — Hokusai's cresting wave over fishing boats, with Mount Fuji diminished in the distance.

- **Historical Context.** Made by **Katsushika Hokusai** (1760–1849) in his seventies as part of *Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji*. Despite Edo-period isolationism, Dutch trade brought **Prussian blue** pigment and Western linear perspective to Japan; both are key to this print. After Perry forced Japan open in the 1850s, these prints flooded Europe and sparked **Japonisme**, influencing Van Gogh, Whistler, Monet, and Debussy.
- **Function.** An inexpensive, mass-produced **ukiyo-e** ("floating world") print sold as a domestic tourist souvenir — feeding popular interest in pilgrimages to sacred Mount Fuji.
- **Content.** A colossal wave with claw-like crest looms over three **oshiokuri-bune** fishing boats; in the distance, under the wave's trough, tiny snow-capped **Mount Fuji** sits low on the horizon. The composition juxtaposes nature's overwhelming force with the serene eternity of the mountain.
- **Form.** Woodblock print with separate blocks for each color, all outlined by a final black key block. Hokusai exploits the new synthetic **Prussian blue** for the deep sea and sky. Western-style low horizon and diminishing perspective; native flattening of space and bold outline. The wave's foam spray mirrors the snow on Fuji.

**Vocabulary:** Japanese woodblock · Japonisme · polychrome · Prussian blue · Ukiyo-e
