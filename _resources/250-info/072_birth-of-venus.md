## #72 — Birth of Venus

**Sandro Botticelli** · c. 1484–1486 C.E. · Tempera on canvas · Uffizi, Florence · 172.5 × 278.5 cm

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Sandro_Botticelli_-_La_nascita_di_Venere_-_Google_Art_Project_-_edited.jpg/500px-Sandro_Botticelli_-_La_nascita_di_Venere_-_Google_Art_Project_-_edited.jpg" width="240" alt="Birth of Venus">

### Summary

The **earliest surviving large-scale painting of a nude mythological female** since antiquity.

- **Historical Context.** Painted in **Florence** during the early Renaissance, almost certainly commissioned by a member of the **Medici** family (likely Lorenzo di Pierfrancesco). Botticelli drew on Homer's Homeric Hymn describing Venus born of sea foam, on Poliziano's poetry, and on the ancient **Aphrodite of Knidos** (modest pose). Canvas was increasingly favored over panel in the humid conditions of Medici country villas.
- **Function.** Likely a decorative piece for the Medici's **Villa di Castello**, perhaps a wedding gift; Venus signaled love, fertility, and civic virtue.
- **Content.** Venus stands nude on a shell, drifting to shore; at left, the wind gods Zephyr and Chloris/Aura blow her in; at right, a nymph (Hora of Spring) waits with a flower-strewn cloak. Her pose echoes the *Venus Pudica* gesture.
- **Form.** Tempera gives the flesh a translucent glow. The composition is strikingly **flat**: gold-outlined hair, stylized V-shaped waves, figures on a single plane, and heavy linear patterning take precedence over depth. Contrapposto stance; idealized, elongated body.

**Vocabulary:** tempera · Renaissance · nymph · contrapposto · Venus Pudica
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.4, 3.5

### Connections to other works in the image set

#### → #80 — Venus of Urbino (Titian, c. 1538)

**Theme:** Marital duties, including motherhood

> Both paintings depict Venus at its center as a symbol of love, and in the case of the Venus of Urbino, sensuality. The beauty of Venus is evident in both paintings, and is used to convey messages, albeit of differing themes. The paintings also fulfilled more private uses, rather than for public display and grandeur, and were likely hung in the homes of the patrons.

---

#### → #34 — Doryphoros (Spear Bearer) (Polykleitos, c. 450–440 B.C.E.)

**Theme:** To display a canon of proportions of idealized form, beauty, and athleticism.

> Both pieces of art are full-length nudes, although the spear-bearer is a male. Additionally, Doryphoros represents an idealized male and human form of the time, just as Venus in the Birth of Venus is idealized, her face free of any blemishes and her golden hair long and flowing. The Doryphoros also displays a contrapposto stance, one that Venus also adopts.

#### → #35 — Acropolis (Iktinos and Kallikrates, 447–410 B.C.E.)

**Theme:** East Pediment depicts the birth of Athena

> Both the pediment and the painting depict the birth of goddesses and their origins, and each goddess is welcomed by those surrounding them. The statues of the East Pediment are also in one plane and display an essentially flat scene, although the statue itself is three dimensional, much like the Birth of Venus.
