## #51 — San Vitale

**Early Byzantine Europe** · c. 526–547 C.E. · Brick, marble, and stone veneer; mosaic · Ravenna, Italy · ~60 × 18 m

### Summary

A **centrally planned** Byzantine church in Ravenna whose sanctuary walls are lined with the most vivid Byzantine mosaics in the West — the imperial panels of **Justinian and Theodora**.

- **Historical Context.** Sponsored by the banker **Julianus Argentarius** and completed under **Justinian** after the Byzantine reconquest of Ravenna from the Ostrogoths. Celebrates Justinian's authority in both church and state and asserts orthodoxy after Arian rule.
- **Function.** Originally a **martyrium of Saint Vitalis**, and a congregational church. Used to proclaim the unity of imperial and religious power: Justinian and Theodora are shown making liturgical offerings.
- **Content.** Biblical and imperial **mosaics**: Christ as cosmic ruler in the apse; Old Testament scenes (Abraham, Melchizedek) on the sanctuary walls; and the famous twin panels of **Justinian with his attendants** (left) and **Theodora with hers** (right), both **haloed**, presenting the **paten and chalice** for the Eucharist.
- **Form.** **Octagonal centrally-planned** church with an **ambulatory** wrapping a domed core. Layered arches and connecting columns create weightless interior; **tesserae** in gold and rich color flatten figures and float them against gold backgrounds. Justinian in royal **purple**; frontal, symmetrical, hieratic.

**Vocabulary:** centrally-planned · ambulatory · spolia · mosaic · martyrium · paten · Eucharist · tesserae
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.2
