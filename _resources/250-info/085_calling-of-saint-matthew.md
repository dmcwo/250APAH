## #85 — Calling of Saint Matthew

**Caravaggio** · c. 1597–1601 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Contarelli Chapel, San Luigi dei Francesi, Rome · 322 × 340 cm

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/The_Calling_of_Saint_Matthew-Caravaggo_%281599-1600%29.jpg/500px-The_Calling_of_Saint_Matthew-Caravaggo_%281599-1600%29.jpg" width="240" alt="The Calling of Saint Matthew">

### Summary

A defining **Italian Baroque** painting whose diagonal shaft of light bears the weight of the miracle.

- **Historical Context.** Caravaggio's first major public commission, secured by his patron **Cardinal Francesco del Monte** for the Contarelli Chapel in the French national church in Rome. Painted at the height of the Counter-Reformation, which pushed artists to create emotionally direct, accessible images.
- **Function.** One of three paintings on the life of Matthew for the chapel. Meant to evangelize ordinary viewers by placing a gospel moment in a recognizable tavern setting.
- **Content.** Four finely dressed figures count money at a table; Levi the tax collector (probably the bearded man at center) points at himself — "who, me?" — as Christ and Peter, in biblical dress, enter from the right. A diagonal **shaft of light** mirrors Christ's outstretched hand, whose pose echoes the finger of God in Michelangelo's Sistine Chapel.
- **Form.** Hallmark Caravaggesque **tenebrism** / chiaroscuro: a raking light from upper right cuts across a dark interior. Contemporary 16th-century clothing vs. Christ's timeless robes collapses sacred and everyday. Figures are life-size, anatomically truthful, and psychologically specific.

**Vocabulary:** Baroque · chiaroscuro · tenebrism · religious work
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 3.2, 3.3
