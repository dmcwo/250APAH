## #168 — Great Mosque of Djenné

**Mali** · Founded c. 1200 C.E.; rebuilt 1906–1907 · Adobe · Djenné, Mali

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Djenne_great_mud_mosque.jpg/250px-Djenne_great_mud_mosque.jpg" width="240" alt="Great Mosque of Djenné">

### Summary

The largest **adobe (mud-brick)** building in the world and the centerpiece of West African **Sudano-Sahelian** architecture.

- **Historical Context.** Djenné was a major city of the Mali Empire and a stop on the trans-Saharan trade routes. The original mosque was founded around 1200 C.E.; it was rebuilt in its present form in 1906–1907 under French colonial rule using traditional techniques. Only Muslims may enter the mosque; non-Muslims are excluded.
- **Function.** The central mosque of Djenné and historically a center of Islamic learning where students from across West Africa studied the **Quran** during the Middle Ages. Also a community gathering place.
- **Content.** Three tall minaret-towers on the qibla (east) wall; the central tower houses the **mihrab** — the niche marking the direction of Mecca. **Ostrich eggs** top the pillars as symbols of fertility and purity. Annual community plastering festivals renew the mud surface.
- **Form.** **Adobe** (sun-dried mud brick) coated with mud plaster. Protruding wooden beams called **torons** serve both as decorative rhythmic elements and as permanent scaffolding for annual replastering. Massive walls taper and are buttressed against heat and seasonal rains.

**Vocabulary:** adobe · mihrab · toron · Sudano-Sahelian
