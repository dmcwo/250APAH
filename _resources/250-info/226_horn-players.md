## #226 — Horn Players

**Jean-Michel Basquiat (USA)** · 1983 C.E. · Acrylic and oil paintstick on canvas panels (triptych) · The Broad, Los Angeles · 96 × 75 in

### Summary

A **Neo-Expressionist triptych** honoring bebop pioneers Charlie Parker and Dizzy Gillespie — and a model of Basquiat's graffiti-infused painting practice.

- **Historical Context.** **Jean-Michel Basquiat** (1960–1988), born in Brooklyn to Puerto Rican and Haitian parents, rose from late-1970s New York street art — tagging sardonic poetry as "**SAMO©**" — to gallery stardom in the early 1980s. He was painted into Andy Warhol's Factory circle. A self-taught painter, Basquiat fused Abstract Expressionism's gestural energy with graffiti, Black cultural history, and medical/anatomical imagery. Died at 27 of an overdose.
- **Function.** An **homage** to Black musicians whose music shaped Basquiat; also a manifesto for a distinctly Black avant-garde, uniting "high" art with street idiom.
- **Content.** Left panel depicts **Charlie Parker** ("Bird") with saxophone; right panel, **Dizzy Gillespie** with his signature bent trumpet. Scrawled text scatters across all three panels: "**ORNITHOLOGY**" (Parker's famous tune and his nickname), **"DIZZY GILLESPIE,"** "Chan" and "Pree" (Parker's wife and daughter), the abstract word "SOAP"; anatomical heads. Words crossed out to make them "more visible."
- **Form.** Bold black ground; figures rendered in distinct blocks of unmodeled color (especially Parker's face). Oil-paintstick and acrylic. Improvisational, layered, graffiti-like — mirroring bebop's velocity and freedom.

**Vocabulary:** Abstract Expressionism · Neo-Expressionism · bebop · triptych
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.1

**Theme:** Heavy Focus on Improvisation, plus the Mirroring of Music in the Artwork.

> Both pieces were created via improvisation, with little attention paid to the deeper meaning of what's being painted. In Kandinsky's case, his paintings were intended to reflect the sounds of a symphony, while in the case of Horn Players, it's likely that Basquiat intended for the art to mirror Bebop music/Free Jazz, which is intentionally unstructured and reliant on improvisation.
