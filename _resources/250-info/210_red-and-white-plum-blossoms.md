## #210 — Red and White Plum Blossoms

**Ogata Kōrin (Japan, Edo period)** · Early 18th century C.E. · Ink, color, and gold leaf on paper (pair of two-fold byōbu screens) · MOA Museum of Art, Atami, Japan · 156 × 172.2 cm each

### Summary

A pair of gold-ground **Rinpa folding screens** by Ogata Kōrin — the canonical expression of Japanese decorative naturalism.

- **Historical Context.** Painted by **Ogata Kōrin** (1658–1716) during the peace and prosperity of the Edo period. Kōrin, born to a wealthy Kyoto textile family, inherited and systematized the decorative tradition of Hon'ami Kōetsu and Tawaraya Sōtatsu; the style is named **Rinpa** after his name. His works defined a visual language used later by his brother Kenzan, Sakai Hōitsu, and into the 20th century.
- **Function.** **Byōbu** (folding screens) were luxury furnishings in elite homes — dividing interior space, blocking drafts, and displaying wealth. Often brought out seasonally; plum blossoms signal late winter into early spring.
- **Content.** Two aged plum trees stand on the outer edges: red blossoms at right, white at left. Between them, a stylized black stream swirls with silver patterns of eddying water. Gold-leaf ground flattens space and abstracts the scene to its essentials.
- **Form.** Signature Rinpa techniques: **tarashikomi** (dripping fresh ink/color into still-wet pigment to create mottled textures in the tree trunks), **mokkotsu** ("boneless" painting without outlines), and the use of gold and silver leaf. Bold two-panel composition unified by the central stream.

**Vocabulary:** tarashikomi · rinpa · mokkotsu · byōbu · naturalism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.2, 3.2, 3.5

### Connections to other works in the image set

#### → #34 — Doryphoros (Spear Bearer) (Polykleitos, original c. 450–440 B.C.E.; Roman copy)

**Theme:** Natural World

> Compared to older Greek works, *Doryphoros (Spear Bearer)* (it's a Roman work) represents a more natural position of the body. Techniques such as contrapposto helped achieve such natural-looking positions. Like the *Doyphoros (Spear Bearer)*, *Red and White Plum Blossoms* also reaches a level of naturalism that is unrivaled by previous works of its culture. Though not perfect in representing nature, the *Red and White Plum Blossoms* displayed a considerably realistic depiction of flowers and a river, similarly to how *Doryphoros (Spear Bearer)* portrayed the ideal human body doing everyday activities.

---

#### → #83 — Hunters in the Snow (Pieter Bruegel the Elder, 1565)

**Theme:** Natural World

> In both *Red and White Plum Blossoms* and *Hunters in the Snow*, a diagonal composition (to some extend for Ogata's work) draws the viewer's eyes to a certain point on the artwork. Both also use the color white in their works. *Hunters in the Snow*, however, depicts normal life (when put in context, it represents the dream lifestyle of the people during the time), and *Red and White Plum Blossom* represented connection with nature. Not all rinpa works depict nature; they also often presented daily lifestyle activities in the compositions.

---

#### → #201 — Travelers Among Mountains and Streams (Fan Kuan, Northern Song China, c. 1000 C.E.)

**Theme:** Natural World

> Both of these works of art capture a scene of nature, symbolizing the bond humans have with nature and how we should respect it. *Red and White Plum Blossoms* differ in that it uses vibrant and contrasting colors in it. In *Travelers Among Mountains and Streams*, the work is very subdued, filled with detail, minimalistic, and lacking color. Rather than sticking to Chinese tradition, the Japanese took Chinese art and added on it to link it with their culture.
