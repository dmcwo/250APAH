## #7 — Jade Cong

**Liangzhu culture (Neolithic China)** · c. 3,300–2,200 B.C.E. · Carved nephrite jade · Liangzhu, China · ~10 × 2.75 in

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Jade_Cong_King%2C_2018-06-09_01.jpg/250px-Jade_Cong_King%2C_2018-06-09_01.jpg" width="240" alt="Jade Cong">

### Summary

A **Liangzhu jade ritual object** — square outside, round inside — central to Neolithic Chinese burial practice.

- **Historical Context.** Produced by the **Liangzhu culture** of the lower Yangtze region during the late Neolithic. Neolithic Chinese practiced **ancestor veneration**, burying the dead with jades; jade was already seen as the epitome of beauty and virtue, a status Confucius would later codify. Patterns on congs parallel those on jades found elsewhere across Central Asia, reflecting river-network exchange.
- **Function.** **Funerary and ritual.** Jade congs were buried in large numbers, arranged around the body (often the best pieces near the chest and stomach), presumably to aid or accompany the soul into the afterlife.
- **Content.** Stylized **face patterns** combining human-like and beast-like features — likely spirits or deities in the Liangzhu animist / pagan religious system, later absorbed into Buddhist, Taoist, and Confucian traditions.
- **Form.** Three-dimensional carving in **nephrite jade** (the harder, more durable of the two true jades), ranging from cream to green depending on iron content. A characteristic **square outer profile around a circular inner tube** — a form read later as symbolizing earth (square) and heaven (round circle).

**Vocabulary:** bi · cong · nephrite
