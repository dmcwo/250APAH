## #47 — Ludovisi Battle Sarcophagus

**Late Imperial Roman** · c. 250–260 C.E. · Proconnesian marble · Rome, Italy · ~5 ft tall

### Summary

A high-relief Roman **battle sarcophagus** showing Romans overwhelming Goths in a writhing, claustrophobic tangle — late Empire funerary art shaped by the **Crisis of the Third Century**.

- **Historical Context.** Made during the **Crisis of the Third Century** (235–284 C.E.), when plague, civil war, and barbarian invasion nearly unraveled the Roman Empire. Depictions of military triumph in this period are partly propaganda against very real battlefield losses.
- **Function.** A **funerary monument** for a wealthy (unidentified) Roman military commander, probably the young horseman at the center. An **X** on his forehead signals devotion to **Mithraism**, a soldiers' mystery cult.
- **Content.** A mass of Roman soldiers tramples and impales **Goth** warriors, whose curly hair, helmetless heads, and anguished faces mark them as barbarians. At the center, the commander rides triumphantly, his victory asserted by the contrast of his composure against the chaos.
- **Form.** **High relief** in white marble. **Horror vacui** — the surface has almost no negative space. **Anti-classical**: expressive, pain-filled faces replace serene idealization; scale differences are smaller than typical hieratic scale but the overall composition still privileges the central horseman.

**Vocabulary:** anti-classical · sarcophagus · high relief · Crisis of the Third Century
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 3.3
