## #31 — Temple of Minerva (Veii) and sculpture of Apollo

**Etruscan; master sculptor Vulca** · c. 510–500 B.C.E. · Original temple of wood, mud brick, and tufa; terracotta sculpture · Veii, near Rome, Italy · Apollo of Veii ~5 ft 11 in

### Summary

An Etruscan temple (known mainly from foundations and literary accounts) whose roof once bore a row of painted terracotta statues, including the striding **Apollo of Veii** attributed to the master **Vulca**.

- **Historical Context.** The Etruscans developed temple architecture under Greek influence but adapted it for local materials; because the temple used **wood, mud brick, and tufa**, only foundations survive. Etruscan art is typically anonymous, but ancient sources attribute the temple sculptures to **Vulca** of Veii. The **Apollo of Veii** is the only near-complete survivor.
- **Function.** A place of **worship** modeled distantly on Greek practice but distinctly Etruscan in orientation: accessible only from a frontal staircase and set on a high podium. The rooftop sculptures re-enacted a **mythological scene** (Apollo and Hercules disputing the Ceryneian hind).
- **Content.** An Etruscan-plan temple: deep front porch, three cellas at the rear. The acroterion sculptures depicted a dynamic mythic confrontation — Apollo striding forward in lively motion, muscles straining, drapery rippling.
- **Form.** **Terracotta** sculpture is brightly painted, heavier and more stylized than Greek marble equivalents. **Tuscan columns** (unfluted, simple base and capital); 5:6 floor-plan proportions; decorative color. The **Archaic smile** and patterned hair show Greek influence, while the taut striding pose and rooftop placement are Etruscan inventions.

**Vocabulary:** terracotta · column · Etruscan · Tuscan column
