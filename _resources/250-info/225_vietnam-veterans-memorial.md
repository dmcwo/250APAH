## #225 — Vietnam Veterans Memorial

**Maya Lin (USA)** · 1982 C.E. · Black polished granite · Washington, D.C. · Two walls each 246′9″ long, max 10.1 ft high

### Summary

A V-shaped black granite **cut into the earth** inscribed with the names of over 58,000 American dead — a deliberately austere counter-monument by a 21-year-old undergraduate.

- **Historical Context.** Designed by **Maya Lin** as a 21-year-old Yale undergraduate, selected anonymously from 1,421 entries in a national competition. Deeply controversial on completion — critics, including some veterans, called it a "black gash of shame" for breaking with heroic monument traditions. A figurative sculpture (*Three Soldiers*, Frederick Hart, 1984) and a Women's Memorial were added nearby as compromises. The Wall rapidly became one of the most visited memorials in the U.S.
- **Function.** A commemorative monument listing the **58,000+ Americans killed or missing** in Vietnam (1959–1975), chronologically rather than alphabetically, allowing each name to reflect its moment. Visitors touch names, leave offerings, and see themselves reflected in the polished stone.
- **Content.** No figurative imagery. The walls meet at a 125° angle, one arm pointing to the Washington Monument, the other to the Lincoln Memorial — literally embedding the loss within the symbolic axis of the nation.
- **Form.** Minimalist: two tapering wings of polished **black granite** descend into a cut in the earth. Reflective surface doubles the viewer into the monument. Names engraved in Optima typeface.

**Vocabulary:** Minimalism · medium · architectural style
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 3.2
