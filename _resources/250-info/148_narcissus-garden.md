## #148 — Narcissus Garden

**Yayoi Kusama** (Japanese / New York avant-garde) · Original installation and performance 1966 · ~1,500 mirrored plastic balls · First exhibited at Venice Biennale (1966)

### Summary

An **immersive mirror installation** that implicates the viewer in their own narcissism.

- **Historical Context.** Kusama (b. Nagano, Japan, 1929) moved to **New York** in 1958 and joined the avant-garde scene, drawing on abstract expressionism. She first exhibited *Narcissus Garden* at the **33rd Venice Biennale in 1966**, where — in a gold kimono — she acted as a street peddler selling the mirror spheres individually for $2 until organizers shut her down. That performance doubled as self-promotion and as a protest against art's commodification.
- **Function.** To confront viewers with their own vanity. The 1,500 reflective spheres distort and multiply the viewer's image; in the age of smartphones, the piece now generates selfies and social-media posts — which has made it a commentary on the attention economy as well as on classical narcissism.
- **Content.** References the Greek myth of **Narcissus**, cursed by **Nemesis** to fall in love with his own reflection; the title and mirrored ball-field recreate his pond. The arrangement varies by site: Venice, Le Consortium, Central Park, the Jardin des Tuileries, the Rockaways.
- **Form.** Identical, uniform silver plastic spheres placed in organic groupings that respond to the host landscape — lake, field, ruined building — with walkable channels between groups. The spheres are simultaneously a single work, a performance, and a crowd of reflective selves.

**Vocabulary:** narcissism · Narcissus · installation · performance
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.4, 3.2, 3.3

**Theme:** prevalence of photography as harmful

> Both Elevating Photography to the Level of Art and Narcissus Garden offer commentary on the prevalence of photography in society. Elevating Photography to the Level of Art directly criticizes photography. Its message is that photography should not be considered art as it requires no skill to take a photo in contrast to actually painting or drawing. The selfie-encouraging nature of Narcissus Garden also reflects how photography has become commonplace and easy to do in today's society.

#### → #93 — Palace of Versailles (Louis Le Vau and Jules Hardouin-Mansart, begun 1669)

**Theme:** reflections, grandeur

> The Hall of Mirrors in the Palace of Versailles is similar to Narcissus Garden in that both involve the viewer in the artwork through his/her reflection. However, the Hall of Mirrors is elaborately decorated, while Narcissus Garden is more minimalist and spartan. In addition, the Hall of Mirrors had functional use: it was used as a passageway for Louis XVI and for family and court functions. In contrast, Narcissus Garden is an artwork which contemplates the issues of self-absorption and society's obsession with looks.
