## #153 — Chavín de Huántar

**Chavín culture** · 900–200 B.C.E. · Stone and granite (architectural complex and Lanzón); hammered gold alloy (jewelry) · Northern highlands, Peru · 3,150 ft elevation

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Chav%C3%ADn_de_Hu%C3%A1ntar_-_51149086708.jpg/250px-Chav%C3%ADn_de_Hu%C3%A1ntar_-_51149086708.jpg" width="240" alt="Chavín de Huántar">

### Summary

The **religious capital** of the pre-Incan **Chavín** culture in highland Peru.

- **Historical Context.** Located at high altitude in the Andes between the **Huachesca and Mosna rivers** — a location considered spiritually charged and a major site of **pilgrimage**. Construction began around 900 B.C.E. and continued in phases; the site is dominated by its **Old Temple** around which the later complex grew. Chavín art is famous for its **anthropomorphic** animal-human fusions.
- **Function.** A pilgrimage and religious center. The Old Temple's interior maze of tunnels (**galleries**) channels sound and air, likely to produce disorienting effects on visitors approaching the god.
- **Content.** Archaeological complex including the Old Temple, relief sculpture on a ruined stairway, and a gold nose ornament with serpent motifs. At the center of the Old Temple's maze stands the **Lanzón** — a 15-foot blade-shaped granite monolith carved in low relief with a composite jaguar-serpent-human deity.
- **Form.** Roughly dressed stone walls and floors, with smoother stones for interior decoration. Low-relief carvings are symmetrical and geometric. Materials (granite, gold alloy) were relatively high-quality for their region.

**Vocabulary:** Lanzón stone · galleries · anthropomorphism · pilgrimage
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.4, 2.2
