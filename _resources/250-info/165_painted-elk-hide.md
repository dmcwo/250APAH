## #165 — Painted elk hide

Attributed to **Cotsiogo (Cadzi Cody)**, Eastern Shoshone · c. 1890–1900 C.E. · Natural pigments on elk hide · Wind River Reservation, Wyoming · 81 × 78 in.

### Summary

An Eastern Shoshone **hide painting** made for tourist sale, depicting banned and permitted rituals together.

- **Historical Context.** **Cotsiogo** (Cadzi Cody, c. 1866–1912), Eastern Shoshone, was relocated with his tribe to the **Wind River Reservation** in Wyoming. There he produced hide paintings for sale to Euro-American tourists as a way to earn a living under the reservation system. Hide painting on the Plains was traditionally narrative — recording history — and men typically painted on tipis, shields, and robes.
- **Function.** Commercial art that doubled as cultural preservation. Cotsiogo depicted scenes tourists wanted: buffalo hunts, the **Sun Dance**, and the secular **Wolf Dance**. Because the U.S. had outlawed the Sun Dance (1935), Cotsiogo often blended the two dances in a single image so the religious ritual could still be shown.
- **Content.** Feathered and bustle-clad men circle a buffalo head between two poles — the Sun Dance to the creator. Around this central ritual, women gather by a fire, and men on horses hunt and butcher buffalo. The buffalo was sacred to Shoshone life.
- **Form.** Local red, yellow, brown, green, and blue pigments painted freehand on a dried, outstretched elk hide. Two-dimensional figures with **composite view** (torso frontal, head and legs in profile). Movement conveyed by spatial composition and the gallop of horses.

**Vocabulary:** figural motif · pigment · Sun Dance · Wolf Dance · composite view
