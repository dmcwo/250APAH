## #20 — Temple of Amun-Re and Hypostyle Hall

**New Kingdom Egypt, 18th–19th Dynasties** · Temple c. 1550 B.C.E.; Hall c. 1250 B.C.E. · Cut sandstone and mud brick · Karnak, Egypt · Complex ~60 acres; hypostyle hall 340 × 170 ft with 66-ft columns

### Summary

The colossal religious heart of **Karnak (Thebes)** — expanded over centuries by New Kingdom pharaohs including **Thutmose I and Rameses II** — centered on a **hypostyle hall** with 134 columns.

- **Historical Context.** Begun under **Senusret I** (Middle Kingdom) and continuously expanded during the New Kingdom, when Thebes was Egypt's capital. The composite god **Amun-Re** (fusing local Theban Amun with the sun god Re) rose to national prominence, and successive pharaohs built at Karnak to link themselves with the god and with past dynasties.
- **Function.** A **temple complex** considered Amun-Re's **earthly dwelling** — part religious center, part working estate for the priesthood, part royal burial site. Access grew more restricted toward the interior: only priests and the pharaoh entered the inner sanctuary.
- **Content.** Multiple pylons frame sequential courtyards leading to the **hypostyle hall** — a forest of columns whose taller central nave allows **clerestory lighting** — then the barque shrine and sanctuary housing the cult statue of Amun-Re. Obelisks, sphinxes, and painted relief narrate battles and divine myths.
- **Form.** **Post-and-lintel** sandstone construction. The hall's **lotus** and **papyrus** column capitals symbolize Upper and Lower Egypt; ground level rises and light dims as one moves inward — a physical metaphor for the approach to the divine. Two axes (N–S and E–W); an avenue of **sphinxes** once connected Karnak to Luxor.

**Vocabulary:** pylon · peristyle court · hypostyle hall · sanctuary · clerestory · hieroglyphs · colonnade
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.1, 3.2
