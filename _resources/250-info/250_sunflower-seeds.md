## #250 — Kui Hua Zi (Sunflower Seeds)

**Ai Weiwei (Chinese)** · 2010–2011 C.E. · Sculpted and painted porcelain · Installed in the Turbine Hall, Tate Modern, London · 100 million+ pieces; variable display

### Summary

**Over 100 million hand-painted porcelain sunflower seeds** carpeting the Tate's Turbine Hall — Ai Weiwei's mass-collaborative meditation on China, propaganda, and conformity.

- **Historical Context.** **Ai Weiwei** (b. 1957 in Beijing) is China's most prominent contemporary artist and political dissident. *Sunflower Seeds* was commissioned for the Tate Modern's Unilever Series and opened in 2010. Each seed was hand-sculpted and hand-painted by a team of roughly **1,600 artisans in Jingdezhen** — the imperial porcelain city, whose workshops had been decimated by mass production. The project revived traditional craft at scale and provided years of employment to Jingdezhen workers. Sunflower seeds were the **motif of Maoist propaganda** (Mao as sun, the people as sunflowers turning toward him); they are also shared casually among friends in everyday Chinese life.
- **Function.** Installation art meets **social project**. Originally visitors could walk on and touch the carpet of seeds; after silicosis concerns from porcelain dust, the Tate cordoned off the surface and required observation from the edges or a raised walkway.
- **Content.** A vast rectangle of seed-shaped porcelains. Each looks nearly identical from a distance but reveals individual handwork up close — each one distinct yet part of an overwhelming mass, a metaphor for Chinese citizens under a homogenizing state.
- **Form.** Hand-thrown porcelain forms, hand-painted black stripes, fired and distributed like grain. The installation itself is simple (a rectangle), but the **scale** and **collective labor** behind it are the point.

**Vocabulary:** political art · interactive art · hyper-realistic
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.2, 2.3
