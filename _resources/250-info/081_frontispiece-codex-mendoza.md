## #81 — Frontispiece of the Codex Mendoza

**Viceroyalty of New Spain** · c. 1541–1542 C.E. · Ink and color on paper · Bodleian Library, Oxford

### Summary

A **post-Conquest Aztec codex** that grafts indigenous iconography onto a European book format to transmit Mexica history to the Spanish crown.

- **Historical Context.** Commissioned around 1541 by Antonio de **Mendoza**, first Viceroy of New Spain, as a gift for Emperor **Charles V**. Native tlacuilos (scribes) used pre-conquest pictographic conventions while Spanish annotators added glosses in alphabetic script. The ship carrying it was seized by French privateers, and it ended up in England via André Thevet.
- **Function.** Ethnographic and political report: it details the founding of **Tenochtitlan**, the reigns of Aztec rulers, tribute lists, and daily life — equal parts history, census, and legitimizing document under Spanish rule.
- **Content.** The frontispiece shows the founding of Tenochtitlan: an **eagle perched on a cactus** at the center of a blue X-shaped canal system dividing the city into four quarters, framed by ten founding chieftains, turquoise fire-drill glyphs for each year of the founding cycle, and conquered cities at the bottom (Colhuacan, Tenayuca).
- **Form.** Bound like a European codex but rendered in Aztec style: flat color fields, glyphic place-names, stylized profile figures. The water-and-land glyph forms an X (*atl-tlachinolli*) at center; outer band shows 51 year glyphs.

**Vocabulary:** codex · tlacuilo · glyph · Tenochtitlan · viceroyalty
