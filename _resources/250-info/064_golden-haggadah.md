## #64 — Golden Haggadah (The Plagues of Egypt, Scenes of Liberation, and Preparation for Passover)

**Late medieval Spain (Jewish, likely Barcelona workshop)** · c. 1320 C.E. · Illuminated manuscript (pigments and gold leaf on vellum) · British Library, London

### Summary

A lavishly illustrated **Jewish Haggadah** — the liturgical book read at the **Passover seder** — whose gold-ground miniatures narrate the Exodus story with Gothic courtly elegance.

- **Historical Context.** Produced in **late-medieval Christian Spain** (likely Barcelona), where a significant Jewish population coexisted — often uneasily — with Catholic rulers before the expulsions of 1492. Although Jewish tradition discouraged figurative imagery in synagogue books, private devotional manuscripts like the Haggadah freely featured figural scenes.
- **Function.** A **Haggadah** is read aloud during the **Passover seder**, retelling the Israelites' liberation from Egyptian slavery. The Golden Haggadah was a wealthy family's deluxe copy, meant to be used at the family Passover table.
- **Content.** Fourteen full-page miniatures — four to a page, framed in Gothic architecture — narrate **Genesis and Exodus**: Adam naming the animals, the Ten Plagues, Pharaoh's pursuit, the parting of the Red Sea, and **preparations for Passover** (baking matzah, sacrificing lambs, cleaning houses of leaven).
- **Form.** **Pigment and gold leaf on vellum**. The gold-diapered backgrounds give the book its name. The figures are in the **Gothic** courtly style — elongated, elegant, with expressive gestures — showing the integration of Jewish content into the surrounding Christian artistic idiom.
