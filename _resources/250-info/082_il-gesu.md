## #82 — Il Gesù, including Triumph of the Name of Jesus ceiling fresco

**Giacomo della Porta (façade); Giovanni Battista Gaulli (fresco)** · Façade 1568–1584; fresco 1676–1679 · Brick, marble, fresco, stucco · Rome · 75 × 35 m

### Summary

The **mother church of the Jesuits**, whose façade template and illusionistic ceiling define the Italian **Baroque**.

- **Historical Context.** Conceived by **St. Ignatius of Loyola**, founder of the Society of Jesus, and bankrolled by Cardinal **Alessandro Farnese**. Church built during the Counter-Reformation to model a new preaching-focused architecture; Gaulli's ceiling fresco came a century later at the height of Roman high Baroque.
- **Function.** Principal Jesuit church; its plan and façade became the prototype for Jesuit churches worldwide (St. Michael, Munich; St. Ignatius, Buenos Aires). Designed to draw large audiences to hear sermons and participate in ornate liturgy — Counter-Reformation theatrics.
- **Content.** The **ceiling fresco** shows the sacred monogram **IHS** glorified in a burst of golden light as saints and angels ascend, while the damned (painted on stucco that spills over the frame) plummet into darkness below.
- **Form.** Della Porta's two-story façade has **six pairs of Corinthian pilasters** below, **four pairs** above, joined by scrolling volutes — a proto-Baroque departure from Renaissance classicism. Gaulli's ceiling exploits **trompe l'oeil** and sculpted stucco bursting through painted sky (*di sotto in sù*), abolishing the boundary between architecture and image.

**Vocabulary:** façade · Jesuits · trompe l'oeil · Baroque

### Connections to other works in the image set

#### → #89 — Ecstasy of Saint Teresa (Gian Lorenzo Bernini, c. 1647–1652)

**Theme:** Italian Baroque

> Both works are highly characteristic of the Baroque period, with rich decoration, and a very high degree of theatricality. The lighting in the church and the Ecstasy of St. Theresa both emphasize the drama of the situation, and relay great emotion and passion. Both also intend to connect emotionally with the audience, and the tension and feeling in both works is almost tangible. Both are also of religious subjects, and are intended to inspire greater devotion in the audience, so to counter the Reformation.

#### → #85 — Calling of Saint Matthew (Caravaggio, c. 1597–1601)

**Theme:** Mannerism

> Both works have a high emotion, and tension, though evoke different emotions, as Il Gesù inspires awe at the glory of God, the Calling of St. Mathew is infused with the tension of the scene as the tax collector, Mathew, as sinner, is questioning whether he has been chosen to follow Jesus. The Calling of St. Mathew thus acts to challenge the audience to see themselves as St. Mathew, while the Il Gesù ceiling fresco urges the viewers to triumph in the Sacred Name of Jesus. Both in style are similar due to the theatricality, and the emphasis of light in unusual ways (in the Calling of St. Mathew, the light shines not through not through the window, but rather mysteriously from the direction of the hand of Jesus) and are intended to evoke piety in the observers.

#### → #88 — San Carlo alle Quattro Fontane (Francesco Borromini, 1638–1646)

**Theme:** Baroque

> Both the façade of the Il Gesù and the façade of the San Carlo alle Quattro Fontane exhibit deviations from the classical style and the styles prevalent in the earlier Renaissance. The San Carlo alle Quattro Fontane does so in its undulating exterior, and its two levels of columns is similar to the façade of the Il Gesù. The San Carlo alle Quattro Fontane also has sculptures in the niches of the façade. The deviations from the Renaissance norm set both works in the Baroque period.
