## #170 — Sika dwa kofi (Golden Stool)

**Ashanti peoples** (south-central Ghana) · c. 1700 C.E. · Gold over wood and cast-gold attachments · 18 in h. × 24 in l. × 12 in w.

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Golden_stool_31_January_1935.jpg/250px-Golden_stool_31_January_1935.jpg" width="240" alt="Golden Stool">

### Summary

The **sacred soul of the Asante nation** — a royal stool that may never be sat on or touch the ground.

- **Historical Context.** According to Asante tradition, the stool descended from the sky in the late 1600s onto the lap of King **Osei Tutu**, brought down by his priest **Okomfo Anokye** as a unifying object for the newborn Asante confederacy. In 1900 the British governor Frederick Hodgson demanded to sit on the stool, triggering the **War of the Golden Stool / Yaa Asantewaa War**. The Asante lost the war but kept the stool hidden; it was never captured.
- **Function.** A national palladium. The stool contains the **soul (sunsum)** of the Asante nation, is never placed on the ground, and only the king is allowed to touch it. Used in coronation (the new king is raised above but not onto the stool) and in major state ceremonies; bells attached to the sides alert the king of danger.
- **Content.** A crescent-shaped seat on a flat base; cast-gold scale, bells, and figural attachments; entire surface sheathed in gold.
- **Form.** A single block of **wood** carved and inlaid with sheet and cast **gold** attachments. Symmetrical and proportioned — lightweight (wood) but visually bearing the weight of solid gold.

**Vocabulary:** Ashanti · fetish · Sika Dwa Kofi · replica
