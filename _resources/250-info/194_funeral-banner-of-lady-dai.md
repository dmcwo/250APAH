## #194 — Funeral banner of Lady Dai (Xin Zhui)

**Western Han dynasty, China** · c. 168 B.C.E. · Painted silk · Tomb 1, Mawangdui, Changsha, Hunan · 205 × 92 × 47.7 cm (T-shaped)

### Summary

A T-shaped silk banner mapping the **Han cosmos** — underworld, earth, and heaven — draped over the coffin of a marquise.

- **Historical Context.** **Li Cang**, first Marquis of Dai and prime minister of the Changsha kingdom, died 186 B.C.E.; his wife **Xin Zhui (Lady Dai)** was buried at Mawangdui around 168 B.C.E. Their tomb was rediscovered in 1972 during construction of a hospital — one of the greatest 20th-century Chinese archaeological finds, yielding Lady Dai's famously well-preserved body, lacquerwares, silks, and medical texts. Silk was a mark of Han elite status and the backbone of the Silk Road trade opened under the Han emperors.
- **Function.** A **mingjing** funerary banner carried at the head of the funeral procession, then draped over the innermost coffin to guide the *hun* (celestial soul) to heaven and keep the *po* (earthly soul) anchored to the tomb.
- **Content.** Three registers. **Top (heaven):** sun with crow, crescent moon with toad, the serpent-bodied Queen Mother **Xiwangmu** flanked by gate guards. **Middle (earth):** Lady Dai herself with three attendants on a platform, preparing her journey; below, mourners at a ritual feast before her coffin. **Bottom (underworld):** a giant holds up the earth above coiling fish and chthonic creatures.
- **Form.** Painted in mineral pigments on fine silk; fluid calligraphic line. The T-shape drapes over the coffin, the horizontal bar covering its head. Hierarchical scale: Lady Dai is larger than her attendants.

**Vocabulary:** banner · Yin and Yang · Western Han · silk
