## #96 — Fruit and Insects

**Rachel Ruysch** · 1711 C.E. · Oil on wood · Uffizi, Florence · 44 × 60 cm

### Summary

A **Dutch Golden Age still life** by the most celebrated female flower painter of her era, marrying botanical science with Protestant moral allegory.

- **Historical Context.** **Rachel Ruysch** (1664–1750) began exhibiting at 15 and became the Dutch Republic's most documented woman painter. Her father Frederik Ruysch was a noted professor of **botany and anatomy**; she trained under flower painter Willem van Aelst. The Protestant Dutch Republic's market-driven art scene encouraged specialization in genres like flowers, still life, and landscape — the era of "tulip mania."
- **Function.** Domestic decoration for the Dutch middle and upper-middle class. Functioned as both a showpiece of scientific observation and a **vanitas** — reminding the Protestant viewer that earthly abundance fades and faith endures.
- **Content.** An impossible mixed-season bouquet: **squash and corn** (autumn) beside **peaches** (summer); grapes (wine/Christ's blood) beside wheat (bread/Christ's body), an allusion to the Eucharist. Insects alight on rotting fruit — signs of decay and mortality.
- **Form.** Oil on wood. Complementary **red–green** balance; solid forms in front, airy stems behind; photograph-precise botanical rendering drawn from Ruysch's scientific sketchbooks. The composition looks spontaneously natural but is meticulously composed.

**Vocabulary:** still life · Dutch Golden Age · botany · vanitas · tulip mania
