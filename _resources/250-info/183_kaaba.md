## #183 — The Kaaba

**Islamic** · pre-Islamic origin; rebuilt 631–632 C.E. (and many times since) · Granite masonry, covered with silk Kiswa embroidered in gold and silver thread · Mecca, Saudi Arabia · 43 × 36.2 × 42.2 ft

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/The_Ka%27ba%2C_Great_Mosque_of_Mecca%2C_Saudi_Arabia_%284%29.jpg/250px-The_Ka%27ba%2C_Great_Mosque_of_Mecca%2C_Saudi_Arabia_%284%29.jpg" width="240" alt="The Kaaba">

### Summary

The **spiritual center of Islam** — the cube at the heart of the Masjid al-Haram in Mecca.

- **Historical Context.** Traditionally said to have been first built by **Ibrahim (Abraham)** as a simple open rectangle; rebuilt by Muhammad's **Quraysh** tribe c. 608 and repeatedly since. The **Black Stone**, said to have been given to Ibrahim by the angel Gabriel, is the only part of the original that still stands. Before Islam it housed pagan idols; these were destroyed when Muhammad returned to Mecca in 630. Since then the Kaaba has been the supreme focus of Islamic devotion; non-Muslims cannot enter the city.
- **Function.** The **qibla** — the direction of prayer — for all Muslims five times a day. The centerpiece of the **Hajj**, the pilgrimage that every able Muslim is required to make at least once. Pilgrims **circumambulate** (*tawaf*) the Kaaba seven times and try to touch or kiss the Black Stone.
- **Content.** A nearly cubic granite building covered each year by a new black silk **Kiswa** embroidered in gold with the **shahada** ("There is no god but Allah, and Mohammed is the Messenger of Allah") and other Quranic inscriptions in Arabic calligraphy. The Black Stone is set in the east corner. Interior holds pillars and gold plates.
- **Form.** Simple stone cube — the plainest geometry carrying the greatest spiritual value. Black Kiswa against the gold calligraphy creates sharp contrast.

**Vocabulary:** Kiswa · circumambulation · Hajj · qibla · Black Stone
