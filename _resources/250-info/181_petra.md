## #181 — Petra, Jordan: Treasury and Great Temple

**Nabataean, Ptolemaic, and Roman** · c. 400 B.C.E.–100 C.E. · Cut rock · Jordan · Treasury façade ~130 ft; Great Temple ~57 ft

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Treasury_petra_crop.jpeg/250px-Treasury_petra_crop.jpeg" width="240" alt="Petra Treasury">

### Summary

A rock-cut **Nabataean capital** where Hellenistic, Egyptian, and Arabian styles converge.

- **Historical Context.** Capital of the **Nabataean Kingdom** until the Roman conquest in 106 C.E. Wealth came from its control of the caravan trade in incense, spices, and luxury goods. Stylistic syncretism on the Treasury's façade reflects contact with the **Ptolemaic** Greeks in Alexandria and later the Romans. Petra declined as sea routes and cities like **Palmyra** took over the trade.
- **Function.** The Treasury (Al-Khazneh) was originally a **royal crypt and temple**. The Great Temple was the principal religious structure, probably dedicated to the Nabataean god **Dushara**, though a small theater inside may also mark it as a palace or assembly hall. The whole city functioned as a state capital and trading hub.
- **Content.** The Treasury is a shallow rock-cut façade with a **broken pediment**, central **tholos**, Corinthian columns, and relief figures including dancing Amazons and the Greco-Egyptian goddess **Isis-Tyche**. The Great Temple rises in two levels (only foundations and columns survive) and is enclosed by colonnades ending in a small apse.
- **Form.** Carved directly out of the pink-red sandstone cliff face — an Arabian innovation. Treasury façade fuses Greek pediment/tholos/Corinthian order, Egyptian obelisk bases, and Nabataean rock-cut technique. Entry from a narrow slot canyon dramatically reveals the façade.

**Vocabulary:** tholos · pediment · Ionic · Doric · Corinthian · mausoleum · crypt · façade
