## #204 — The David Vases

**Yuan Dynasty, China (made in Jingdezhen)** · 1351 C.E. · White porcelain with cobalt-blue underglaze · British Museum, London · 63.8 cm tall, 19.6 cm diameter

### Summary

A pair of dated **blue-and-white porcelain** altar vases — the benchmark for understanding 14th-century Yuan ceramic production and the global cobalt trade.

- **Historical Context.** Made in **Jingdezhen**, the imperial kiln city of south China, in 1351 during the Mongol **Yuan Dynasty**. Yuan-era blue-and-white depended on **cobalt imported from Iran** along Silk Road trade routes, a spectacular case of cross-cultural exchange. The vases bear a dated inscription naming donor **Zhang Wenjin** of Yushan, who commissioned them along with an incense burner as offerings to a Daoist temple dedicated to a deified general.
- **Function.** Altar vases for a **Daoist temple** — religious votive objects. Later collected by **Sir Percival David** (early 20th c.), whose collection became the foundation of Chinese ceramic connoisseurship in the West.
- **Content.** Tall *yuhuchunping*-derived form scaled up to altar size. Registers of ornament: stylized dragons chasing pearls, phoenixes, cranes among clouds, scrolling peonies and lotus, plantain leaves; elephant-head handles on the neck. A dated dedicatory inscription runs around the neck's upper side.
- **Form.** Hard white porcelain body covered with clear glaze; decoration painted in cobalt-blue pigment *under* the glaze, fired at high temperature. Shape echoes ancient bronze ritual vessel proportions.

**Vocabulary:** underglaze · porcelain · cobalt blue
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.1, 2.2
