## #231 — Untitled #228, from the History Portraits series

**Cindy Sherman (USA)** · 1990 C.E. · Chromogenic color photograph · Museum of Modern Art, New York · 6′10″ tall

### Summary

Cindy Sherman **dressed as the Old Testament heroine Judith**, photographed as a monumental, ironic restaging of the Renaissance and Baroque Judith-and-Holofernes tradition.

- **Historical Context.** Made during **Sherman's History Portraits series (1988–1990)**, painted in Rome and photographed after returning to New York. Sherman (b. 1954) had already established herself with **Untitled Film Stills** (late 1970s), all staged self-portraits. In the History Portraits she restages Old Master paintings — not to flatter them but to expose the artifice and gender coding of "great art." Aligned with feminist art theory of the 1970s–90s that interrogated the "male gaze."
- **Function.** A **feminist critique** of both the canon of Western painting (where women are either saints, seductresses, or victims) and the tropes of heroism. It challenges viewers to see the mask underneath supposedly naturalistic portrayals.
- **Content.** Sherman poses as **Judith** standing over the severed head of **Holofernes** — a story from the Book of Judith retold countless times by Caravaggio, Artemisia Gentileschi, and others. Sherman's Judith wears red silk, has grotesquely oversized hands and feet, a sickly pallor, and a cold, masculine stare. Holofernes' head is comically fake.
- **Form.** Large-scale color photograph shot to mimic Old Master lighting and composition, yet obviously artificial: visible prosthetic body parts, green-tinged theatrical lighting, cheap fabrics. Photography performing as painting.

**Vocabulary:** contemporary · photography · Old Testament · Judith · Holofernes · Feminist Movement
