## #28 — Peplos Kore from the Acropolis

**Archaic Greek** · c. 530 B.C.E. · Marble with painted details · Acropolis (Athens); now Acropolis Museum · 118 cm tall

### Summary

An Archaic **kore** — a young woman in a heavy woolen **peplos** — found on the **Athenian Acropolis**, once vividly painted and likely a votive image of a goddess.

- **Historical Context.** Excavated from the Athenian Acropolis in the late 19th century. Korai (female clothed) and kouroi (male nude) figures were a defining genre of Archaic Greek sculpture, often set up as votive dedications on the sanctuary. Her iconography may identify her as **Artemis** or **Athena** rather than a generic maiden — arm holes suggest she once held a bow.
- **Function.** A **votive offering** on the Acropolis, likely dedicated by a male worshipper to a goddess; possibly an image of the goddess herself. Korai could also serve as grave markers.
- **Content.** Frontal young woman in a heavy woolen **peplos** belted at the waist; missing left arm once extended forward. Distinctive **Archaic smile**, carefully styled wavy hair, remnants of bright paint (red, blue, yellow) once covered her garment with animal patterns.
- **Form.** **Parian marble** with **encaustic paint**. Rigid, frontal stance; squared shoulders; symmetrical. Bright original polychromy contradicts the common misconception of white Greek marble; smooth planes of fabric replace the earlier stylized drapery.

**Vocabulary:** kore · peplos · encaustic paint
