## #230 — Pink Panther

**Jeff Koons (USA)** · 1988 C.E. · Glazed porcelain · Museum of Modern Art, New York · 41 × 20½ × 19 in

### Summary

A glossy porcelain **kitsch icon** pairing a blonde Hollywood starlet with a cartoon character — part of Koons's provocative **Banality** series.

- **Historical Context.** Made by **Jeff Koons** (b. 1955) for his **Banality** series (1988), which elevated mass-culture ephemera to fine-art status using traditionally high-art craft. Porcelain — once an imperial luxury — was now a mass commodity, a contradiction Koons exploited. The series drew fierce debate about commodification, taste, and originality (Koons was later sued for copyright infringement over another Banality piece). The **Pink Panther**, a cartoon character from the 1960s Blake Edwards films, is wrapped around the figure of film star **Jayne Mansfield**, whose trademark color was pink.
- **Function.** **Critique (or embrace) of kitsch** — a deliberately provocative collapse of adult sexuality and childhood cartoons, of high and low culture. Also a marketable luxury object for the 1980s art market.
- **Content.** A partially nude, idealized female figure (Mansfield-like) cradles the pink-furred Pink Panther; her head tilts back, her eyes closed, her red-lipped smile broad. The cartoon's weariness contrasts with her ecstatic passivity.
- **Form.** Glazed porcelain produced in an edition of three plus one artist's proof in a German workshop. Saccharine pastel palette (pink, mint, lemon). Highly polished surfaces; deliberately commercial sheen.

**Vocabulary:** contemporary art · kitsch · porcelain · Jayne Mansfield · Pink Panther · consumerism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 1.4
