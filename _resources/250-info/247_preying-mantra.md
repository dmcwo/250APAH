## #247 — Preying Mantra

**Wangechi Mutu (Kenyan-American)** · 2006 C.E. · Mixed media on Mylar · Brooklyn Museum, New York · 73.25 × 54.25 in

### Summary

A **collaged Afrofuturist Eve** reclining on a Kuba-cloth blanket with a green snake — Mutu's layered interrogation of race, gender, and the art-historical female body.

- **Historical Context.** **Wangechi Mutu** (b. 1972 in Nairobi, Kenya) trained in Wales and at Yale; she lives and works in New York and Nairobi. She is a leading voice in **Afrofuturism**, using collage, sculpture, and film to imagine hybrid Black female identities. Her work responds to European colonial imagery of African women as exotic, primitive, or grotesque, and to the long Western tradition of the reclining female nude.
- **Function.** **Feminist and postcolonial critique**. Evokes biblical Eve with her snake, praying-mantis predation, and reclining odalisque conventions, then collages them into a single hybrid being who is simultaneously predator and prey.
- **Content.** A dark-skinned, nude **female figure** reclines in a tree. Her body parts are collaged from many sources — her left ear, for example, has chicken feet and insect pincers. She holds a green snake in her left hand and looks out at the viewer. She rests on a brightly patterned blanket resembling Kuba cloth from the DRC. The title puns "**praying mantis**" on "**preying mantra**."
- **Form.** Mixed-media collage on translucent Mylar — ink, acrylic, magazine cutouts, photographs. Saturated magenta, yellow, and blue. Visibly layered, textured surface. Diagonal composition from lower left to upper right.

**Vocabulary:** Afrofuturism · collage · hybridity · Mylar
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.2, 1.3, 3.2

#### → #105 — Self-Portrait (Elisabeth Louise Vigée Le Brun, 1790)

**Theme:** Power of women, sensuality

> In her self-portrait, Le Brun portrays herself as a powerful woman of high status, showing off her wealth, independence, and professional skills as the court painter for Marie Antoinette. The female figure in Preying Mantra also possesses hidden power similar to that of a praying mantis. Both pieces incorporate female sensuality, with Le Brun's parted mouth and the female figure's nudity.
