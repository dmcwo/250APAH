## #218 — Buk Mask

**Torres Strait Islanders (Mabuiag Island, between Australia and Papua New Guinea)** · Mid to late 19th century C.E. · Turtle shell, raffia, feathers, and shell · Torres Strait · 54.6 × 63.5 × 57.8 cm

### Summary

A rare **Torres Strait turtle-shell mask** surmounted by a frigate-bird totem, almost certainly used in dance ceremonies for death, fertility, or initiation.

- **Historical Context.** Made by Torres Strait Islanders in the mid–late 1800s using polished plates of **sea-turtle shell** lashed together — a technique unique in the world. By the 1870s, **London Missionary Society missionaries** arrived and prompted the destruction or surrender of traditional religious objects, making surviving masks few; most in museums today were collected by Europeans during that transitional period.
- **Function.** Worn in male-performed dances connected to **death, fertility, or initiation**, accompanied by drums, chants, and fire to invoke mythical ancestors.
- **Content.** A human face with shell eyes and a latticework "beard"; surmounted by a **frigate bird** totem with outstretched wings ornamented with disks that resemble ceremonial pendants. Raffia hair and feather plumes hang and move in the wind.
- **Form.** Plates of turtle shell heated, bent, and sewn together with vegetable fibers — a subtractive/additive technique. Dynamic, intended to be seen in motion. The composite human–bird imagery is typical of Torres Strait ceremonial art.

**Vocabulary:** Torres Strait · effigy · totem
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.4
