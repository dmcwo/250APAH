## #35 — Acropolis (Parthenon)

**Iktinos and Kallikrates, High Classical Greek** · c. 447–410 B.C.E. · Marble · Athens, Greece · Cella 29.8 × 19.2 m

### Summary

The **Parthenon**, crown of the **Athenian Acropolis**, built under **Pericles** as a Doric temple to **Athena Parthenos** — a high-water mark of Classical Greek architecture engineered to look perfectly regular through subtle curves.

- **Historical Context.** Designed by **Iktinos and Kallikrates**, with sculptural program by **Phidias**, during Athens's democratic Golden Age after the Persian invasion (480 B.C.E.) had destroyed the earlier Acropolis. Converted into a church and later a mosque; partly blown up in 1687 when Ottoman powder stored inside was hit by Venetian fire. Many of the sculptures are now in the British Museum.
- **Function.** A **temple to Athena Parthenos** (Athena the Virgin), housing a colossal chryselephantine cult statue by Phidias. Not a congregational space — ritual happened outside; every four years the **Panathenaic procession** culminated with the presentation of a peplos to Athena's old image.
- **Content.** Pediments depict Athena's birth (east) and her contest with Poseidon (west); metopes show battles (Centaurs vs. Lapiths, Gigantomachy, etc.); the continuous **Ionic frieze** depicts the **Panathenaic Procession**, including the **Plaque of the Ergastines** presenting the peplos.
- **Form.** **Doric peripteral** temple: 8 × 17 columns; marble post-and-lintel construction. Calculated **optical refinements** — columns with subtle **entasis**, stylobate curved upward, corner columns thicker — create the illusion of perfect straight geometry.

**Vocabulary:** Doric · Ionic · stylobate · pediment · triglyphs · metopes · cella / naos · peripteral · colonnade · entablature · peplos · frieze · marble
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.4, 2.2, 3.1
