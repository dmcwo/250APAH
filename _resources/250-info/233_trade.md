## #233 — Trade (Gifts for Trading Land with White People)

**Jaune Quick-to-See Smith (Salish, Kootenai, and French-Cree)** · 1992 C.E. · Oil paint and mixed media, collage, objects, canvas · Chrysler Museum of Art, Norfolk, Virginia · 60 × 170 in (triptych)

### Summary

A massive **Native American counter-monument** to Columbus's quincentenary, indicting five centuries of exploitative "trade" with Indigenous peoples.

- **Historical Context.** **Jaune Quick-to-See Smith** (b. 1940), raised on the Flathead Reservation in Montana, created *Trade* for the **1992 quincentenary** of Columbus's arrival — part of her "Quincentenary Non-Celebration" series. She countered the triumphal narrative with a work about land theft, stereotyping, and the commodification of Native identity. Earned an MA in art from the University of New Mexico, 1980.
- **Function.** A **political protest** and historical reckoning. Makes explicit the unequal trade — blankets, trinkets, alcohol — for enormous tracts of Native land, and the continuing commodification of Native imagery in American consumer culture.
- **Content.** A **triptych canvas** collaged with newspapers, comics, photos, tobacco and gum wrappers, and ads stereotyping Native life. Drips of red paint recall blood. A dark silhouette of a canoe overlays the collage. Above the canvas hangs a clothesline of cheap, mass-produced **"Indian" kitsch** — toy tomahawks, a Washington Redskins hat, feather headdresses, Red Man chewing tobacco tins — offered as if for trade.
- **Form.** Large horizontal triptych. Aggressive layering of collage, drips, and outline contrasts with the commodity objects suspended above. Dominant red palette.

**Vocabulary:** collage · triptych · mixed media
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 2.1, 2.2

**Theme:** Abstraction, unsettling, disillusionment

> The two pieces both seek to challenge the United States' stereotypes of their cultures. Trade's function is similar – to reveal the commoditization and oppression of Natives by the American government.
