## #19 — The Code of Hammurabi

**Susian / Babylonian (Hammurabi)** · c. 1792–1750 B.C.E. · Basalt · found at Susa (modern Iran); now Louvre, Paris · 7 ft 4 in tall

### Summary

A basalt **stele** carved with the **earliest extensive codified law**, crowned by a relief of **Hammurabi receiving authority from the sun god Shamash**.

- **Historical Context.** Erected under **Hammurabi**, who ruled northern and western Mesopotamia from **Babylon**. The stele was later carried off to **Susa** as war booty. Its 300-plus laws, written in **cuneiform**, regulate commerce, family, property, and crime, and famously establish "**eye for an eye**" (lex talionis). The punishments scale by social class.
- **Function.** A public monument publishing the king's law and asserting its **divine origin**, so the code would outlast Hammurabi himself.
- **Content.** At the top, a high-relief scene: **Hammurabi stands before the enthroned Shamash**, god of justice, who hands him a rod and ring (symbols of authority). Below, densely inscribed laws cover the stone in cuneiform.
- **Form.** A tall, tapering **basalt stele** in high relief. Figures shown in the **Egyptian composite convention** (shoulders frontal, face in profile). King and god are nearly **equal in height** — Hammurabi is only slightly smaller — emphasizing their near-equivalent standing. Lightning bolts radiate from Shamash's shoulders.

**Vocabulary:** stele · cuneiform · hierarchical scale · relief
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 1.4
