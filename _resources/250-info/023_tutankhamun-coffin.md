## #23 — Tutankhamun's tomb, innermost coffin

**New Kingdom Egypt, 18th Dynasty** · c. 1323 B.C.E. · Gold with inlay of enamel and semiprecious stones · Egyptian Museum, Cairo · 6 ft 7 in long

### Summary

The innermost of three nested coffins holding the mummy of the boy-king **Tutankhamun** — **solid gold** inlaid with semiprecious stones, discovered intact by Howard Carter in 1922.

- **Historical Context.** Son of **Akhenaten**, Tutankhamun took the throne as a child and **reversed his father's monotheism**, restoring worship of Amun and the capital at **Thebes**. He died around eighteen. The tomb's unlooted state made its contents the defining image of Egyptian royal burial.
- **Function.** A **mummiform coffin** to house and protect the king's body on its journey to the **afterlife** — its expense and imagery also proclaim his divine status.
- **Content.** The king in idealized youth wearing the **nemes headcloth** and **false beard**, arms crossed over the chest holding the **crook and flail** (attributes of Osiris). Protective goddesses **Nekhbet** (vulture) and **Wadjet** (cobra) spread their wings across the torso; **Isis** and **Nephthys** are engraved at the feet. The surface is densely covered with hieroglyphic spells.
- **Form.** **Solid gold** with inlays of **colored glass and semiprecious stones** (lapis lazuli, turquoise, carnelian). A nearly vacuum-free **horror vacui** surface; vertically symmetrical; life-size and body-hugging. Original **scrap-gold value** alone is estimated at $1.7 million.

**Vocabulary:** pharaoh · sarcophagus · hieroglyphics · ankh · horror vacui
