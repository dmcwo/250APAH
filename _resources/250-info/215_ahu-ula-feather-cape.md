## #215 — 'Ahu 'ula (Feather cape)

**Polynesia (Hawaii)** · before 1850 C.E. · Feathers and olonā fiber · National Maritime Museum · 64 × 15½ in

### Summary

A feather cape of extraordinary rarity and labor — a **Hawaiian chiefly garment** embodying sacred mana.

- **Historical Context.** 'Ahu 'ula were made exclusively for the **ali'i** (chiefs) of the Hawaiian Islands. The cape came to symbolize the unification of Hawaii under **King Kamehameha** in the late 18th century; many were given as diplomatic gifts to visiting European captains, including Captain Cook, beginning a long dispersal into Western collections. Making a cape required roughly **500,000 feathers** from native forest birds (now largely extinct), each harvested laboriously.
- **Function.** A **rank garment** worn only by ali'i during ceremonies and battle; transmitted across generations to carry the wearer's lineage of mana (spiritual authority). Length and density signaled status.
- **Content.** Semicircular in later examples (trapezoidal earlier). Vivid red feathers of the 'i'iwi bird fill the ground; yellow (rare, harvested one feather at a time) and black feathers from the extinct 'ō'ō form geometric bands, crescents, and triangles.
- **Form.** Tiny feather bundles tied in overlapping rows onto a netted base of hand-twisted **olonā** bast fiber. Red was the royal color across Polynesia; yellow was scarce and doubly prestigious.

**Vocabulary:** tropical honeycreepers · cloak · scarcity · ali'i · mana · olonā
