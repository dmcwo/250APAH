## #12 — White Temple and its Ziggurat

**Sumerian** · c. 3500–3000 B.C.E. · Mud brick · Uruk (modern Warka, Iraq) · ~40 ft / 12 m high

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/White_Temple_ziggurat_in_Uruk.jpg/250px-White_Temple_ziggurat_in_Uruk.jpg" width="240" alt="White Temple and its Ziggurat">

### Summary

One of the earliest monumental religious structures: a whitewashed temple set atop a stepped **ziggurat** in the world's first major city.

- **Historical Context.** Built at **Uruk** (modern Warka, Iraq), a pioneer of urban life in Mesopotamia and birthplace of **cuneiform** writing. Uruk society was stratified, headed by **priests**, and polytheistic. The complex towered over the city's defensive walls.
- **Function.** A tribute to the **Mesopotamian sky god Anu**. Access was restricted to a small group of priests and, likely, the ruling elite; it also served administrative and economic roles, since religion and government were intertwined. The height brought worship physically closer to the sky.
- **Content.** A rectangular temple with a **tripartite plan** (long central cella flanked by side rooms) sitting atop a high ziggurat; the corners orient to the cardinal points. A stepped **altar** sat in the cella where, symbolically, Anu was believed to descend.
- **Form.** Mud brick covered in **white plaster**, producing a luminous light-catching exterior. Sloping sides with recessed bands; a single stairway ramp climbing the north side; a "**bent-axis**" plan requiring a ninety-degree turn to reach the altar.

**Vocabulary:** ziggurat · Anu · cuneiform · cella · bent-axis plan
**Learning Objectives:** LO 3.1, 3.3, 3.5

**Theme:** Honorary, similar structure

> Both the Great Pyramids at Giza, Egypt and the White Temple and its Ziggurat were honorary—the White Temple to the Mesopotamian sky god Anu, and the Pyramids to the Egyptian kings Menkaura, Khafre, and Khufu. The Pyramids, however, served a funerary purpose. Both structures have a similar sloping structure, though the Pyramids are significantly taller.

#### → #157 — Templo Mayor (Mexica/Aztec, 1375–1520 C.E.)

**Theme:** Pay homage to key deities, similar structure

> Both the White Temple and the Templo Mayor are dedicated to key deities in their respective cultures. The White Temple is dedicated to Anu, while the Templo Mayor is a set of twin temples, one of which is dedicated to Tlaloc (Aztec god of water, rain, and agriculture) and the other to Huitzilopochti (war, fire, and sun god). Additionally, both temples have a very similar structure, positioned at the center of the city, and accessible by two sets of stairs.

#### → #192 — Great Stupa at Sanchi (India, c. 300 B.C.E.–100 C.E.)

**Theme:** Associated with key figures/deities, similar materials used

> The Great Stupa at Sanchi and the White Temple both show respect and importance of key religious figures. However, they differ in that the Great Stupa represents Buddha himself, while the White Temple is a place to worship the sky god Anu. The two structures are made out of the same material—mud brick, though the stupa has not been gone over with white plaster.

### Compare-and-contrast exercise (LO 3.5)

#### Great Stupa at Sanchi (Madhya Pradesh, India, c. 300 B.C.E.–100 C.E.) — *not in image set*

**Prompt:** Students analyze relationships between works of art based on their similarities and differences. What are some similarities and differences between the White Temple and its Ziggurat and the Great Stupa at Sanchi?

> Both the White Temple and its Ziggurat and the Great Stupa at Sanchi are dedicated to important figures in each of their religions/belief systems. The White Temple is dedicated to Anu, the Mesopotamian sky god, while the Great Stupa at Sanchi is dedicated to Buddha (Buddhism). However, a key difference here is that while the White Temple is dedicated to Anu, the Stupa is considered to *be* Buddha himself—the base represents his crossed legs, the middle portion his torso, and the top his head. The two structures are also similar in that they were constructed with consideration to the presence of a heavenly realm in the sky. The Mesopotamians believed that deities resided in the sky, and therefore sought to make their temples as tall as possible in order to be closer to the sky. Similar, a spire atop the Great Stupa symbolizes an axis of rotation through the earth's center, through which a connection between Earth and the heavens was established. Both these structures were constructed from mud brick. However, the White Temple was done over in white plaster. Additionally, the White Temple and its Ziggurat could be entered, while the Great Stupa was a solid structure and could only be circumambulated around.
