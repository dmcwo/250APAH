## #18 — King Menkaura and Queen

**Egyptian, Old Kingdom, Fourth Dynasty** · c. 2490–2472 B.C.E. · Greywacke · Giza, Egypt (Menkaura Valley Temple) · 56 × 22½ × 21¾ in

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Statue_of_Menkaura_and_Queen_Khamerernebty_II._in_Boston_1.jpg/330px-Statue_of_Menkaura_and_Queen_Khamerernebty_II._in_Boston_1.jpg" width="240" alt="King Menkaura and Queen">

### Summary

A rigid, paired greywacke portrait of **pharaoh Menkaura and his queen** (probably Khamerernebty II) — a canonical Old Kingdom **dyad statue** designed to house the royal **ka**.

- **Historical Context.** Found in Menkaura's Valley Temple near his pyramid at Giza. Menkaura's complex was never finished — resources were waning — and he did not live to complete it. Ancient Egyptians believed a person's **ka** needed a preserved likeness to return to in the afterlife.
- **Function.** A **tomb dyad** to guarantee the king's rebirth in the afterlife. Idealized, ageless bodies present Menkaura as eternally youthful and divinely powerful.
- **Content.** Menkaura and a royal woman (likely his consort) stand side-by-side, both striding forward with the **left foot advanced** (Menkaura's more pronounced). She embraces him with one arm around his waist and the other across his upper arm.
- **Form.** High relief against a back slab; originally **brightly painted** (traces of red on the king's face, yellow on the queen's face, black on her hair). **Smooth, geometric bodies**, equal height conveying parity; frontal, symmetrical, with fists clenched (a standard royal gesture).

**Vocabulary:** ka · high relief · visual hierarchy / hieratic scale
**Learning Objectives:** LO 3.2
