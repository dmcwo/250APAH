## #132 — Improvisation 28 (second version)

**Wassily Kandinsky** (Der Blaue Reiter / Expressionism) · 1912 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum, New York · 43⅞ × 63⅞ in.

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/18/Vasily_Kandinsky_Improvisation_28_%28second_version%29.jpg" width="240" alt="Improvisation 28 (second version)">

### Summary

An early landmark of **pure abstraction**, translating music into paint.

- **Historical Context.** Painted in **Munich** during Kandinsky's **Der Blaue Reiter** ("Blue Rider") period — one of the two great German **Expressionist** groups. Russian-born Kandinsky developed his abstract theory in Munich, exchanging ideas with Franz Marc and other avant-garde artists and drawing on Russian folk art's stylized floral patterns.
- **Function.** To make **atonal music** visible. The title *Improvisation* refers to the act of making, not the subject; Kandinsky wanted the painting to evoke the discordant, unresolved feeling of music without a tonic note.
- **Content.** Nominally a landscape — faint mountains and hill forms emerge on close inspection — but deliberately left so abstract that each viewer reads their own forms. A rough divide two-thirds across the canvas echoes passages in a musical composition.
- **Form.** Oil on canvas; sweeping overlapping lines in saturated color set against a grey-navy ground. Straight lines clash with curves; smaller half-circles near the bottom suggest staccato notes. Intentionally disunified to mirror atonal music.

**Vocabulary:** Expressionism · Der Blaue Reiter · Improvisation
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.2, 2.2, 3.2

#### → #130 — The Portuguese (Georges Braque, 1911)

**Theme:** Complete abstraction

> While Kandinsky's work shows music by expressing several seemingly related forms, Braque explores representing an object so many times, from so many viewpoints, that it becomes unrecognizable. Both use abstraction to convey an idea that would normally be too obvious to the reader.
