## #134 — Memorial Sheet for Karl Liebknecht (*In Memoriam Karl Liebknecht*)

**Käthe Kollwitz** (German Expressionism) · 1919–1920 C.E. · Woodcut · Museum of Modern Art, New York · 1 ft 1¾ in × 1 ft 7⅝ in

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/K%C3%A4the_Kollwitz_Gedenkblatt_f%C3%BCr_Karl_Liebknecht_1920.jpg/330px-K%C3%A4the_Kollwitz_Gedenkblatt_f%C3%BCr_Karl_Liebknecht_1920.jpg" width="240" alt="Memorial Sheet for Karl Liebknecht">

### Summary

A post-WWI woodcut that recasts a **political assassination** as a modern **Lamentation**.

- **Historical Context.** Made in the **disillusionment** after WWI, when German artists turned to print because it was cheap and mass-reproducible — ideal for political statements. Communist leader **Karl Liebknecht** was arrested and killed in custody in 1919 during the leftist uprising against the Socialist-led Weimar government. Kollwitz herself was closer to the Socialists, but had been moved by Liebknecht's charisma at a public speech; his family commissioned her to memorialize him.
- **Function.** A memorial, not an endorsement. Kollwitz mourns the man while withholding approval of his Communist politics, and casts his unjust death as evidence of the corruption of power — adopting the **Lamentation** format to compare his sacrifice to Christ's.
- **Content.** Liebknecht's body lies along the bottom of the sheet, draped in pure white. Men, women, and children crowd above him; one mourner bends over the body with hand to mouth. Downcast faces, slightly individualized, echo the grief of followers at Christ's Lamentation.
- **Form.** Two-dimensional **woodcut** print in black, white, and tan. Curvilinear lines carry most of the modeling; no naturalistic shading. Kollwitz had switched from etching to woodcut after seeing Ernst Barlach's work, finding the coarser medium truer to the monumental subject.

**Vocabulary:** printmaking · woodblock · Lamentation · German Expressionism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.3, 1.4
