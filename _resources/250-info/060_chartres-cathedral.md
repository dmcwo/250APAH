## #60 — Chartres Cathedral

**Gothic Europe** · Original c. 1145–1155 C.E.; reconstructed c. 1194–1220 C.E. · Limestone, stained glass · Chartres, France · 430 × 105 ft; nave 121 ft high

### Summary

A High **Gothic** cathedral whose **flying buttresses** liberated the walls for **vast stained-glass** windows, made the Virgin Mary its patron, and drew pilgrims to the relic of her **Sancta Camisa**.

- **Historical Context.** Located ~95 km SW of Paris. Begun in the Romanesque style (1145–1155), largely destroyed by fire in 1194, and rebuilt in the **High Gothic** style (1194–1220). The asymmetric west towers preserve this history — Romanesque spire on the right, Gothic on the left. The relic of Mary's **Sancta Camisa** (tunic she allegedly wore at Christ's birth) made Chartres a major pilgrimage site.
- **Function.** Bishop's seat and **pilgrimage cathedral**, a house for relics, and a classroom of stone and glass for the illiterate faithful.
- **Content.** Three major sculpted **portal tympana** on the west, south, and north façades with accompanying jamb statues (Royal Portal on the west is an icon of High Romanesque-into-Gothic carving). Three large **rose windows** depict the Last Judgment (west), Christ (south), and the Virgin and Child (north); the interior holds ~150 medieval **stained glass** windows including the famous "Chartres blue."
- **Form.** **Gothic limestone** with **ribbed groin vaults**, **pointed arches**, and a **seven-bay nave** fronted by a **narthex**. External **flying buttresses** transfer the vault's thrust outward, freeing the walls for vast window openings. Stained glass floods the interior with jewel-toned light.

**Vocabulary:** Romanesque · Gothic · stained glass · rose window · clerestory · flying buttress · narthex · nave
