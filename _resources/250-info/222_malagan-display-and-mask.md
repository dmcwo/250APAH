## #222 — Malagan display and mask

**People of northern New Ireland Province, Papua New Guinea** · 20th century C.E. · Wood, pigment, fiber, and shell · New Ireland · 28 × 16 × 18 in (mask)

### Summary

Ephemeral **mortuary sculptures** of northern New Ireland — carved to hold the souls of the dead during ritual and, in most cases, destroyed afterward.

- **Historical Context.** **Malangan** (or malagan) refers to a cycle of elaborate, months-long mortuary ceremonies practiced on New Ireland in the Bismarck Archipelago. Preparation begins after a funeral and can take months or years; families pool resources to commission sculptures and masks from specialist carvers and to host feasts and dances. Ceremonies affirm clan identity and the transfer of land rights as well as dismiss the dead.
- **Function.** Sculptures temporarily house the **souls of the deceased** before dispatching them to the afterworld; they are treated as spiritually dangerous. After the climactic ceremony most malangan sculptures are **burned or left to rot** — only masks and musical instruments are retained for future use. **Tatanua masks** are worn by male dancers honoring the dead.
- **Content.** Composite images of humans, fish, birds, snakes, and celestial motifs; every element evokes clan myth and genealogy. Designs belong to specific lineages; the clan headman retains the designs in memory.
- **Form.** Carved wood painted in ritually ordered **black, white, yellow, and red** pigments; decorated with fiber, shell, and lime. Asymmetrical paint on left and right sides of the mask so the dancer's turn reveals a new face.

**Vocabulary:** Malangan · Tatanua dance · Tatanua masks
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.1, 2.2
