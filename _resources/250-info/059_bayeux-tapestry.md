## #59 — Bayeux Tapestry

**Romanesque Europe (English or Norman)** · c. 1066–1080 C.E. · Embroidery on linen · Bayeux, France · ~20 in × 230 ft

### Summary

A 230-ft embroidered **continuous narrative** of the **1066 Norman Conquest of England** — ending with **William's victory over Harold** at the Battle of Hastings — probably made in Canterbury and likely commissioned by William's half-brother, **Bishop Odo**.

- **Historical Context.** Commemorates the **Battle of Hastings (1066)**, where **William of Normandy** defeated **King Harold, Earl of Wessex** for the English throne. High-quality Anglo-Saxon needlework points to a **Canterbury** workshop. Patron is likely **Odo, Bishop of Bayeux** (William's half-brother), who appears prominently.
- **Function.** A **political monument in textile form**. Probably displayed at first in Odo's hall (its scale and secular content suggest a grand secular hanging), later in Bayeux Cathedral. Tells the Norman version of events, justifying William's claim.
- **Content.** **75 scenes** with Latin **tituli** narrating events from King Edward the Confessor's death to Harold's accession, William's invasion fleet and feast, the battle itself, Harold's death (famously, "hit in the eye" by an arrow), and Norman victory. The end (likely William's coronation) is now lost. Upper and lower borders carry animals, **Aesop's Fables**, and scenes of hunting and husbandry.
- **Form.** **Embroidered wool** on linen — a true tapestry is woven, so this is technically an embroidery. **Ten shades** of wool yarn dyed with madder (reds), dyer's rocket (yellow), and woad (blues/greens). Figures outlined in black, flat colors, blank backgrounds; the middle zone carries the main story with upper/lower borders as commentary.
- **Vocabulary:** tapestry · tituli · textile · continuous narrative
