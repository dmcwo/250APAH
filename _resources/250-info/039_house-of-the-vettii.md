## #39 — House of the Vettii

**Imperial Roman** · c. second century B.C.E.; rebuilt c. 62–79 C.E. · Cut stone and fresco · Pompeii, Italy · Ground floor ~3,609 sq ft

### Summary

A richly frescoed **Pompeiian domus** owned by two **freedman brothers** — the Vettii — whose grand atrium, peristyle garden, and ostentatious wall paintings survive because of the **79 C.E. eruption of Vesuvius**.

- **Historical Context.** The brothers **Aulus Vettius Restitutus** and **Aulus Vettius Conviva** were wealthy freedmen merchants who bought and redecorated the house after the 62 C.E. earthquake displaced many old elite families. The house is a paradigm of the Roman **Fourth-Style** wall painting and exemplifies new freedman wealth in late-1st-century Pompeii.
- **Function.** Private residence *and* stage for the Roman **patron-client ritual**. Patrons received clients in the **atrium** each morning; the house's scale and decoration broadcast status and cemented the patron's authority.
- **Content.** Standard domus plan: **fauces** (narrow entry), **atrium** with **impluvium**, **tablinum**, and a columned **peristyle garden**. Prominent **strongboxes** in the atrium displayed wealth; frescoes in the Fourth Style — including mythological scenes and the famous image of **Priapus** — line the walls.
- **Form.** Stone masonry carrying an inward-facing plan organized around open courtyards; colonnaded peristyle around a formal garden. Fourth-Style frescoes combine illusionistic architectural vistas with framed mythological panels in saturated reds, yellows, and blacks.

**Vocabulary:** atrium · impluvium · peristyle garden
