## #9 — Ambum Stone

**Unknown (ancestors of the Enga peoples)** · c. 1500 B.C.E. · Greywacke · Enga / Ambum Valley, Papua New Guinea · 7.9 × 3.0 × 5.5 in

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8c/Ambum_Stone.webp/250px-Ambum_Stone.webp.png" width="240" alt="Ambum Stone">

### Summary

A finely carved Papua New Guinean **zoomorphic pestle**, probably representing a fetal echidna and venerated as a "bone of the ancestors."

- **Historical Context.** Carved in the **Enga (or Inga) highlands of Papua New Guinea**, in a culture whose **"big man" power system** has remained relatively stable for millennia. Enga society was largely **animistic**, attributing power to nature and ancestors; authority was negotiated through control of pigs and other resources redistributed at ritual events.
- **Function.** Dual purpose. Stones like this were thought to be imbued by "big men" with supernatural power, **buried on ancestral land**, and fed periodic pig sacrifices to keep the ancestors favorable and the land fertile. It also served as a **mortar** for pounding food or herbs — a ritual object that was also a tool.
- **Content.** A stylized **fetus of an echidna** (spiny anteater), valued for its fat and hunted in the region. Enga call such stones **"simting bilong tumbuna"** — "bones of the ancestors."
- **Form.** Hand-carved **greywacke**, a hard volcanic sandstone. Bulging ears/eyes, a snout closer to a fruit bat than an anteater, arms and legs tucked around a round belly in a fetal pose — an **amalgam of animal and human features**. Vertically symmetrical, curving throughout with almost no straight lines; the elongated neck shows wear/patina consistent with use as a pestle handle.

**Vocabulary:** mortar · pestle · zoomorphic · greywacke · animism · echidna · fetal position
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.4, 3.2
