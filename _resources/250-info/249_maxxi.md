## #249 — MAXXI National Museum of XXI Century Arts

**Zaha Hadid (Iraqi-British)** · 2009–2010 C.E. · Glass, steel, and reinforced concrete · Rome, Italy · ~27,000 m²

### Summary

Zaha Hadid's sinuous, board-formed-concrete **contemporary art museum** in Rome — a flowing, non-rectilinear response to the ancient city's classical order.

- **Historical Context.** Commissioned in 1998 via international competition, won by **Zaha Hadid** (1950–2016), the first woman to win the Pritzker Prize. Completed in 2009 and opened in 2010; won the **Stirling Prize** the same year. Italy's first major **publicly funded contemporary-art institution** in decades, and a landmark in Rome's shift from pure classical heritage toward contemporary identity. Hadid drew on Russian **Constructivism**, Malevich's Suprematism, and the ribboning minaret at Samarra. Her other major works include the Guangzhou Opera House and the London Aquatics Centre.
- **Function.** Multidisciplinary museum: **MAXXI Arte** (contemporary art) and **MAXXI Architettura** (contemporary architecture), with auditorium, library, bookshop, café, and temporary-exhibition galleries. Hadid described the building as a "**campus for art**" rather than an object container.
- **Content.** Intersecting concrete tubes and ribboning corridors form flowing, overlapping gallery spaces. Black steel staircases hang suspended over the main atrium; an open truss-and-louver ceiling floods the interior with daylight. Exterior walls of board-formed concrete curve to embrace the former barracks site.
- **Form.** Continuous, non-rectilinear architecture blurring interior and exterior. Neutral gray-and-white palette; vast glazing; sweeping pathways instead of fixed rooms. CAD-enabled complex curves.

**Vocabulary:** curvilinear · modernism · constructivism
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.2, 1.3, 2.2
