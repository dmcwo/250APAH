## #29 — Sarcophagus of the Spouses

**Etruscan** · c. 520 B.C.E. · Terracotta · from Cerveteri (ancient Caere), Italy; now National Etruscan Museum, Rome · 3 ft 9½ in × 6 ft 7 in

### Summary

A life-size Etruscan **terracotta cinerary urn** topped by a husband and wife reclining together at a banquet — a rare glimpse of Etruscan funerary ritual and of a society where women dined beside men.

- **Historical Context.** Excavated in the 19th century at the Banditaccia necropolis of **Caere** (modern Cerveteri). The Etruscans' own language is only partly deciphered, so their tombs provide the richest evidence for their culture. **Terracotta** dominated Etruscan art because Italian marble was not yet exploited.
- **Function.** A **cinerary container** to hold the cremated remains of an elite couple and assert their status in the afterlife. The banquet iconography reflects the Etruscan **celebratory** approach to funerary rites.
- **Content.** A smiling couple recline side-by-side on a wineskin-shaped cushion; the woman pours drops of perfume into her husband's hand while he embraces her shoulders. She may hold a **pomegranate**, symbol of immortality. Stylized almond eyes, beaded braids, and pointed shoes are characteristically Etruscan.
- **Form.** **Terracotta**, fired in sections and then joined — technically ambitious at this scale. Figures rendered in **high relief** above the waist, schematic below. Rounded Mediterranean faces but distinctly Etruscan angular joints and elongated fingers and toes.

**Vocabulary:** sarcophagus · anthropoid · sculpture
