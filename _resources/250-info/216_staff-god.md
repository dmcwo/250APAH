## #216 — Staff God

**Cook Islanders (Rarotonga, Polynesia)** · Late 18th to early 19th century C.E. · Wood carving wrapped in barkcloth · Cook Islands · 396 cm long (156 × 23.5 × 23.5 in)

### Summary

A wrapped wooden staff-god from **Rarotonga** combining male and female imagery — probably dedicated to the creator god **Tangaroa** and one of the last surviving examples of its kind.

- **Historical Context.** Made on Rarotonga (Cook Islands) before European missionaries from the **London Missionary Society** arrived in 1821. The missionaries denounced such figures as idolatrous and pressured converts to destroy or deface them; this is the only known complete wrapped staff-god to survive. Its phallus, formerly at the base, was removed, likely by missionaries. The figure later entered the British Museum.
- **Function.** Devotional object, probably tied to **fertility**, lineage, and domestic prosperity. Likely invoked the creator god Tangaroa or a specific ancestral deity.
- **Content.** A large carved head at one end; the shaft carries alternating small male figures in profile (carved in relief) and female figures incised into the surface, suggesting both sexes and the cycle of reproduction. Wrapped extensively in decorated barkcloth — a woman's craft.
- **Form.** Long cylindrical wood staff carved by men; wrapped in **tapa** barkcloth painted and beaten by women, reflecting complementary gender roles. Originally richly decorated at both ends; now missing the phallus.

**Vocabulary:** barkcloth · mana · Tangaroa · idolatry
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.4, 2.2, 3.3
