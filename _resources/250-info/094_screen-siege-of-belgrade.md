## #94 — Screen with the Siege of Belgrade and hunting scene

**Circle of the González Family** (New Spain) · c. 1697–1701 C.E. · Oil on wood, inlaid with mother-of-pearl · Mexico · 90½ × 108⅝ in

### Summary

A two-sided **biombo enconchado** whose materials and form embody the syncretism of early 18th-century New Spain.

- **Historical Context.** Created in early 18th-century **New Spain**, a viceroyalty spanning Mexico, much of the modern U.S., Florida, and the Philippines. The culture was highly syncretic — shaped by Spaniards, indigenous Americans, and the Japanese (the screen form itself). Commissioned for viceroy **José Sarmiento de Valladares**.
- **Function.** The battle side served as **political propaganda**, shown to officials the viceroy met with — depicting a very contemporary Hapsburg victory. The hunting side faced the room where the viceroy's wife entertained guests, functioning decoratively while still displaying the family's wealth.
- **Content.** Battle side depicts the **1698 Siege of Belgrade** (Hapsburgs vs. Ottomans) with cramped streets of fighting figures, linear and atmospheric perspective, and emotional soldier faces. The reverse shows a serene hunting landscape emphasizing scenery over figures. Both sides feature floral borders showcasing the shell inlay.
- **Form.** Oil on wood (a Spanish medium), Mexican-invented **enconchado** (shell inlay) technique, and Japanese-derived folding screen format — literally layered continents of influence. The lacquerware base was inspired by imported Japanese lacquer boxes.

**Vocabulary:** screen · biombo · Battle of Belgrade · biombo enconchado
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 2.2, 3.5

> (compared to the hunting side) In both works, the emphasis is on the composition rather than the people in it, although there are people present. In both scenes there is a use of atmospheric perspective to give the viewer a sense of depth and an increased appreciation for nature.
