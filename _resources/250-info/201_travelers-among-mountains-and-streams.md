## #201 — Travelers Among Mountains and Streams

**Fan Kuan (Northern Song Dynasty, China)** · c. 1000 C.E. · Ink and colors on silk, hanging scroll · National Palace Museum, Taipei · 206.3 × 103.3 cm

### Summary

The quintessential **Northern Song landscape** and a foundational work of Chinese ink painting.

- **Historical Context.** Painted around 1000 C.E. by **Fan Kuan**, a recluse of the Taoist and Neo-Confucian tradition who reportedly lived in the Zhongnan and Taihua mountains to study nature directly. Northern Song intellectuals believed that patient observation of the natural world revealed underlying **li** (cosmic principles) — Fan Kuan's signature, found only in the 20th century hidden among the foliage, confirmed his authorship.
- **Function.** A hanging scroll for private contemplation, inviting the viewer to wander mentally through the landscape — a substitute for actual travel among mountains and a way to align oneself with nature's **qi** (vital energy).
- **Content.** A towering central mountain dominates the upper two-thirds; a thread-like waterfall plunges from a cleft. Below, mist separates the looming peak from a rocky foreground where a tiny mule train of travelers and a temple roof emerge among trees — barely visible, emphasizing human insignificance within the cosmos.
- **Form.** Monumental composition stacks foreground, middle ground, and background without linear perspective. Fan Kuan's signature "raindrop texture strokes" (*yudian cun*) build up the massive rock face; ink wash and careful gradations create atmosphere and depth.

**Vocabulary:** Buddhism · Bodhisattva · Confucianism · Daoism · li · Literati · Neo-Confucianism · qi
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.1, 3.2
