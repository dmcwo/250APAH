## #235 — Rebellious Silence, from the Women of Allah series

**Shirin Neshat (Iran/USA); photograph by Cynthia Preston** · 1994 C.E. · Ink on gelatin silver print · Various collections · Variable dimensions

### Summary

A stark self-portrait bisected by a rifle — Neshat's most famous photograph, from a series confronting **Iranian Muslim women's identity** after the Islamic Revolution.

- **Historical Context.** **Shirin Neshat** (b. 1957) left Iran in 1974 to study in California; when she returned in 1990, the **1979 Islamic Revolution** had transformed the country she remembered. Founded by Ayatollah Khomeini, the revolution imposed strict dress codes including the mandatory chador, reshaped women's legal status, and was followed by the devastating Iran–Iraq War (1980–1988). The **Women of Allah** series (1993–1997), photographed by Cynthia Preston, addresses these upheavals from the perspective of a diasporic Iranian woman.
- **Function.** **Political and personal confrontation** with stereotypes of Muslim women as either oppressed victims or revolutionary martyrs; refuses both simplistic Western and Iranian narratives. Opens space for ambiguity.
- **Content.** Neshat faces the viewer directly in black chador; a vertical rifle barrel bisects her face between the eyes. Her exposed skin is covered in **Farsi calligraphy** — poems by Iranian poet **Tahereh Saffarzadeh** about female martyrdom and revolutionary duty. Text becomes veil.
- **Form.** Black-and-white gelatin silver print, overlaid with black ink calligraphy by the artist's own hand. Rigid frontal composition, strong geometric division by the gun.

**Vocabulary:** Muslim · Islamic Revolution · Chador · Farsi calligraphy · Women of Allah
