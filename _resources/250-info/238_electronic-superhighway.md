## #238 — Electronic Superhighway: Continental U.S., Alaska, Hawaii

**Nam June Paik (Korean-American)** · 1995 C.E. · Fifty-one-channel video installation with custom electronics, neon, steel, wood · Smithsonian American Art Museum, Washington, D.C. · 15 × 40 × 4 ft

### Summary

A map of the United States built from **336 televisions and neon tubing** — the "father of video art" visualizing the pre-internet information age.

- **Historical Context.** **Nam June Paik** (1932–2006), born in Seoul and trained as a composer in Tokyo and Germany, joined John Cage and the **Fluxus** avant-garde in the 1960s and pioneered video as an art form. Paik himself coined the term "electronic superhighway" in 1974 to predict a global information network. This installation, made for the Smithsonian in 1995, was created on the verge of mass internet adoption.
- **Function.** An **immersive, nation-scale diagram** of American media culture. Each state becomes a constellation of looping television images — a portrait through media consumption. Both celebratory and cautionary about information overload.
- **Content.** Fifty-one channels of video, one for each state (plus the District of Columbia), loop clips tied to that state's identity: *The Wizard of Oz* for Kansas, civil-rights footage for Alabama, presidential primaries for Iowa. A live camera in D.C. shows the viewer staring back. Colored neon tubing traces the state borders.
- **Form.** 336 cathode-ray TVs embedded in a steel-and-wood map. Dense grid of flashing color and image. Must be seen frontally, like a wall. Meant to overwhelm.

**Vocabulary:** Fluxus · Superhighway · information overload
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 3.2, 3.5
