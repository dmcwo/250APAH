## #108 — Liberty Leading the People

**Eugène Delacroix** · 1830 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Louvre, Paris · 260 × 325 cm

### Summary

A **French Romantic** allegory that became the iconic image of popular revolution.

- **Historical Context.** Painted to celebrate the **July Revolution of 1830** that overthrew King Charles X (brother of the guillotined Louis XVI) and installed the "Citizen King" **Louis-Philippe I**. Delacroix — a **Rubéniste** (color-first) as opposed to David's **Poussiniste** (line-first) heirs — was a leader of the French Romantic movement. The government purchased the painting but hid it from public view for fear of its revolutionary power.
- **Function.** Political painting: memorial, **propaganda**, and national-pride piece. Depicts the French people of all classes uniting against tyranny.
- **Content.** A bare-breasted allegorical **Liberty** in a **Phrygian cap** strides over the dead, holding the **tricolor flag** in one hand and a musket with bayonet in the other. Behind her: a bourgeois man in a top hat (possibly Delacroix himself), a street urchin with dueling pistols (inspiration for *Les Misérables*' Gavroche), and a worker in laborer's garb — the revolution as cross-class alliance. Dead soldiers and civilians foreground the cost.
- **Form.** Pyramidal composition anchoring on the flag's red and Liberty's white dress. Bright highlights against a **smoky, atmospheric background**; vigorous brushwork; contrast and color (not Neoclassical line) carry emotional weight. Scale heightens heroic drama.

**Vocabulary:** Neoclassicism · French Romanticism · July Revolution of 1830 · Phrygian cap
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.1, 2.2, 2.3
