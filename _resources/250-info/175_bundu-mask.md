## #175 — Bundu mask

**Sande Society, Mende peoples** · 19th–20th century C.E. · Wood, cloth, and fiber · West African forests of Sierra Leone and Liberia

### Summary

The only major African mask **carved, worn, and danced by women** — a Sande initiation mask.

- **Historical Context.** The **Mende** are likely descendants of the Mali Empire, now in Sierra Leone and Liberia. The **Sande Society** is a women's secret society that initiates girls into womanhood, teaches sexual and moral comportment, and confers fertility. Unique in West Africa for centering female power.
- **Function.** Worn during **Sande initiation rituals** celebrating the transition from girl to woman. Commissioned by women for women; conceals the dancer (the Sowei) and personifies an ideal of female beauty, health, and serenity.
- **Content.** An idealized female face: **elongated forehead**, **elaborate hairstyle**, small downturned lips, nearly shut eyes, and **neck rolls** — all signs of beauty. Neck rolls symbolize fertility; closed lips signal discretion; small eyes signal inwardness.
- **Form.** **Subtractive wood carving** polished to a shiny black (symbol of water and purity). Each mask is uniquely carved in its hairstyle; facial features are conventional.

**Vocabulary:** Sande Society · conceptual representation · subtractive sculpture · Sowei
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 1.3, 1.4, 2.2
