## #191 — The Ardabil Carpet

**Maqsud Kashani (inscribed name), Safavid Iran** · 1539–1540 C.E. · Wool and silk, knotted pile · Victoria and Albert Museum, London · 34′6″ × 17′6″

### Summary

The most celebrated Persian carpet in the world and an icon of the **Safavid dynasty**.

- **Historical Context.** Commissioned for the **shrine of Sufi saint Safi al-Din** at Ardabil in northwest Iran, ancestor of the Safavid rulers. The Safavid Empire (founded 1501) tied Shi'a Islam to state identity; the court sponsored luxury workshops in Tabriz, Kashan, and Isfahan. An inscribed couplet by the poet Hafiz names **Maqsud Kashani**, a court official who likely oversaw production. One of a matched pair; its twin is in LACMA.
- **Function.** Placed in the shrine as a devotional furnishing honoring the saint and advertising Safavid wealth and piety. Too precious for daily use.
- **Content.** Non-figural Islamic design: a central sunburst medallion with 16 pendants, flanked by two mosque lamps of unequal size (a trick of perspective making them appear equal from the shrine entrance). Dense floral arabesques fill the red field; corner quadrants mirror the medallion.
- **Form.** Woven on a silk warp with wool pile; approximately **340 knots per square inch** (standard carpets have 80–160) yielding roughly 25 million knots. Natural dyes: indigo, madder, pomegranate. Symmetrical, balanced, flat.

**Vocabulary:** knots · Safi al-Din · Safavid · inscription · mosque lamp
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 2.1, 2.3, 3.1, 3.2, 3.5
