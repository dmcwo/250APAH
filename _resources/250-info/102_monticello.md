## #102 — Monticello

**Thomas Jefferson (architect)** · 1768–1809 C.E. · Brick, glass, stone, and wood · Charlottesville, Virginia · 110 × 87′9″ × 44′7″

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Thomas_Jefferson%27s_Monticello_%28cropped%29.JPG/330px-Thomas_Jefferson%27s_Monticello_%28cropped%29.JPG" width="240" alt="Monticello">

### Summary

Jefferson's self-designed **Neoclassical** Virginia estate — his 40-year "essay in architecture."

- **Historical Context.** Built on a hilltop in Albemarle County, Virginia ("little mountain" in Italian). Jefferson inherited the land at 21 and began construction in 1768. Largely self-taught, he drew on extensive reading about **ancient Rome** and the **Italian Renaissance**. After his wife Martha's death, a trip to **France** exposed him to European architecture and inspired a near-total redesign; the second Monticello was roughly twice the size of the first and incorporated his inventions (revolving bookshelves, copying machines).
- **Function.** A **plantation house** for Jefferson and the enslaved people who labored there; he lived in the finished home only between 1808 and his death in 1826. Now restored by the Thomas Jefferson Foundation and operated as a museum.
- **Content.** 43 rooms total across the estate; 33 in the main house, with twelve cellars. Features a **dome** with an **oculus** skylight, 12 additional skylights, 8 fireplaces, north and south **pavilions**, and orchards, a vineyard, and an 18-acre ornamental grove.
- **Form.** **Neoclassical** design using Italian Renaissance principles; **Doric columns**, porticoes, pediment above the doorway, and a Palladian-inspired plan. Built from red bricks dug from the construction site itself.

**Vocabulary:** Neoclassical · columns · Enlightenment · dome · oculus · pavilion · porticoes · Palladian · pediment
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.1, 2.3, 3.2
