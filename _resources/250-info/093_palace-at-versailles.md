## #93 — The Palace at Versailles

**Louis Le Vau and Jules Hardouin-Mansart (architects)** · Begun 1669 C.E. · Masonry, stone, wood, iron, gold leaf; marble and bronze sculpture; gardens · Versailles, France

### Summary

**Louis XIV's absolutist stage** — the definitive French Baroque royal residence, and Europe's template for palace architecture for the next century.

- **Historical Context.** **Louis XIV** — the "Sun King" — transformed his father's modest hunting lodge into the seat of his absolute monarchy, relocating his court in 1682 to remove nobles from Parisian power and place them under his watchful eye. **Le Vau**, **Hardouin-Mansart**, painter **Charles Le Brun**, and landscape architect **André Le Nôtre** led the teams.
- **Function.** Royal residence, seat of government, courtly theater, and propaganda. A machine for the cult of the Sun King, with daily rituals (*lever*, *coucher*) playing out for rotating audiences of nobles.
- **Content.** The palace's **Hall of Mirrors** (Galerie des Glaces) — 17 mirror-clad arches facing 17 windows onto the gardens — narrates Louis's reign in Le Brun's ceiling paintings. **Le Nôtre's gardens** stretch the axis to the horizon with parterres, fountains, and allées identifying the king with Apollo.
- **Form.** Long classical façade punctuated with pilasters, balustrades, and sculpture; sprawling axial plan (axis mundi) radiating from the king's bedchamber. Interior mixes classical order (columns, arches) with flamboyant gilded ornament, allegorical painting, and crystal chandeliers.

**Vocabulary:** Baroque · absolutism · axial plan · parterre
