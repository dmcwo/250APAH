## #91 — Las Meninas

**Diego Velázquez** · c. 1656 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Museo del Prado, Madrid · 10′ 5″ × 9′ 1″

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg/400px-Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg" width="240" alt="Las Meninas">

### Summary

A **Spanish Baroque** group portrait that folds the painter, the royal family, and the viewer into a single enigmatic space.

- **Historical Context.** Painted by Velázquez at the court of **Philip IV** of Spain, where he served as chamberlain and court painter in the Alcázar Palace, Madrid. Velázquez's last great work; the red cross of **Santiago** on his chest was reportedly added after his knighthood was awarded (1659) — perhaps by the king himself.
- **Function.** Meant to hang in Philip IV's private office. Part group portrait, part self-portrait, part meditation on painting as a liberal art — Velázquez asserts the dignity of his profession at a time when painters were considered craftsmen.
- **Content.** **Infanta Margarita**, her *meninas* (ladies-in-waiting), a dwarf, a dog, and a chaperone occupy the foreground; Velázquez stands at a huge canvas to the left; king and queen appear reflected in a mirror on the back wall, suggesting they occupy the viewer's position.
- **Form.** Loose, atmospheric brushwork. Complex **spatial ambiguity**: we cannot tell whether we are the king's subjects, the painter's sitters, or both. Tenebrist side-lighting pulls figures from shadow; a doorway at the back opens into deeper space and frames a courtier silhouetted against light.

**Vocabulary:** fourth wall · chiaroscuro · self-portrait
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.2, 3.2, 3.5
