## #15 — Seated Scribe

**Egyptian, Old Kingdom, Fourth Dynasty** · c. 2620–2500 B.C.E. · Painted limestone (with rock crystal, magnesite, and copper inlaid eyes; wooden nipples) · Saqqara, Egypt

### Summary

A vivid painted limestone statuette of a scribe in cross-legged pose — one of the most lifelike surviving portraits of a non-royal individual from the Old Kingdom.

- **Historical Context.** From the necropolis at **Saqqara**, made during the **Fourth Dynasty** of the Old Kingdom. **Scribes** were revered in Egyptian society for their literacy; funerary statues of scribes commemorated that role in the afterlife.
- **Function.** A **tomb statue** honoring the deceased scribe; likely placed above a descriptive funerary text about him. It served as a permanent substitute for the scribe's **ka**.
- **Content.** A middle-aged man **seated cross-legged** holding a partially unrolled papyrus scroll; alert, slightly paunchy body. He is **not** the idealized youthful body of royal statuary.
- **Form.** **Painted limestone** with striking **inlaid eyes** (rock crystal, magnesite, copper) that give the gaze extraordinary presence. The seated pose is a departure from the rigid standing poses of royal Egyptian sculpture.
