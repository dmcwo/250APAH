## #126 — Les Demoiselles d'Avignon

**Pablo Picasso** · 1907 C.E. · Oil on canvas · Museum of Modern Art, New York · 8′ × 7′8″

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4c/Les_Demoiselles_d%27Avignon.jpg/330px-Les_Demoiselles_d%27Avignon.jpg" width="240" alt="Les Demoiselles d'Avignon">

### Summary

Widely cited as the **starting point of Cubism**.

- **Historical Context.** Picasso (b. Málaga, 1881; trained in Barcelona) moved to Paris, where **Gertrude Stein** patronized him and **Cézanne**'s flat, anti-illusionistic painting reshaped his approach. A recent exhibit of **African and Iberian masks** directly inspired the right-hand faces. The figures are prostitutes from a brothel on **Carrer d'Avinyó** (Avignon Street) in Barcelona.
- **Function.** Meant to **upstage Matisse's *Bonheur de Vivre*** (1906) — deliberately provocative, radical, and anti-decorative.
- **Content.** Five nude prostitutes in confrontational poses; fruit on a table at bottom-center; three stare directly at the viewer. (An earlier version included a male client on the left, later painted out.)
- **Form.** Limited red/blue/pink/warm-grey palette; no real background, just sharp planes. Left three figures are abstracted but human; right two wear **mask-like**, geometric faces. Multiple vantage points, no linear perspective, no depth.

**Vocabulary:** Cubism · avant-garde · primitivism · salon
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 2.3, 3.2, 3.4, 3.5

### Compare-and-contrast exercise (LO 3.5)

#### Madame Cézanne in a Red Dress (Paul Cézanne, 1888–1890) — *not in image set*

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/07/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne%2C_1888-90%2C_Madame_C%C3%A9zanne_%28Hortense_Fiquet%2C_1850%E2%80%931922%29_in_a_Red_Dress%2C_oil_on_canvas%2C_116.5_x_89.5_cm%2C_The_Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art%2C_New_York.jpg" width="180" alt="Madame Cézanne in a Red Dress">

**Prompt:** Using specific formal and contextual evidence, compare the two works and analyze the possible influences, if any, that one of the works might have had on the other.

> *Madame Cézanne in a Red Dress* was created decades before *Les Demoiselles d'Avignon*, and Cézanne was known to be one of Picasso's early influences after his arrival in Paris in the early 20th century. While it is not solely this piece, the work on the left was consequential for the work on the right, through their formal similarities.
>
> *Madame Cézanne in a Red Dress* is painted with a more representational style, with actual attention given to details in the human figure, like hands, or eyes. On the other hand, the five figures are almost completely devoid of detail, left as only angular planes of flat colors (shades of reds and pinks). However, both works depict females, and the artists' ideal or interpretation of beauty. Cézanne painted his wife, who is most likely a subject of affection for him, and the five prostitutes were painted in way to push the boundaries of what could be seen as a female figure; even simple shapes could convey the female body. Additionally, there is a lack of realistic detail in both works' backgrounds; there is just enough line to convey the shadow of the creases in fabric and drapery, but the pattern is muddled in *Madame Cézanne*, and the drapery takes on a geometric form in *Les Demoiselles d'Avignon*. Furthermore, *Madame Cézanne* is sitting stiffly in a chair with a stoic expression and gaze towards the viewer, as are three of the five figures in the other piece. Both works do not convey any real depth, with subjects placed in a claustrophobic position that eliminates the illusion of three dimensions on the two-dimensional works. For example, *Madame Cézanne* is placed seemingly right against the wall, with other objects, like the painting, on the wall as well. Picasso achieves the same flatness by reducing the objects in the painting to abstractions, like the table on which the fruit is placed, as well as using several different vantage points.
