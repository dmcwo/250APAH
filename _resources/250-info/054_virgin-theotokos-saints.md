## #54 — Virgin (Theotokos) and Child between Saints Theodore and George

**Early Byzantine Europe** · Sixth or early seventh century C.E. · Encaustic on wood · Monastery of Saint Catherine, Mount Sinai, Egypt · 69 × 48 cm

### Summary

One of the **oldest surviving Christian icons**, preserved at St. Catherine's Monastery on **Mount Sinai** — the Virgin flanked by warrior saints under the descending hand of God.

- **Historical Context.** Made in the early Byzantine Empire, whose official religion was **Eastern Orthodox Christianity**. Survived the **Iconoclasm** controversy (c. 726–843) because Sinai lay outside Byzantine political control. The image helped fuel the debate about whether sacred images were legitimate intermediaries or forbidden idols.
- **Function.** An **icon**, hung on the **iconostasis** or handled during ritual. Icons were repainted from frequent touching; they were not worshipped but venerated as conduits for prayer to the holy figures they depicted.
- **Content.** **Theotokos** ("God-bearer," the Virgin Mary) enthroned, holding the infant **Christ**; flanked by military saints **Theodore** (left) and **George** (right); behind, two **angels** look up toward heaven as the **hand of God** descends between them.
- **Form.** **Encaustic** (pigments suspended in hot wax) on wood. Large **gold halos** flatten and sanctify the figures; Mary's blue drapery models her volume while the saints stare frontally at the viewer. Classical Hellenistic shading survives alongside the flatter, more frontal conventions that would become Byzantine orthodoxy.

**Vocabulary:** encaustic · icons / iconoclasm · iconostasis
**Learning Objectives:** LO 1.3, 1.4, 2.1, 2.2, 3.5
