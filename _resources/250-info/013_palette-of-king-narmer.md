## #13 — Palette of King Narmer

**Predynastic Egypt** · c. 3000–2920 B.C.E. · Greywacke / siltstone · from the temple of Horus, Hierakonpolis · 25 in tall

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Narmer_Palette.jpg/330px-Narmer_Palette.jpg" width="240" alt="Palette of King Narmer">

### Summary

A ceremonial **cosmetic palette** commemorating (or ceremonially enacting) the **unification of Upper and Lower Egypt** under **King Narmer** — foundational to Egyptian pictorial conventions.

- **Historical Context.** Made in Predynastic Egypt and deposited as a **votive object** in the temple of Horus at **Hierakonpolis**. Narmer is traditionally credited as the unifier of Upper and Lower Egypt; the palette is among the earliest works to fuse **Egyptian pictorial conventions** that would persist for three millennia.
- **Function.** Nominally a **palette for grinding eye makeup** (used to decorate cult images of gods), but monumental in scale and far too elaborate for daily use — it is a **ceremonial / votive** object buried beneath the temple floor.
- **Content.** Narmer appears twice, once in the **White Crown** of Upper Egypt smiting an enemy, once in the **Red Crown** of Lower Egypt inspecting beheaded foes. Hathor heads flank the top; two long-necked **serpopards** frame the central grinding circle; a falcon-Horus perches above papyrus (Lower Egypt).
- **Form.** Low-relief carving in **greywacke** organized into horizontal **registers**. **Hierarchical scale** (Narmer is the largest figure); **composite view** (head and legs in profile, torso frontal); a flat, two-dimensional idealization that sets the template for later Egyptian art.

**Vocabulary:** votive object · hierarchical scale · register · composite view
**Learning Objectives:** LO 2.1, 2.2, 3.1
